**Modern architecture**, also called **modernist architecture**, or **the modern movement**, is an [architectural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architectural "Architectural") [movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architectural_movement "Architectural movement") and [style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architectural_style "Architectural style") that was prominent in the 20th century, between the earlier [Art Deco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_Deco_Architecture "Art Deco Architecture") and later [postmodern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodern_architecture "Postmodern architecture") movements. Modern architecture was based upon new and innovative technologies of construction (particularly the use of [glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass "Glass"), [steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steel "Steel"), and [concrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concrete "Concrete")); the principle of [functionalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functionalism_\(architecture\) "Functionalism (architecture)") (i.e. that [form should follow function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Form_follows_function "Form follows function")); an embrace of [minimalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimalism "Minimalism"); and a rejection of [ornament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ornament_\(art\) "Ornament (art)").[^1]

According to [Le Corbusier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Corbusier "Le Corbusier"), the roots of the movement were to be found in the works of [Eugène Viollet-le-Duc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Viollet-le-Duc "Eugène Viollet-le-Duc"),[^2] while [Mies van der Rohe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mies_van_der_Rohe "Mies van der Rohe") was heavily inspired by [Karl Friedrich Schinkel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Friedrich_Schinkel "Karl Friedrich Schinkel").[^3] [^4] The movement emerged in the first half of the 20th century and became dominant after [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II") until the 1980s, when it was gradually replaced as the principal style for institutional and [corporate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corporation "Corporation") buildings by [postmodern architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodern_architecture "Postmodern architecture").[^5]

## Origins

Modern architecture emerged at the end of the 19th century from revolutions in technology, engineering, and building materials, and from a desire to break away from historical architectural styles and invent something that was purely functional and new.

The revolution in materials came first, with the use of [cast iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cast_iron "Cast iron"), [drywall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drywall "Drywall"), [plate glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plate_glass "Plate glass"), and reinforced concrete, to build structures that were stronger, lighter, and taller. The [cast plate glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cast_plate_glass "Cast plate glass") process was invented in 1848, allowing the manufacture of very large windows. [The Crystal Palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Crystal_Palace "The Crystal Palace") by [Joseph Paxton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Paxton "Joseph Paxton") at the [Great Exhibition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Exhibition "Great Exhibition") of 1851 was an early example of iron and plate glass construction, followed in 1864 by the first glass and metal [curtain wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curtain_wall_\(architecture\) "Curtain wall (architecture)"). These developments together led to the first steel-framed skyscraper, the ten-story [Home Insurance Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home_Insurance_Building "Home Insurance Building") in Chicago, built in 1884 by [William Le Baron Jenney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Le_Baron_Jenney "William Le Baron Jenney") [^6] and based on the works of Viollet le Duc.

French industrialist François Coignet was the first to use iron reinforced concrete, that is, concrete strengthened with iron bars, as a technique for constructing buildings.[^7] In 1853 Coignet built the first iron reinforced concrete structure, a four-storey house in the suburbs of Paris.[^7] A further important step forward was the invention of the safety [elevator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elevator "Elevator") by [Elisha Otis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elisha_Otis "Elisha Otis"), first demonstrated at the [New York Crystal Palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_Crystal_Palace "New York Crystal Palace") exposition in 1854, which made tall office and apartment buildings practical.[^8] Another important technology for the new architecture was electric light, which greatly reduced the inherent danger of fires caused by gas in the 19th century.[^9]

The debut of new materials and techniques inspired architects to break away from the neoclassical and eclectic models that dominated European and American architecture in the late 19th century, most notably [eclecticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eclecticism_in_art "Eclecticism in art"), [Victorian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victorian_architecture "Victorian architecture") and [Edwardian architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwardian_architecture "Edwardian architecture"), and the [Beaux-Arts architectural style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beaux-Arts_architecture "Beaux-Arts architecture").[^10] This break with the past was particularly urged by the architectural theorist and historian [Eugène Viollet-le-Duc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Viollet-le-Duc "Eugène Viollet-le-Duc"). In his 1872 book *Entretiens sur l'architecture*, he urged: "use the means and knowledge given to us by our times, without the intervening traditions which are no longer viable today, and in that way we can inaugurate a new architecture. For each function its material; for each material its form and its ornament." [^11] This book influenced a generation of architects, including [Louis Sullivan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Sullivan "Louis Sullivan"), [Victor Horta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victor_Horta "Victor Horta"), [Hector Guimard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hector_Guimard "Hector Guimard"), and [Antoni Gaudí](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoni_Gaud%C3%AD "Antoni Gaudí").[^12]

At the end of the 19th century, a few architects began to challenge the traditional [Beaux Arts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beaux-Arts_architecture "Beaux-Arts architecture") and [Neoclassical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture "Neoclassical architecture") styles that dominated architecture in Europe and the United States. The [Glasgow School of Art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glasgow_School_of_Art "Glasgow School of Art") (1896–99) designed by [Charles Rennie Mackintosh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Rennie_Mackintosh "Charles Rennie Mackintosh"), had a façade dominated by large vertical bays of windows.[^13] The [Art Nouveau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_Nouveau "Art Nouveau") style was launched in the 1890s by [Victor Horta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victor_Horta "Victor Horta") in Belgium and [Hector Guimard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hector_Guimard "Hector Guimard") in France; it introduced new styles of decoration, based on vegetal and floral forms. In Barcelona, [Antoni Gaudi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoni_Gaudi "Antoni Gaudi") conceived architecture as a form of sculpture; the façade of the [Casa Batlló](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casa_Batll%C3%B3 "Casa Batlló") in Barcelona (1904–1907) had no straight lines; it was encrusted with colorful mosaics of stone and ceramic tiles.[^14]

Architects also began to experiment with new materials and techniques, which gave them greater freedom to create new forms. In 1903–1904 in Paris [Auguste Perret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auguste_Perret "Auguste Perret") and [Henri Sauvage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Sauvage "Henri Sauvage") began to use [reinforced concrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reinforced_concrete "Reinforced concrete"), previously only used for industrial structures, to build apartment buildings.[^15] Reinforced concrete, which could be molded into any shape, and which could create enormous spaces without the need of supporting pillars, replaced stone and brick as the primary material for modernist architects. The first concrete apartment buildings by Perret and Sauvage were covered with ceramic tiles, but in 1905 Perret built the first concrete parking garage on 51 rue de Ponthieu in Paris; here the concrete was left bare, and the space between the concrete was filled with glass windows. [Henri Sauvage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Sauvage "Henri Sauvage") added another construction innovation in an apartment building on Rue Vavin in Paris (1912–1914); the reinforced concrete building was in steps, with each floor set back from the floor below, creating a series of terraces. Between 1910 and 1913, Auguste Perret built the [Théâtre des Champs-Élysées](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Th%C3%A9%C3%A2tre_des_Champs-%C3%89lys%C3%A9es "Théâtre des Champs-Élysées"), a masterpiece of reinforced concrete construction, with Art Deco sculptural bas-reliefs on the façade by [Antoine Bourdelle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine_Bourdelle "Antoine Bourdelle"). Because of the concrete construction, no columns blocked the spectator's view of the stage.[^16]

[Otto Wagner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Wagner "Otto Wagner"), in Vienna, was another pioneer of the new style. In his book *Moderne Architektur* (1895) he had called for a more rationalist style of architecture, based on "modern life".[^17] He designed a stylized ornamental metro station at [Karlsplatz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karlsplatz "Karlsplatz") in Vienna (1888–89), then an ornamental [Art Nouveau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_Nouveau "Art Nouveau") residence, Majolika House (1898), before moving to a much more geometric and simplified style, without ornament, in the [Austrian Postal Savings Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austrian_Postal_Savings_Bank "Austrian Postal Savings Bank") (1904–1906). Wagner declared his intention to express the function of the building in its exterior. The reinforced concrete exterior was covered with plaques of marble attached with bolts of polished aluminum. The interior was purely functional and spare, a large open space of steel, glass, and concrete where the only decoration was the structure itself.[^18]

The Viennese architect [Adolf Loos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Loos "Adolf Loos") also began to refrain from using ornament in his buildings. His [Steiner House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steiner_House "Steiner House"), in Vienna (1910), was an example of what he called [rationalist architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationalist_architecture "Rationalist architecture"); it had a simple [stucco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stucco "Stucco") rectangular façade with square windows and no ornament. The fame of the new movement, which became known as the [Vienna Secession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vienna_Secession "Vienna Secession") spread beyond Austria. [Josef Hoffmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josef_Hoffmann "Josef Hoffmann"), a student of Wagner, constructed a landmark of early modernist architecture, the [Stoclet Palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoclet_Palace "Stoclet Palace"), in Brussels, in 1906–1911. This residence, built of brick covered with Norwegian marble, was composed of geometric blocks, wings, and a tower. A large pool in front of the house reflected its cubic forms. The interior was decorated with paintings by [Gustav Klimt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Klimt "Gustav Klimt") and other artists, and the architect even designed clothing for the family to match the architecture.[^19]

In Germany, a modernist industrial movement, *[Deutscher Werkbund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deutscher_Werkbund "Deutscher Werkbund")* (German Work Federation) had been created in Munich in 1907 by [Hermann Muthesius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Muthesius "Hermann Muthesius"), a prominent architectural commentator. Its goal was to bring together designers and industrialists, to turn out well-designed, high-quality products, and in the process to invent a new type of architecture.[^20] The organization originally included twelve architects and twelve business firms, but quickly expanded. The architects include [Peter Behrens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Behrens "Peter Behrens"), [Theodor Fischer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_Fischer "Theodor Fischer") (who served as its first president), [Josef Hoffmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josef_Hoffmann "Josef Hoffmann") and [Richard Riemerschmid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Riemerschmid "Richard Riemerschmid").[^21] In 1909 Behrens designed one of the earliest and most influential industrial buildings in the modernist style, the [AEG turbine factory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AEG_turbine_factory "AEG turbine factory"), a functional monument of steel and concrete. In 1911–1913, [Adolf Meyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Meyer_\(architect\) "Adolf Meyer (architect)") and [Walter Gropius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Gropius "Walter Gropius"), who had both worked for Behrens, built another revolutionary industrial plant, the Fagus Factory in Alfeld an der Laine, a building without ornament where every construction element was on display. The *Werkbund* organized a major exposition of modernist design in Cologne just a few weeks before the outbreak of the First World War in August 1914. For the 1914 Cologne exhibition, [Bruno Taut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bruno_Taut "Bruno Taut") built a revolutionary glass pavilion.[^22]

[Frank Lloyd Wright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright "Frank Lloyd Wright") was a highly original and independent American architect who refused to be categorized in any one architectural movement. Like [Le Corbusier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Corbusier "Le Corbusier") and [Ludwig Mies van der Rohe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Mies_van_der_Rohe "Ludwig Mies van der Rohe"), he had no formal architectural training. From 1887 to 1893 he worked in the Chicago office of [Louis Sullivan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Sullivan "Louis Sullivan"), who pioneered the first tall steel-frame office buildings in Chicago, and who famously stated " [form follows function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Form_follows_function "Form follows function") ".[^23] Wright set out to break all the traditional rules. He was particularly famous for his [Prairie Houses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prairie_Houses "Prairie Houses"), including the [Winslow House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winslow_House_\(River_Forest,_Illinois\) "Winslow House (River Forest, Illinois)") in [River Forest, Illinois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/River_Forest,_Illinois "River Forest, Illinois") (1893–94); [Arthur Heurtley House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Heurtley_House "Arthur Heurtley House") (1902) and [Robie House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robie_House "Robie House") (1909); sprawling, geometric residences without decoration, with strong horizontal lines which seemed to grow out of the earth, and which echoed the wide flat spaces of the American prairie. His [Larkin Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larkin_Administration_Building "Larkin Administration Building") (1904–1906) in [Buffalo, New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York "Buffalo, New York"), [Unity Temple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unity_Temple "Unity Temple") (1905) in [Oak Park, Illinois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oak_Park,_Illinois "Oak Park, Illinois") and Unity Temple had highly original forms and no connection with historical precedents.[^24]

At the end of the 19th century, the first [skyscrapers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skyscrapers "Skyscrapers") began to appear in the United States. They were a response to the shortage of land and high cost of real estate in the center of the fast-growing American cities, and the availability of new technologies, including fireproof steel frames and improvements in the safety [elevator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elevator "Elevator") invented by [Elisha Otis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elisha_Otis "Elisha Otis") in 1852. The first steel-framed "skyscraper", The [Home Insurance Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home_Insurance_Building "Home Insurance Building") in Chicago, was ten stories high. It was designed by [William Le Baron Jenney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Le_Baron_Jenney "William Le Baron Jenney") in 1883, and was briefly the tallest building in the world. [Louis Sullivan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Sullivan "Louis Sullivan") built another monumental new structure, the [Carson, Pirie, Scott and Company Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carson,_Pirie,_Scott_and_Company_Building "Carson, Pirie, Scott and Company Building"), in the heart of Chicago in 1904–1906. While these buildings were revolutionary in their steel frames and height, their decoration was borrowed from [Neo-Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance_Revival_architecture "Renaissance Revival architecture"), [Neo-Gothic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-Gothic "Neo-Gothic") and [Beaux-Arts architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beaux-Arts_architecture "Beaux-Arts architecture"). The [Woolworth Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woolworth_Building "Woolworth Building"), designed by [Cass Gilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cass_Gilbert "Cass Gilbert"), was completed in 1912, and was the tallest building in the world until the completion of the [Chrysler Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chrysler_Building "Chrysler Building") in 1929. The structure was purely modern, but its exterior was decorated with Neo-Gothic ornament, complete with decorative buttresses, arches and spires, which caused it to be nicknamed the "Cathedral of Commerce".[^25]

## Rise of modernism in Europe (1918–1931)

After the first World War, a prolonged struggle began between architects who favored the more traditional styles of [neo-classicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-classical_architecture "Neo-classical architecture") and the [Beaux-Arts architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beaux-Arts_architecture "Beaux-Arts architecture") style, and the modernists, led by [Le Corbusier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Corbusier "Le Corbusier") and [Robert Mallet-Stevens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Mallet-Stevens "Robert Mallet-Stevens") in France, [Walter Gropius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Gropius "Walter Gropius") and [Ludwig Mies van der Rohe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Mies_van_der_Rohe "Ludwig Mies van der Rohe") in Germany, and [Konstantin Melnikov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konstantin_Melnikov "Konstantin Melnikov") in the new [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"), who wanted only pure forms and the elimination of any decoration. [Louis Sullivan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Sullivan "Louis Sullivan") popularized the axiom *[Form follows function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Form_follows_function "Form follows function")* to emphasize the importance of utilitarian simplicity in modern architecture. [Art Deco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_Deco "Art Deco") architects such as [Auguste Perret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auguste_Perret "Auguste Perret") and [Henri Sauvage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Sauvage "Henri Sauvage") often made a compromise between the two, combining modernist forms and stylized decoration.

A fine example of extensive European modernist architecture style is the [Modernist architecture of Kaunas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modernist_architecture_of_Kaunas "Modernist architecture of Kaunas").[^26] [Kaunas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaunas "Kaunas") in the interwar period served as the [temporary capital of Lithuania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temporary_capital_of_Lithuania "Temporary capital of Lithuania") and required extensive expansion, modernization because initially it was a quite small city.[^26] In 2023, the Modernist architecture of Kaunas was recognized as the [UNESCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO "UNESCO") [World Heritage Site](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Heritage_Site "World Heritage Site").[^26]

The dominant figure in the rise of modernism in France was Charles-Édouard Jeanerette, a Swiss-French architect who in 1920 took the name [Le Corbusier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Corbusier "Le Corbusier"). In 1920 he co-founded a journal called *'L'Espirit Nouveau* and energetically promoted architecture that was functional, pure, and free of any decoration or historical associations. He was also a passionate advocate of a new urbanism, based on planned cities. In 1922 he presented a design of a city for three million people, whose inhabitants lived in identical sixty-story tall skyscrapers surrounded by open parkland. He designed modular houses, which would be mass-produced on the same plan and assembled into apartment blocks, neighborhoods, and cities. In 1923 he published "Toward an Architecture", with his famous slogan, "a house is a machine for living in." [^27] He tirelessly promoted his ideas through slogans, articles, books, conferences, and participation in Expositions.

To illustrate his ideas, in the 1920s he built a series of houses and villas in and around Paris. They were all built according to a common system, based upon the use of reinforced concrete, and of reinforced concrete pylons in the interior which supported the structure, allowing glass curtain walls on the façade and open floor plans, independent of the structure. They were always white, and had no ornament or decoration on the outside or inside. The best-known of these houses was the [Villa Savoye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Villa_Savoye "Villa Savoye"), built in 1928–1931 in the Paris suburb of [Poissy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poissy "Poissy"). An elegant white box wrapped with a ribbon of glass windows around on the façade, with living space that opened upon an interior garden and countryside around, raised up by a row of white pylons in the center of a large lawn, it became an icon of modernist architecture.[^28]

In Germany, two important modernist movements appeared after the first World War, The [Bauhaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bauhaus "Bauhaus") was a school founded in [Weimar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weimar "Weimar") in 1919 under the direction of [Walter Gropius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Gropius "Walter Gropius"). Gropius was the son of the official state architect of Berlin, who studied before the war with [Peter Behrens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Behrens "Peter Behrens"), and designed the modernist Fagus turbine factory. The Bauhaus was a fusion of the prewar Academy of Arts and the school of technology. In 1926 it was transferred from Weimar to Dessau; Gropius designed the new school and student dormitories in the new, purely functional modernist style he was encouraging. The school brought together modernists in all fields; the faculty included the modernist painters [Vasily Kandinsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasily_Kandinsky "Vasily Kandinsky"), [Joseph Albers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josef_Albers "Josef Albers") and [Paul Klee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Klee "Paul Klee"), and the designer [Marcel Breuer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcel_Breuer "Marcel Breuer").

Gropius became an important theorist of modernism, writing *The Idea and Construction* in 1923. He was an advocate of standardization in architecture, and the mass construction of rationally designed apartment blocks for factory workers. In 1928 he was commissioned by the [Siemens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siemens "Siemens") company to build apartment for workers in the suburbs of Berlin, and in 1929 he proposed the construction of clusters of slender eight- to ten-story high-rise apartment towers for workers.

While Gropius was active at the Bauhaus, [Ludwig Mies van der Rohe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Mies_van_der_Rohe "Ludwig Mies van der Rohe") led the modernist architectural movement in Berlin. Inspired by the [De Stijl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Stijl "De Stijl") movement in the Netherlands, he built clusters of concrete summer houses and proposed a project for a glass office tower. He became the vice president of the German Werkbund, and became the head of the Bauhaus from 1930 to 1933. proposing a wide variety of modernist plans for urban reconstruction. His most famous modernist work was the German pavilion for the 1929 international exposition in Barcelona. It was a work of pure modernism, with glass and concrete walls and clean, horizontal lines. Though it was only a temporary structure, and was torn down in 1930, it became, along with Le Corbusier's [Villa Savoye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Villa_Savoye "Villa Savoye"), one of the best-known landmarks of modernist architecture. A reconstructed version now stands on the original site in Barcelona.[^29]

When the Nazis came to power in Germany, they viewed the Bauhaus as a training ground for communists, and closed the school in 1933. Gropius left Germany and went to England, then to the United States, where he and [Marcel Breuer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcel_Breuer "Marcel Breuer") both joined the faculty of the [Harvard Graduate School of Design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_Graduate_School_of_Design "Harvard Graduate School of Design"), and became the teachers of a generation of American postwar architects. In 1937 Mies van der Rohe also moved to the United States; he became one of the most famous designers of postwar American skyscrapers.[^29]

[Expressionism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expressionism "Expressionism"), which appeared in Germany between 1910 and 1925, was a counter-movement against the strictly functional architecture of the Bauhaus and Werkbund. Its advocates, including [Bruno Taut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bruno_Taut "Bruno Taut"), [Hans Poelzig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Poelzig "Hans Poelzig"), [Fritz Höger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_H%C3%B6ger "Fritz Höger") and [Erich Mendelsohn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_Mendelsohn "Erich Mendelsohn"), wanted to create architecture that was poetic, expressive, and optimistic. Many expressionist architects had fought in World War I and their experiences, combined with the political turmoil and social upheaval that followed the [German Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Revolution "German Revolution") of 1919, resulted in a utopian outlook and a romantic socialist agenda.[^30] Economic conditions severely limited the number of built commissions between 1914 and the mid-1920s,[^31] As result, many of the most innovative expressionist projects, including [Bruno Taut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bruno_Taut "Bruno Taut") 's *Alpine Architecture* and [Hermann Finsterlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Finsterlin "Hermann Finsterlin") 's *Formspiels*, remained on paper. [Scenography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scenography "Scenography") for theatre and films provided another outlet for the expressionist imagination,[^32] and provided supplemental incomes for designers attempting to challenge conventions in a harsh economic climate. A particular type, using bricks to create its forms (rather than concrete) is known as [Brick Expressionism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brick_Expressionism "Brick Expressionism").

[Erich Mendelsohn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_Mendelsohn "Erich Mendelsohn"), (who disliked the term Expressionism for his work) began his career designing churches, silos, and factories which were highly imaginative, but, for lack of resources, were never built. In 1920, he finally was able to construct one of his works in the city of Potsdam; an observatory and research center called the [Einsteinium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einsteinium "Einsteinium"), named in tribute to [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein"). It was supposed to be built of reinforced concrete, but because of technical problems it was finally built of traditional materials covered with plaster. His sculptural form, very different from the austere rectangular forms of the Bauhaus, first won him commissions to build movie theaters and retail stores in Stuttgart, Nuremberg, and Berlin. His [Mossehaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mossehaus "Mossehaus") in Berlin was an early model for the [streamline moderne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Streamline_moderne "Streamline moderne") style. His [Columbushaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbushaus "Columbushaus") on Potsdamer Platz in Berlin (1931) was a prototype for the modernist office buildings that followed. (It was torn down in 1957, because it stood in the zone between East and West Berlin, where the [Berlin Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Wall "Berlin Wall") was constructed.) Following the rise of the Nazis to power, he moved to England (1933), then to the United States (1941).[^33]

[Fritz Höger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_H%C3%B6ger "Fritz Höger") was another notable Expressionist architect of the period. His [Chilehaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilehaus "Chilehaus") was built as the headquarters of a shipping company, and was modeled after a giant steamship, a triangular building with a sharply pointed bow. It was constructed of dark brick, and used external piers to express its vertical structure. Its external decoration borrowed from Gothic cathedrals, as did its internal arcades. [Hans Poelzig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Poelzig "Hans Poelzig") was another notable expressionist architect. In 1919 he built the [Großes Schauspielhaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gro%C3%9Fes_Schauspielhaus "Großes Schauspielhaus"), an immense theater in Berlin, seating five thousand spectators for theater impresario [Max Reinhardt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Reinhardt "Max Reinhardt"). It featured elongated shapes like stalagmites hanging down from its gigantic dome, and lights on massive columns in its foyer. He also constructed the [IG Farben building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IG_Farben_building "IG Farben building"), a massive corporate headquarters, now the main building of [Goethe University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goethe_University "Goethe University") in Frankfurt. [Bruno Taut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bruno_Taut "Bruno Taut") specialized in building large-scale apartment complexes for working-class Berliners. He built twelve thousand individual units, sometimes in buildings with unusual shapes, such as a giant horseshoe. Unlike most other modernists, he used bright exterior colors to give his buildings more life The use of dark brick in the German projects gave that particular style a name, [Brick Expressionism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brick_Expressionism "Brick Expressionism").[^34]

The Austrian philosopher, architect, and social critic [Rudolf Steiner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Steiner "Rudolf Steiner") also departed as far as possible from traditional architectural forms. His [Second Goetheanum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goetheanum "Goetheanum"), built from 1926 near [Basel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basel "Basel"), [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland") and [Mendelsohn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_Mendelsohn "Erich Mendelsohn") 's [Einsteinturm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einsteinturm "Einsteinturm") in Potsdam, Germany, were based on no traditional models and had entirely original shapes.

Proto-Constructvism began developing in the 1910s in the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire"), advocated by local architects such as [Oleksandr Ginzburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Oleksandr_Ginzburg&action=edit&redlink=1 "Oleksandr Ginzburg (page does not exist)").[^35] [^36] After the [Russian Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Revolution "Russian Revolution") of 1917, Russian avant-garde artists and architects began searching for a new Soviet style which could replace traditional neoclassicism. The new architectural movements were closely tied with the literary and artistic movements of the period, the [futurism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Futurism "Futurism") of poet [Vladimir Mayakovskiy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Mayakovskiy "Vladimir Mayakovskiy"), the [Suprematism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suprematism "Suprematism") of painter [Kasimir Malevich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kasimir_Malevich "Kasimir Malevich"), and the colorful [Rayonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rayonism "Rayonism") of painter [Mikhail Larionov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Larionov "Mikhail Larionov"). The most startling design that emerged was the tower proposed by painter and sculptor [Vladimir Tatlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Tatlin "Vladimir Tatlin") for the Moscow meeting of the Third [Communist International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_International "Communist International") in 1920: he proposed two interlaced towers of metal four hundred meters high, with four geometric volumes suspended from cables. The movement of Russian [Constructivist architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constructivist_architecture "Constructivist architecture") was launched in 1921 by a group of artists led by [Aleksandr Rodchenko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleksandr_Rodchenko "Aleksandr Rodchenko"). Their manifesto proclaimed that their goal was to find the "communist expression of material structures". Soviet architects began to construct workers' clubs, communal apartment houses, and communal kitchens for feeding whole neighborhoods.[^37]

One of the first prominent constructivist architects to emerge in Moscow was [Konstantin Melnikov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konstantin_Melnikov "Konstantin Melnikov"), the number of working clubs – including [Rusakov Workers' Club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rusakov_Workers%27_Club "Rusakov Workers' Club") (1928) – and his own living house, [Melnikov House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Melnikov_House "The Melnikov House") (1929) near [Arbat Street](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arbat_Street "Arbat Street") in Moscow. Melnikov traveled to Paris in 1925 where he built the Soviet Pavilion for the [International Exhibition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Exhibition_of_Modern_Decorative_and_Industrial_Arts "International Exhibition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts") in Paris in 1925; it was a highly geometric vertical construction of glass and steel crossed by a diagonal stairway, and crowned with a hammer and sickle. The leading group of constructivist architects, led by [Vesnin brothers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vesnin_brothers "Vesnin brothers") and [Moisei Ginzburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moisei_Ginzburg "Moisei Ginzburg"), was publishing the 'Contemporary Architecture' journal. This group created several major constructivist projects in the wake of the First Five Year Plan – including colossal [Dnieper Hydroelectric Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dnieper_Hydroelectric_Station "Dnieper Hydroelectric Station") (1932) – and made an attempt to start the standardization of living blocks with Ginzburg's [Narkomfin building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narkomfin_building "Narkomfin building"). A number of architects from the pre-Soviet period also took up the constructivist style. The most famous example was [Lenin's Mausoleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lenin%27s_Mausoleum "Lenin's Mausoleum") in Moscow (1924), by [Alexey Shchusev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexey_Shchusev "Alexey Shchusev") (1924) [^38]

The main centers of constructivist architecture were Moscow and Leningrad; however, during the industrialization many constructivist buildings were erected in provincial cities. The regional industrial centers, including [Ekaterinburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yekaterinburg "Yekaterinburg"), [Kharkiv](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kharkiv "Kharkiv") or [Ivanovo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ivanovo "Ivanovo"), were rebuilt in the constructivist manner; some cities, like [Magnitogorsk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitogorsk "Magnitogorsk") or [Zaporizhzhia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sotsmisto,_Zaporizhzhia "Sotsmisto, Zaporizhzhia"), were constructed anew (the so-called *sotsgorod*, or 'socialist city').

The style fell markedly out of favor in the 1930s, replaced by the more grandiose nationalist styles that Stalin favored. Constructivist architects and even [Le Corbusier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Corbusier "Le Corbusier") projects for the new [Palace of the Soviets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palace_of_the_Soviets "Palace of the Soviets") from 1931 to 1933, but the winner was an early Stalinist building in the style termed [Postconstructivism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postconstructivism "Postconstructivism"). The last major Russian constructivist building, by [Boris Iofan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boris_Iofan "Boris Iofan"), was built for the [Paris World Exhibition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exposition_Internationale_des_Arts_et_Techniques_dans_la_Vie_Moderne "Exposition Internationale des Arts et Techniques dans la Vie Moderne") (1937), where it faced the pavilion of Nazi Germany by Hitler's architect [Albert Speer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Speer "Albert Speer").[^39]

The New Objectivity (in German Neue Sachlichkeit, sometimes also translated as New Sobriety) is a name often given to the Modern architecture that emerged in Europe, primarily German-speaking Europe, in the 1920s and 30s. It is also frequently called Neues Bauen (New Building). The New Objectivity took place in many German cities in that period, for example in Frankfurt with its [Neues Frankfurt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neues_Frankfurt "Neues Frankfurt") project.

### Modernism becomes a movement: CIAM (1928)

By the late 1920s, modernism had become an important movement in Europe. Architecture, which previously had been predominantly national, began to become international. The architects traveled, met each other, and shared ideas. Several modernists, including [Le Corbusier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Corbusier "Le Corbusier"), had participated in the competition for the headquarters of the [League of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/League_of_Nations "League of Nations") in 1927. In the same year, the German Werkbund organized an architectural exposition at the [Weissenhof Estate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weissenhof_Estate "Weissenhof Estate") [Stuttgart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stuttgart "Stuttgart"). Seventeen leading modernist architects in Europe were invited to design twenty-one houses; Le Corbusier, and [Ludwig Mies van der Rohe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Mies_van_der_Rohe "Ludwig Mies van der Rohe") played a major part. In 1927 Le Corbusier, Pierre Chareau, and others proposed the foundation of an international conference to establish the basis for a common style. The first meeting of the *[Congrès Internationaux d'Architecture Moderne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congr%C3%A8s_International_d%27Architecture_Moderne "Congrès International d'Architecture Moderne")* or International Congresses of Modern Architects (CIAM), was held in a chateau on [Lake Leman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Leman "Lake Leman") in Switzerland 26–28 June 1928. Those attending included Le Corbusier, [Robert Mallet-Stevens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Mallet-Stevens "Robert Mallet-Stevens"), [Auguste Perret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auguste_Perret "Auguste Perret"), [Pierre Chareau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Chareau "Pierre Chareau") and [Tony Garnier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tony_Garnier_\(architect\) "Tony Garnier (architect)") from France; [Victor Bourgeois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victor_Bourgeois "Victor Bourgeois") from Belgium; [Walter Gropius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Gropius "Walter Gropius"), [Erich Mendelsohn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_Mendelsohn "Erich Mendelsohn"), [Ernst May](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_May "Ernst May") and Ludwig Mies van der Rohe from Germany; [Josef Frank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josef_Frank_\(architect\) "Josef Frank (architect)") from Austria; [Mart Stam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mart_Stam "Mart Stam") and [Gerrit Rietveld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerrit_Rietveld "Gerrit Rietveld") from the Netherlands, and [Adolf Loos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Loos "Adolf Loos") from Czechoslovakia. A delegation of Soviet architects was invited to attend, but they were unable to obtain visas. Later members included [Josep Lluís Sert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josep_Llu%C3%ADs_Sert "Josep Lluís Sert") of Spain and [Alvar Aalto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alvar_Aalto "Alvar Aalto") of Finland. No one attended from the United States. A second meeting was organized in 1930 in Brussels by Victor Bourgeois on the topic "Rational methods for groups of habitations". A third meeting, on "The functional city", was scheduled for Moscow in 1932, but was cancelled at the last minute. Instead, the delegates held their meeting on a cruise ship traveling between Marseille and Athens. On board, they together drafted a text on how modern cities should be organized. The text, called The [Athens Charter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athens_Charter "Athens Charter"), after considerable editing by Corbusier and others, was finally published in 1957 and became an influential text for city planners in the 1950s and 1960s. The group met once more in Paris in 1937 to discuss public housing and was scheduled to meet in the United States in 1939, but the meeting was cancelled because of the war. The legacy of the CIAM was a roughly common style and doctrine which helped define modern architecture in Europe and the United States after World War II.[^40]

## Art Deco

- ![Pavilion of the Galeries Lafayette Department Store at the Paris International Exposition of Decorative Arts (1925)](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Paris-FR-75-Expo_1925_Arts_d%C3%A9coratifs-pavillon_des_Galeries_Lafayette.jpg/500px-Paris-FR-75-Expo_1925_Arts_d%C3%A9coratifs-pavillon_des_Galeries_Lafayette.jpg)

	Pavilion of the [Galeries Lafayette](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galeries_Lafayette "Galeries Lafayette") Department Store at the [Paris International Exposition of Decorative Arts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Exhibition_of_Modern_Decorative_and_Industrial_Arts "International Exhibition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts") (1925)
- ![La Samaritaine department store, by Henri Sauvage, Paris, (1925–28)](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/P1030956_Paris_Ier_La_Samaritaine_rwk.JPG/500px-P1030956_Paris_Ier_La_Samaritaine_rwk.JPG)
	La Samaritaine department store, by Henri Sauvage, Paris, (1925–28)
	[La Samaritaine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_Samaritaine "La Samaritaine") department store, by [Henri Sauvage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Sauvage "Henri Sauvage"), Paris, (1925–28)

The [Art Deco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_Deco "Art Deco") architectural style (called *Style Moderne* in France), was modern, but it was not modernist; it had many features of modernism, including the use of reinforced concrete, glass, steel, chrome, and it rejected traditional historical models, such as the [Beaux-Arts style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beaux-Arts_style "Beaux-Arts style") and [Neo-classicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-classicism "Neo-classicism"); but, unlike the modernist styles of Le Corbusier and Mies van der Rohe, it made lavish use of decoration and color. It reveled in the symbols of modernity; lightning flashes, sunrises, and zig-zags. Art Deco had begun in France before World War I and spread through Europe; in the 1920s and 1930s it became a highly popular style in the United States, South America, South Africa, India, China, Australia, and Japan. In Europe, Art Deco was particularly popular for department stores and movie theaters. The style reached its peak in Europe at the [International Exhibition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Exhibition_of_Modern_Decorative_and_Industrial_Arts "International Exhibition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts") in 1925, which featured art deco pavilions and decoration from twenty countries. Only two pavilions were purely modernist; the Esprit Nouveau pavilion of Le Corbusier, which represented his idea for a mass-produced housing unit, and the pavilion of the USSR, by [Konstantin Melnikov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konstantin_Melnikov "Konstantin Melnikov") in a flamboyantly [futurist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Futurist_architecture "Futurist architecture") style.[^41]

Later French landmarks in the Art Deco style included the [Grand Rex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_Rex "Grand Rex") movie theater in Paris, [La Samaritaine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_Samaritaine "La Samaritaine") department store by [Henri Sauvage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Sauvage "Henri Sauvage") (1926–28) and the Social and Economic Council building in Paris (1937–38) by [Auguste Perret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auguste_Perret "Auguste Perret"), and the [Palais de Tokyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palais_de_Tokyo "Palais de Tokyo") and [Palais de Chaillot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palais_de_Chaillot "Palais de Chaillot"), both built by collectives of architects for the 1937 Paris [Exposition Internationale des Arts et Techniques dans la Vie Moderne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exposition_Internationale_des_Arts_et_Techniques_dans_la_Vie_Moderne "Exposition Internationale des Arts et Techniques dans la Vie Moderne").[^42]

In the late 1920s and early 1930s, an exuberant American variant of Art Deco appeared in the [Chrysler Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chrysler_Building "Chrysler Building"), [Empire State Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_State_Building "Empire State Building") and [Rockefeller Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rockefeller_Center "Rockefeller Center") in New York City, and [Guardian Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guardian_Building "Guardian Building") in Detroit. The first skyscrapers in Chicago and New York had been designed in a neo-gothic or neoclassical style, but these buildings were very different; they combined modern materials and technology (stainless steel, concrete, aluminum, chrome-plated steel) with Art Deco geometry; stylized zig-zags, lightning flashes, fountains, sunrises, and, at the top of the Chrysler building, Art Deco "gargoyles" in the form of stainless steel radiator ornaments. The interiors of these new buildings, sometimes termed Cathedrals of Commerce", were lavishly decorated in bright contrasting colors, with geometric patterns variously influenced by Egyptian and Mayan pyramids, African textile patterns, and European cathedrals, [Frank Lloyd Wright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright "Frank Lloyd Wright") himself experimented with [Mayan Revival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mayan_Revival_architecture "Mayan Revival architecture"), in the concrete cube-based [Ennis House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ennis_House "Ennis House") of 1924 in Los Angeles. The style appeared in the late 1920s and 1930s in all major American cities. The style was used most often in office buildings, but it also appeared in the enormous movie palaces that were built in large cities when sound films were introduced.[^43]

The beginning of the [Great Depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Depression "Great Depression") in 1929 brought an end to lavishly decorated Art Deco architecture and a temporary halt to the construction of new skyscrapers. It also brought in a new style, called " [Streamline Moderne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Streamline_Moderne "Streamline Moderne") " or sometimes just Streamline. This style, sometimes modeled after for the form of ocean liners, featured rounded corners, strong horizontal lines, and often nautical features, such as superstructures and steel railings. It was associated with modernity and especially with transportation; the style was often used for new airport terminals, train and bus stations, and for gas stations and diners built along the growing American highway system. In the 1930s the style was used not only in buildings, but in railroad locomotives, and even refrigerators and vacuum cleaners. It both borrowed from [industrial design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_design "Industrial design") and influenced it.[^44]

In the United States, the Great Depression led to a new style for government buildings, sometimes called [PWA Moderne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PWA_Moderne "PWA Moderne"), for the [Public Works Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_Works_Administration "Public Works Administration"), which launched gigantic construction programs in the U.S. to stimulate employment. It was essentially classical architecture stripped of ornament, and was employed in state and federal buildings, from post offices to the largest office building in the world at that time, [Pentagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Pentagon "The Pentagon") (1941–43), begun just before the United States entered the Second World War.[^45]

During the 1920s and 1930s, Frank Lloyd Wright resolutely refused to associate himself with any architectural movements. He considered his architecture to be entirely unique and his own. Between 1916 and 1922, he broke away from his earlier prairie house style and worked instead on houses decorated with textured blocks of cement; this became known as his "Mayan style", after the pyramids of the ancient Mayan civilization. He experimented for a time with modular mass-produced housing. He identified his architecture as "Usonian", a combination of USA, "utopian" and "organic social order". His business was severely affected by the beginning of the [Great Depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Depression "Great Depression") that began in 1929; he had fewer wealthy clients who wanted to experiment. Between 1928 and 1935, he built only two buildings: a hotel near [Chandler, Arizona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandler,_Arizona "Chandler, Arizona"), and the most famous of all his residences, [Fallingwater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fallingwater "Fallingwater") (1934–37), a vacation house in Pennsylvania for Edgar J. Kaufman. Fallingwater is a remarkable structure of concrete slabs suspended over a waterfall, perfectly uniting architecture and nature.[^46]

The Austrian architect [Rudolph Schindler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolph_Schindler_\(architect\) "Rudolph Schindler (architect)") designed what could be called the first house in the modern style in 1922, the Schindler house. Schindler also contributed to American modernism with his design for the [Lovell Beach House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lovell_Beach_House "Lovell Beach House") in [Newport Beach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newport_Beach "Newport Beach"). The Austrian architect [Richard Neutra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Neutra "Richard Neutra") moved to the United States in 1923, worked for a short time with Frank Lloyd Wright, also quickly became a force in American architecture through his modernist design for the same client, the [Lovell Health House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lovell_House "Lovell House") in Los Angeles. Neutra's most notable architectural work was the [Kaufmann Desert House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaufmann_Desert_House "Kaufmann Desert House") in 1946, and he designed hundreds of further projects.[^47]

The [1937 Paris International Exposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exposition_Internationale_des_Arts_et_Techniques_dans_la_Vie_Moderne "Exposition Internationale des Arts et Techniques dans la Vie Moderne") in Paris effectively marked the end of the Art Deco, and of pre-war architectural styles. Most of the pavilions were in a neoclassical Deco style, with colonnades and sculptural decoration. The pavilions of Nazi Germany, designed by [Albert Speer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Speer "Albert Speer"), in a German neoclassical style topped by eagle and swastika, faced the pavilion of the Soviet Union, topped by enormous statues of a worker and a peasant carrying a hammer and sickle. As to the modernists, Le Corbusier was practically, but not quite invisible at the Exposition; he participated in the Pavilion des temps nouveaux, but focused mainly on his painting.[^48] The one modernist who did attract attention was a collaborator of Le Corbusier, [Josep Lluis Sert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josep_Lluis_Sert "Josep Lluis Sert"), the Spanish architect, whose pavilion of the [Second Spanish Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Spanish_Republic "Second Spanish Republic") was pure modernist glass and steel box. Inside it displayed the most modernist work of the Exposition, the painting *[Guernica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guernica_\(Picasso\) "Guernica (Picasso)")* by [Pablo Picasso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pablo_Picasso "Pablo Picasso"). The original building was destroyed after the Exposition, but it was recreated in 1992 in Barcelona.

The rise of nationalism in the 1930s was reflected in the [Fascist architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fascist_architecture "Fascist architecture") of Italy, and [Nazi architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_architecture "Nazi architecture") of Germany, based on classical styles and designed to express power and grandeur. The Nazi architecture, much of it designed by [Albert Speer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Speer "Albert Speer"), was intended to awe the spectators by its huge scale. Adolf Hitler intended to turn Berlin into the capital of Europe, grander than Rome or Paris. The Nazis closed the Bauhaus, and the most prominent modern architects soon departed for Britain or the United States. In Italy, Benito Mussolini wished to present himself as the heir to the glory and empire of ancient Rome.[^49] Mussolini's government was not as hostile to modernism as The Nazis; the spirit of [Italian Rationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationalism_\(architecture\) "Rationalism (architecture)") of the 1920s continued, with the work of architect [Giuseppe Terragni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giuseppe_Terragni "Giuseppe Terragni"). His *Casa del Fascio* in Como, headquarters of the local Fascist party, was a perfectly modernist building, with geometric proportions (33.2 meters long by 16.6 meters high), a clean façade of marble, and a Renaissance-inspired interior courtyard. Opposed to Terragni was Marcello Piacitini, a proponent of monumental fascist architecture, who rebuilt the University of Rome, and designed the Italian pavilion at the 1937 Paris Exposition, and planned a grand reconstruction of Rome on the fascist model.[^50]

[World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II") (1939–1945) and its aftermath was a major factor in driving innovation in building technology, and in turn, architectural possibilities.[^45] [^52] The wartime industrial demands resulted in shortages of steel and other building materials, leading to the adoption of new materials, such as aluminum, The war and postwar period brought greatly expanded use of [prefabricated building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prefabricated_building "Prefabricated building"); largely for the military and government. The semi-circular metal [Nissen hut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nissen_hut "Nissen hut") of World War I was revived as the [Quonset hut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quonset_hut "Quonset hut"). The years immediately after the war saw the development of radical experimental houses, including the enameled-steel [Lustron house](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lustron_house "Lustron house") (1947–1950), and Buckminster Fuller's experimental aluminum [Dymaxion House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dymaxion_House "Dymaxion House").[^52] [^53]

The unprecedented destruction caused by the war was another factor in the rise of modern architecture. Large parts of major cities, from Berlin, Tokyo, and Dresden to Rotterdam and east London; all the port cities of France, particularly [Le Havre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Havre "Le Havre"), Brest, Marseille, Cherbourg had been destroyed by bombing. In the United States, little civilian construction had been done since the 1920s; housing was needed for millions of American soldiers returning from the war. The postwar housing shortages in Europe and the United States led to the design and construction of enormous government-financed housing projects, usually in run-down center of American cities, and in the suburbs of Paris and other European cities, where land was available,

One of the largest reconstruction projects was that of the city center of Le Havre, destroyed by the Germans and by Allied bombing in 1944; 133 hectares of buildings in the center were flattened, destroying 12,500 buildings and leaving 40,000 persons homeless. The architect [Auguste Perret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auguste_Perret "Auguste Perret"), a pioneer in the use of reinforced concrete and prefabricated materials, designed and built an entirely new center to the city, with apartment blocks, cultural, commercial, and government buildings. He restored historic monuments when possible, and built a new church, St. Joseph, with a lighthouse-like tower in the center to inspire hope. His rebuilt city was declared a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2005.[^54]

## Le Corbusier and the Cité Radieuse (1947–1952)

- ![The Chapel of Notre-Dame-du-Haut in Ronchamp (1950–1955)](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/59/RonchampCorbu.jpg/500px-RonchampCorbu.jpg)
	The Chapel of Notre-Dame-du-Haut in Ronchamp (1950–1955)
	The [Chapel of Notre-Dame-du-Haut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Notre_Dame_du_Haut "Notre Dame du Haut") in Ronchamp (1950–1955)

Shortly after the War, the French architect [Le Corbusier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Corbusier "Le Corbusier"), who was nearly sixty years old and had not constructed a building in ten years, was commissioned by the French government to construct a new apartment block in [Marseille](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marseille "Marseille"). He called it [Unité d'Habitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit%C3%A9_d%27Habitation "Unité d'Habitation") in Marseille, but it more popularly took the name of the [Cité Radieuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cit%C3%A9_Radieuse "Cité Radieuse") (and later "Cité du Fada" "City of the crazy one" in Marseille French), after his book about futuristic urban planning. Following his doctrines of design, the building had a concrete frame raised up above the street on pylons. It contained 337 duplex apartment units, fit into the framework like pieces of a puzzle. Each unit had two levels and a small terrace. Interior "streets" had shops, a nursery school, and other serves, and the flat terrace roof had a running track, ventilation ducts, and a small theater. Le Corbusier designed furniture, carpets, and lamps to go with the building, all purely functional; the only decoration was a choice of interior colors that Le Corbusier gave to residents. Unité d'Habitation became a prototype for similar buildings in other cities, both in France and Germany. Combined with his equally radical organic design for the [Chapel of Notre-Dame du-Haut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Notre_Dame_du_Haut "Notre Dame du Haut") at [Ronchamp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronchamp "Ronchamp"), this work propelled Corbusier in the first rank of postwar modern architects.[^55]

## Team X and the 1953 International Congress of Modern Architecture

In the early 1950s, [Michel Écochard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_%C3%89cochard "Michel Écochard"), director of urban planning under the [French Protectorate in Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Protectorate_in_Morocco "French Protectorate in Morocco"), commissioned GAMMA (*[Groupe des Architectes Modernes Marocains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Groupe_des_Architectes_Modernes_Marocains "Groupe des Architectes Modernes Marocains")*)—which initially included the architects [Elie Azagury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elie_Azagury "Elie Azagury"), [George Candillis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Candilis "Georges Candilis"), [Alexis Josic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexis_Josic "Alexis Josic") and [Shadrach Woods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shadrach_Woods "Shadrach Woods") —to design housing in the [Hay Mohammedi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hay_Mohammadi "Hay Mohammadi") neighborhood of [Casablanca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casablanca "Casablanca") that provided a "culturally specific living tissue" for laborers and migrants [from the countryside](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rural_flight "Rural flight").[^56] [Sémiramis](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=S%C3%A9miramis_\(Casablanca\)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Sémiramis (Casablanca) (page does not exist)"), *[Nid d’Abeille](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nid_d%E2%80%99Abeille&action=edit&redlink=1 "Nid d’Abeille (page does not exist)")* (Honeycomb), and [Carrières Centrales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carri%C3%A8res_Centrales "Carrières Centrales") were some of the first examples of this [Vernacular Modernism](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Vernacular_Modernism&action=edit&redlink=1 "Vernacular Modernism (page does not exist)").[^57]

At the 1953 [*Congrès Internationaux d'Architecture* Moderne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congr%C3%A8s_Internationaux_d%27Architecture_Moderne "Congrès Internationaux d'Architecture Moderne") (CIAM), [ATBAT-Afrique](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ATBAT-Afrique&action=edit&redlink=1 "ATBAT-Afrique (page does not exist)") —the Africa branch of *[Atelier des Bâtisseurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Atelier_des_B%C3%A2tisseurs&action=edit&redlink=1 "Atelier des Bâtisseurs (page does not exist)")* founded in 1947 by figures including [Le Corbusier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Corbusier "Le Corbusier"), [Vladimir Bodiansky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Bodiansky "Vladimir Bodiansky"), and [André Wogenscky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Wogenscky "André Wogenscky") —prepared a study of Casablanca's [bidonvilles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanty_town "Shanty town") entitled "Habitat for the Greatest Number".[^58] The presenters, [Georges Candilis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Candilis "Georges Candilis") and [Michel Ecochard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_%C3%89cochard "Michel Écochard"), argued—against doctrine—that architects must consider local culture and climate in their designs.[^59] [^56] [^60] This generated great debate among modernist architects around the world and eventually provoked a schism and the creation of [Team 10](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Team_10 "Team 10").[^59] [^61] [^62] Ecochard's 8x8 meter model at Carrières Centrales earned him recognition as a pioneer in the architecture of [collective housing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-family_residential "Multi-family residential"),[^63] [^64] though his Moroccan colleague [Elie Azagury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elie_Azagury "Elie Azagury") was critical of him for serving as a tool of the French colonial regime and for ignoring the economic and social necessity that Moroccans live in higher density vertical housing.[^65]

## Late modernist architecture

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/FL_Ponte_Vedra_Beach_Arthur_Milam_House07.jpg/250px-FL_Ponte_Vedra_Beach_Arthur_Milam_House07.jpg)

The Milam Residence: an early example of Late modernist architecture.

Late modernist architecture is generally understood to include buildings designed (1968–1980) with exceptions. [Modernist architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modernist_architecture "Modernist architecture") includes the buildings designed between 1945 and the 1960s. The late modernist style is characterized by bold shapes and sharp corners, slightly more defined than [Brutalist architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brutalist_architecture "Brutalist architecture").[^66]

## Postwar modernism in the United States (1945–1985)

The [International Style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Style_\(architecture\) "International Style (architecture)") of architecture had appeared in Europe, particularly in the [Bauhaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bauhaus "Bauhaus") movement, in the late 1920s. In 1932 it was recognized and given a name at an Exhibition at the [Museum of Modern Art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art "Museum of Modern Art") in New York City organized by architect [Philip Johnson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Johnson "Philip Johnson") and architectural critic [Henry-Russell Hitchcock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry-Russell_Hitchcock "Henry-Russell Hitchcock"), Between 1937 and 1941, following the rise of Hitler and the Nazis in Germany, most of the leaders of the German Bauhaus movement found a new home in the United States, and played an important part in the development of American modern architecture.

[Frank Lloyd Wright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright "Frank Lloyd Wright") was eighty years old in 1947; he had been present at the beginning of American modernism, and though he refused to accept that he belonged to any movement, continued to play a leading role almost to its end. One of his most original late projects was the campus of [Florida Southern College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Florida_Southern_College "Florida Southern College") in [Lakeland, Florida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lakeland,_Florida "Lakeland, Florida"), begun in 1941 and completed in 1943. He designed nine new buildings in a style that he described as "The [Child of the Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child_of_the_Sun "Child of the Sun") ". He wrote that he wanted the campus to "grow out of the ground and into the light, a child of the sun".

He completed several notable projects in the 1940s, including the [Johnson Wax Headquarters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnson_Wax_Headquarters "Johnson Wax Headquarters") and the [Price Tower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Price_Tower "Price Tower") in [Bartlesville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartlesville "Bartlesville"), Oklahoma (1956). The building is unusual that it is supported by its central core of four elevator shafts; the rest of the building is cantilevered to this core, like the branches of a tree. Wright originally planned the structure for an apartment building in New York City. That project was cancelled because of the [Great Depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Depression "Great Depression"), and he adapted the design for an oil pipeline and equipment company in Oklahoma. He wrote that in New York City his building would have been lost in a forest of tall buildings, but that in Oklahoma it stood alone. The design is asymmetrical; each side is different.

In 1943 he was commissioned by the art collector [Solomon R. Guggenheim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon_R._Guggenheim "Solomon R. Guggenheim") to design a museum for his collection of modern art. His design was entirely original; a bowl-shaped building with a spiral ramp inside that led museum visitors on an upward tour of the art of the 20th century. Work began in 1946 but it was not completed until 1959, the year that he died.[^51]

[Walter Gropius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Gropius "Walter Gropius"), the founder of the [Bauhaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bauhaus "Bauhaus"), moved to England in 1934 and spent three years there before being invited to the United States by Walter Hudnut of the [Harvard Graduate School of Design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_Graduate_School_of_Design "Harvard Graduate School of Design"); Gropius became the head of the architecture faculty. [Marcel Breuer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcel_Breuer "Marcel Breuer"), who had worked with him at the Bauhaus, joined him and opened an office in Cambridge. The fame of Gropius and Breuer attracted many students, who themselves became famous architects, including [Ieoh Ming Pei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ieoh_Ming_Pei "Ieoh Ming Pei") and [Philip Johnson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Johnson "Philip Johnson"). They did not receive an important commission until 1941, when they designed housing for workers in Kensington, Pennsylvania, near Pittsburgh., In 1945 Gropius and Breuer associated with a group of younger architects under the name TAC ([The Architects Collaborative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Architects_Collaborative "The Architects Collaborative")). Their notable works included the building of the [Harvard Graduate School of Design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_Graduate_School_of_Design "Harvard Graduate School of Design"), the U.S. Embassy in Athens (1956–57), and the headquarters of Pan American Airways in New York (1958–63).[^67]

[Ludwig Mies van der Rohe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Mies_van_der_Rohe "Ludwig Mies van der Rohe") described his architecture with the famous saying, "Less is more". As the director of the school of architecture of what is now called the [Illinois Institute of Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illinois_Institute_of_Technology "Illinois Institute of Technology") from 1939 to 1956, Mies (as he was commonly known) made Chicago the leading city for American modernism in the postwar years. He constructed new buildings for the Institute in modernist style, two high-rise apartment buildings on Lakeshore Drive (1948–51), which became models for high-rises across the country. Other major works included [Farnsworth House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Farnsworth_House "Farnsworth House") in [Plano, Illinois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plano,_Illinois "Plano, Illinois") (1945–1951), a simple horizontal glass box that had an enormous influence on American residential architecture. The Chicago Convention Center (1952–54) and [Crown Hall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crown_Hall "Crown Hall") at the Illinois Institute of Technology (1950–56), and The [Seagram Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seagram_Building "Seagram Building") in New York City (1954–58) also set a new standard for purity and elegance. Based on granite pillars, the smooth glass and steel walls were given a touch of color by the use of bronze-toned I-beams in the structure. He returned to Germany in 1962–68 to build the new Nationalgallerie in Berlin. His students and followers included [Philip Johnson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Johnson "Philip Johnson"), and [Eero Saarinen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eero_Saarinen "Eero Saarinen"), whose work was substantially influenced by his ideas.[^68]

Influential residential architects in the new style in the United States included [Richard Neutra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Neutra "Richard Neutra") and [Charles and Ray Eames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_and_Ray_Eames "Charles and Ray Eames"). The most celebrated work of the Eames was [Eames House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eames_House "Eames House") in [Pacific Palisades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Palisades,_Los_Angeles "Pacific Palisades, Los Angeles"), California, (1949) Charles Eames in collaboration with [Eero Saarinen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eero_Saarinen "Eero Saarinen") It is composed of two structures, an architects residence and his studio, joined in the form of an L. The house, influenced by Japanese architecture, is made of translucent and transparent panels organized in simple volumes, often using natural materials, supported on a steel framework. The frame of the house was assembled in sixteen hours by five workmen. He brightened up his buildings with panels of pure colors.[^69]

[Richard Neutra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Neutra "Richard Neutra") continued to build influential houses in Los Angeles, using the theme of the simple box. Many of these houses erased the line distinction between indoor and outdoor spaces with walls of plate glass.[^70] Neutra's [Constance Perkins House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constance_Perkins_House "Constance Perkins House") in [Pasadena, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasadena,_California "Pasadena, California") (1962) was re-examination of the modest single-family dwelling. It was built of inexpensive material–wood, plaster, and glass–and completed at a cost of just under $18,000. Neutra scaled the house to the physical dimensions of its owner, a small woman. It features a reflecting pool which meanders under of the glass walls of the house. One of Neutra's most unusual buildings was [Shepherd's Grove](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shepherd%27s_Grove "Shepherd's Grove") in [Garden Grove, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garden_Grove,_California "Garden Grove, California"), which featured an adjoining parking lot where worshippers could follow the service without leaving their cars.

Many of the notable modern buildings in the postwar years were produced by two architectural mega-agencies, which brought together large teams of designers for very complex projects. The firm of [Skidmore, Owings & Merrill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skidmore,_Owings_%26_Merrill "Skidmore, Owings & Merrill") was founded in Chicago in 1936 by [Louis Skidmore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Skidmore "Louis Skidmore") and [Nathaniel Owings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nathaniel_Owings "Nathaniel Owings"), and joined in 1939 by engineer [John Merrill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_O._Merrill "John O. Merrill"), It soon went under the name of SOM. Its first big project was [Oak Ridge National Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory "Oak Ridge National Laboratory") in [Oak Ridge, Tennessee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oak_Ridge,_Tennessee "Oak Ridge, Tennessee"), the gigantic government installation that produced plutonium for the first nuclear weapons. In 1964 the firm had eighteen "partner-owners", 54 "associate participants, "and 750 architects, technicians, designers, decorators, and landscape architects. Their style was largely inspired by the work of [Ludwig Mies van der Rohe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Mies_van_der_Rohe "Ludwig Mies van der Rohe"), and their buildings soon had a large place in the New York skyline, including the [Manhattan House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manhattan_House "Manhattan House") (1950–51), [Lever House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lever_House "Lever House") (1951–52) and the [Manufacturers Trust Company Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manufacturers_Trust_Company_Building "Manufacturers Trust Company Building") (1954). Later buildings by the firm include [Beinecke Library](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beinecke_Library "Beinecke Library") at [Yale University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yale_University "Yale University") (1963), the [Willis Tower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willis_Tower "Willis Tower"), formerly Sears Tower in Chicago (1973) and [One World Trade Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_World_Trade_Center "One World Trade Center") in New York City (2013), which replaced the building destroyed in the terrorist attack of 11 September 2001.[^71]

[Wallace Harrison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wallace_Harrison "Wallace Harrison") played a major part in the modern architectural history of New York; as the architectural advisor of the [Rockefeller Family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rockefeller_Family "Rockefeller Family"), he helped design [Rockefeller Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rockefeller_Center "Rockefeller Center"), the major Art Deco architectural project of the 1930s. He was supervising architect for the 1939 New York World's Fair, and, with his partner [Max Abramowitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Abramowitz "Max Abramowitz"), was the builder and chief architect of the [headquarters of the United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Headquarters_of_the_United_Nations "Headquarters of the United Nations"); Harrison headed a committee of international architects, which included [Oscar Niemeyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscar_Niemeyer "Oscar Niemeyer") (who produced the original plan approved by the committee) and [Le Corbusier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Corbusier "Le Corbusier"). Other landmark New York buildings designed by Harrison and his firm included [Metropolitan Opera House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Opera_House_\(Lincoln_Center\) "Metropolitan Opera House (Lincoln Center)"), the master plan for [Lincoln Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lincoln_Center "Lincoln Center"), and [John F. Kennedy International Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_International_Airport "John F. Kennedy International Airport").[^72]

[Philip Johnson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Johnson "Philip Johnson") (1906–2005) was one of the youngest and last major figures in American modern architecture. He trained at Harvard with Walter Gropius, then was director of the department of architecture and modern design at the [Metropolitan Museum of Art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art "Metropolitan Museum of Art") from 1946 to 1954. In 1947, he published a book about [Ludwig Mies van der Rohe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Mies_van_der_Rohe "Ludwig Mies van der Rohe"), and in 1953 designed his own residence, the [Glass House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass_House_\(New_Canaan,_Connecticut\) "Glass House (New Canaan, Connecticut)") in [New Canaan, Connecticut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Canaan,_Connecticut "New Canaan, Connecticut") in a style modeled after Mies's [Farnsworth House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Farnsworth_House "Farnsworth House"). Beginning in 1955 he began to go in his own direction, moving gradually toward expressionism with designs that increasingly departed from the orthodoxies of modern architecture. His final and decisive break with modern architecture was the AT&T Building (later known as the Sony Tower), and now the [550 Madison Avenue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/550_Madison_Avenue "550 Madison Avenue") in New York City, (1979) an essentially modernist skyscraper completely altered by the addition of broken [pediment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pediment "Pediment") with a circular opening. This building is generally considered to mark the beginning of [Postmodern architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodern_architecture "Postmodern architecture") in the United States.[^72]

[Eero Saarinen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eero_Saarinen "Eero Saarinen") (1910–1961) was the son of [Eliel Saarinen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eliel_Saarinen "Eliel Saarinen"), the most famous Finnish architect of the Art Nouveau period, who emigrated to the United States in 1923, when Eero was thirteen. He studied art and sculpture at the academy where his father taught, and then at the [Académie de la Grande Chaumière](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_de_la_Grande_Chaumi%C3%A8re "Académie de la Grande Chaumière") Academy in Paris before studying architecture at Yale University. His architectural designs were more like enormous pieces of sculpture than traditional modern buildings; he broke away from the elegant boxes inspired by Mies van der Rohe and used instead sweeping curves and parabolas, like the wings of birds. In 1948 he conceived the idea of a monument in St. Louis, Missouri in the form of a parabolic arch 192 meters high, made of stainless steel (1948). He then designed the [General Motors Technical Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Motors_Technical_Center "General Motors Technical Center") in Warren, Michigan (1949–55), a glass modernist box in the style of Mies van der Rohe, followed by the IBM Research Center in Yorktown, Virginia (1957–61). His next works were a major departure in style; he produced a particularly striking sculptural design for the [Ingalls Rink](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ingalls_Rink "Ingalls Rink") in [New Haven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Haven "New Haven"), Connecticut (1956–59, an ice skiing rink with a parabolic roof suspended from cables, which served as a preliminary model for next and most famous work, the [TWA Terminal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TWA_Terminal "TWA Terminal") at JFK airport in New York (1956–1962). His declared intention was to design a building that was distinctive and memorable, and also one that would capture the particular excitement of passengers before a journey. The structure is separated into four white concrete parabolic vaults, which together resemble a bird on the ground perched for flight. Each of the four curving roof vaults has two sides attached to columns in a Y form just outside the structure. One of the angles of each shell is lightly raised, and the other is attached to the center of the structure. The roof is connected with the ground by curtain walls of glass. All of the details inside the building, including the benches, counters, escalators, and clocks, were designed in the same style.[^73]

[Louis Kahn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Kahn "Louis Kahn") (1901–74) was another American architect who moved away from the Mies van der Rohe model of the glass box, and other dogmas of the prevailing international style. He borrowed from a wide variety of styles, and idioms, including neoclassicism. He was a professor of architecture at Yale University from 1947 to 1957, where his students included [Eero Saarinen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eero_Saarinen "Eero Saarinen"). From 1957 until his death he was a professor of architecture at the [University of Pennsylvania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania "University of Pennsylvania"). His work and ideas influenced [Philip Johnson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Johnson "Philip Johnson"), [Minoru Yamasaki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minoru_Yamasaki "Minoru Yamasaki"), and [Edward Durell Stone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Durell_Stone "Edward Durell Stone") as they moved toward a more neoclassical style. Unlike Mies, he did not try to make his buildings look light; he constructed mainly with concrete and brick, and made his buildings look monumental and solid. He drew from a wide variety of different sources; the towers of [Richards Medical Research Laboratories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richards_Medical_Research_Laboratories "Richards Medical Research Laboratories") were inspired by the architecture of the Renaissance towns he had seen in Italy as a resident architect at the [American Academy in Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Academy_in_Rome "American Academy in Rome") in 1950. Notable buildings by Kahn in the United States include the [First Unitarian Church of Rochester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Unitarian_Church_of_Rochester_\(building\) "First Unitarian Church of Rochester (building)"), New York (1962); and the [Kimball Art Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kimball_Art_Museum "Kimball Art Museum") in [Fort Worth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fort_Worth "Fort Worth"), Texas (1966–72). Following the example of [Le Corbusier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Corbusier "Le Corbusier") and his design of the government buildings in [Chandigarh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandigarh "Chandigarh"), the capital city of the [Haryana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haryana "Haryana") & [Punjab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punjab,_India "Punjab, India") State of India, Kahn designed the [Jatiyo Sangshad Bhaban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jatiyo_Sangshad_Bhaban "Jatiyo Sangshad Bhaban") (National Assembly Building) in [Dhaka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhaka "Dhaka"), [Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh "Bangladesh") (1962–74), when that country won independence from Pakistan. It was Kahn's last work.[^74]

[I. M. Pei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I._M._Pei "I. M. Pei") (1917–2019) was a major figure in late modernism and the debut of [Post-modern architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-modern_architecture "Post-modern architecture"). He was born in China and educated in the United States, studying architecture at the [Massachusetts Institute of Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology "Massachusetts Institute of Technology"). While the architecture school there still trained in the [Beaux-Arts architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beaux-Arts_architecture "Beaux-Arts architecture") style, Pei discovered the writings of [Le Corbusier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Corbusier "Le Corbusier"), and a two-day visit by Le Corbusier to the campus in 1935 had a major impact on Pei's ideas of architecture. In the late 1930s, he moved to the [Harvard Graduate School of Design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_Graduate_School_of_Design "Harvard Graduate School of Design"), where he studied with [Walter Gropius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Gropius "Walter Gropius") and [Marcel Breuer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcel_Breuer "Marcel Breuer") and became deeply involved in Modernism.[^75] After the war he worked on large projects for the New York real estate developer [William Zeckendorf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Zeckendorf "William Zeckendorf"), before breaking away and starting his own firm. One of the first buildings his own firm designed was the [Green Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Building_\(MIT\) "Green Building (MIT)") at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. While the clean modernist façade was admired, the building developed an unexpected problem; it created a wind tunnel effect, and in strong winds the doors could not be opened. Pei was forced to construct a tunnel so visitors could enter the building during high winds.

Between 1963 and 1967 Pei designed the [Mesa Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesa_Laboratory "Mesa Laboratory") for the [National Center for Atmospheric Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research "National Center for Atmospheric Research") outside [Boulder, Colorado](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boulder,_Colorado "Boulder, Colorado"), in an open area at the foothills of the Rocky Mountains. The project differed from Pei's earlier urban work; it would rest in an open area in the foothills of the [Rocky Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocky_Mountains "Rocky Mountains"). His design was a striking departure from traditional modernism; it looked as if it were carved out of the side of the mountain.[^76]

In the late modernist area, art museums bypassed skyscrapers as the most prestigious architectural projects; they offered greater possibilities for innovation in form and more visibility. Pei established himself with his design for the [Herbert F. Johnson Museum of Art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbert_F._Johnson_Museum_of_Art "Herbert F. Johnson Museum of Art") at [Cornell University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornell_University "Cornell University") in [Ithaca, New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ithaca,_New_York "Ithaca, New York") (1973), which was praised for its imaginative use of a small space, and its respect for the landscape and other buildings around it. This led to the commission for one of the most important museum projects of the period, the new East Wing of the [National Gallery of Art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Art "National Gallery of Art") in Washington, completed in 1978, and to another of Pei's most famous projects, the pyramid at the entrance of [Louvre Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louvre_Museum "Louvre Museum") in Paris (1983–89). Pei chose the pyramid as the form that best harmonized with the Renaissance and neoclassical forms of the historic Louvre, as well as for its associations with Napoleon and the [Battle of the Pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Pyramids "Battle of the Pyramids"). Each face of the pyramid is supported by 128 beams of stainless steel, supporting 675 panels of glass, each 2.9 by 1.9 meters (9 ft 6 in by 6 ft 3 in).[^77]

### Fazlur Rahman Khan

- ![John Hancock Center in Chicago by Fazlur Rahman Khan was the first building to use X-bracing to create the trussed-tube design.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Hancock_tower_2006.jpg/250px-Hancock_tower_2006.jpg)
	John Hancock Center in Chicago by Fazlur Rahman Khan was the first building to use X-bracing to create the trussed-tube design.
	[John Hancock Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Hancock_Center "John Hancock Center") in Chicago by [Fazlur Rahman Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fazlur_Rahman_Khan "Fazlur Rahman Khan") was the first building to use X-bracing to create the trussed-tube design.
- ![Willis Tower in Chicago was the first building to use the bundled-tube design.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/2004-07-14_2600x1500_chicago_lake_skyline.jpg/960px-2004-07-14_2600x1500_chicago_lake_skyline.jpg)
	Willis Tower in Chicago was the first building to use the bundled-tube design.
	[Willis Tower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willis_Tower "Willis Tower") in Chicago was the first building to use the bundled-tube design.

In 1955, employed by the architectural firm [Skidmore, Owings & Merrill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skidmore,_Owings_%26_Merrill "Skidmore, Owings & Merrill") (SOM), he began working in Chicago. He was made a partner in 1966. He worked the rest of his life side by side with Architect Bruce Graham.[^78] Khan introduced design methods and concepts for efficient use of material in building architecture. His first building to employ the tube structure was the [Chestnut De-Witt apartment building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chestnut_De-Witt_apartment_building "Chestnut De-Witt apartment building").[^79] During the 1960s and 1970s, he became noted for his designs for Chicago's 100-story [John Hancock Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Hancock_Center "John Hancock Center"), which was the first building to use the trussed-tube design, and 110-story Sears Tower, since renamed [Willis Tower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willis_Tower "Willis Tower"), the tallest building in the world from 1973 until 1998, which was the first building to use the framed-tube design.

He believed that engineers needed a broader perspective on life, saying, "The technical man must not be lost in his own technology; he must be able to appreciate life, and life is art, drama, music, and most importantly, people." Khan's personal papers, most of which were in his office at the time of his death, are held by the [Ryerson & Burnham Libraries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ryerson_%26_Burnham_Libraries "Ryerson & Burnham Libraries") at the [Art Institute of Chicago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_Institute_of_Chicago "Art Institute of Chicago"). The Fazlur Khan Collection includes manuscripts, sketches, audio cassette tapes, slides and other materials regarding his work.

Khan's seminal work of developing tall building structural systems are still used today as the starting point when considering design options for tall buildings. Tube structures have since been used in many skyscrapers, including the [construction of the World Trade Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construction_of_the_World_Trade_Center "Construction of the World Trade Center"), [Aon Centre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aon_Center_\(Chicago\) "Aon Center (Chicago)"), [Petronas Towers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petronas_Towers "Petronas Towers"), [Jin Mao Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jin_Mao_Building "Jin Mao Building"), [Bank of China Tower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_of_China_Tower,_Hong_Kong "Bank of China Tower, Hong Kong") and most other buildings in excess of 40 stories constructed since the 1960s. The strong influence of tube structure design is also evident in the world's current tallest skyscraper, the [Burj Khalifa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burj_Khalifa "Burj Khalifa") in [Dubai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dubai "Dubai"). According to Stephen Bayley of *[The Daily Telegraph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph "The Daily Telegraph")*:

> Khan invented a new way of building tall.... So Fazlur Khan created the unconventional skyscraper. Reversing the logic of the steel frame, he decided that the building's external envelope could – given enough trussing, framing and bracing – be the structure itself. This made buildings even lighter. The "bundled tube" meant buildings no longer need be boxlike in appearance: they could become sculpture. Khan's amazing insight – he was name-checked by Obama in his Cairo University speech last year – changed both the economics and the morphology of supertall buildings. And it made Burj Khalifa possible: proportionately, Burj employs perhaps half the steel that conservatively supports the Empire State Building.... Burj Khalifa is the ultimate expression of his audacious, lightweight design philosophy.[^80]

In the United States, Minoru Yamasaki found major independent success in implementing unique engineering solutions to then-complicated problems, including the space that elevator shafts took up on each floor, and dealing with his personal fear of heights. During this period, he created a number of office buildings which led to his innovative design of the 1,360 ft (410 m) towers of the World Trade Center in 1964, which began construction 21 March 1966.[^81] The first of the towers was finished in 1970.[^82] Many of his buildings feature superficial details inspired by the pointed arches of [Gothic architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gothic_architecture "Gothic architecture"), and make use of extremely narrow vertical windows. This narrow-windowed style arose from his own personal [fear of heights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrophobia "Acrophobia").[^83] One particular design challenge of the World Trade Center's design related to the efficacy of the elevator system, which was unique in the world. Yamasaki integrated the fastest elevators at the time, running at 1,700 feet per minute. Instead of placing a large traditional elevator shaft in the core of each tower, Yamasaki created the Twin Towers' " [Skylobby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sky_lobby "Sky lobby") " system. The Skylobby design created three separate, connected elevator systems which would serve different segments of the building, depending on which floor was chosen, saving approximately 70% of the space used for a traditional shaft. The space saved was then used for office space.[^84] In addition to these accomplishments, he had also designed the Pruitt-Igoe Housing Project, the largest ever housing project built in the United States, which was fully torn down in 1976 due to bad market conditions and the decrepit state of the buildings themselves. Separately, he had also designed the Century Plaza Towers and One Woodward Avenue, among 63 other projects he had developed during his career.

In France, [Le Corbusier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Corbusier "Le Corbusier") remained the most prominent architect, though he built few buildings there. His most prominent late work was the convent of [Sainte Marie de La Tourette](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sainte_Marie_de_La_Tourette "Sainte Marie de La Tourette") in Eveux-sur-l'Arbresle. The Convent, built of raw concrete, was austere and without ornament, inspired by the medieval monasteries he had visited on his first trip to Italy.[^85]

In Britain, the major figures in modernism included [Wells Coates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wells_Coates "Wells Coates") (1895–1958), [FRS Yorke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FRS_Yorke "FRS Yorke") (1906–1962), [James Stirling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Stirling_\(architect\) "James Stirling (architect)") (1926–1992) and [Denys Lasdun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denys_Lasdun "Denys Lasdun") (1914–2001). Lasdun's best-known work is the [Royal National Theatre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_National_Theatre "Royal National Theatre") (1967–1976) on the south bank of the Thames. Its raw concrete and blockish form offended British traditionalists; [Charles III, King of the U.K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_III "Charles III") compared it with a nuclear power station.

In [Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maison_Mattot "Maison Mattot"), a major figure was Charles Vandenhove (born 1927) who constructed an important series of buildings for the University Hospital Center in [Liège](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge "Liège"). His later work ventured into colorful rethinking of historical styles, such as Palladian architecture.[^86]

In Finland, the most influential architect was [Alvar Aalto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alvar_Aalto "Alvar Aalto"), who adapted his version of modernism to the Nordic landscape, light, and materials, particularly the use of wood. After World War II, he taught architecture in the United States. In Denmark, [Arne Jacobsen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arne_Jacobsen "Arne Jacobsen") was the best-known of the modernists, who designed furniture as well as carefully proportioned buildings.

In Italy, the most prominent modernist was [Gio Ponti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gio_Ponti "Gio Ponti"), who worked often with the structural engineer [Pier Luigi Nervi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pier_Luigi_Nervi "Pier Luigi Nervi"), a specialist in reinforced concrete. Nervi created concrete beams of exceptional length, twenty-five meters, which allowed greater flexibility in forms and greater heights. Their best-known design was the [Pirelli Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pirelli_Building "Pirelli Building") in Milan (1958–1960), which for decades was the tallest building in Italy.[^87]

The most famous Spanish modernist was the Catalan architect [Josep Lluis Sert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josep_Lluis_Sert "Josep Lluis Sert"), who worked with great success in Spain, France, and the United States. In his early career, he worked for a time under Le Corbusier, and designed the Spanish pavilion for the 1937 Paris Exposition. His notable later work included the [Fondation Maeght](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fondation_Maeght "Fondation Maeght") in Saint-Paul-de-Provence, France (1964), and the [Harvard Science Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_Science_Center "Harvard Science Center") in Cambridge, Massachusetts. He served as Dean of Architecture at the [Harvard School of Design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_School_of_Design "Harvard School of Design").

Notable German modernists included [Johannes Krahn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Krahn "Johannes Krahn"), who played an important part in rebuilding German cities after World War II, and built several important museums and churches, notably [St. Martin, Idstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Martin,_Idstein "St. Martin, Idstein"), which artfully combined stone masonry, concrete, and glass. Leading Austrian architects of the style included [Gustav Peichl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Peichl "Gustav Peichl"), whose later works included the [Art and Exhibition Center of the German Federal Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kunst-_und_Ausstellungshalle_der_Bundesrepublik_Deutschland "Kunst- und Ausstellungshalle der Bundesrepublik Deutschland") in Bonn, Germany (1989).

## Tropical Modernism

Tropical Modernism, or Tropical Modern, is a style of architecture that merges modernist architecture principles with tropical [vernacular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vernacular_architecture "Vernacular architecture") traditions, emerging in the mid-20th century. The term is used to describe modernist architecture in various regions of the world, including Latin America, Asia and Africa, as detailed below. Architects adapted to local conditions by using features which encouraged protection from harsh sunlight (such as solar shading) and encouraged the flow of cooling breezes through buildings (through narrow corridors).[^88] Some contend that the style originated in the "hot, humid conditions" of West Africa in the 1940s.[^89] Typical features include geometric screens. [Maxwell Fry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell_Fry "Maxwell Fry") and [Jane Drew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jane_Drew "Jane Drew"), of the [Architectural Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architectural_Association_School_of_Architecture "Architectural Association School of Architecture") architecture school in London, UK, made important contributions to research and practice in the Tropical Modernism style, after founding the School of Tropical Study at the [AA.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architectural_Association_School_of_Architecture "Architectural Association School of Architecture") Speaking about the adoption of modernism in post-independence Ghana, Professor [Ola Ukuku](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ola_Uduku "Ola Uduku"), states that "'those involved in developing Tropical Modernism were actually operating as agents of the colonies at the time'".[^90]

Architectural historians sometimes label Latin American modernism as " [tropical modernism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_Modernism "Tropical Modernism") ". This reflects architects who adapted modernism to the tropical climate as well as the sociopolitical contexts of Latin America.[^91]

Brazil became a showcase of modern architecture in the late 1930s through the work of [Lúcio Costa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%BAcio_Costa "Lúcio Costa") (1902–1998) and [Oscar Niemeyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscar_Niemeyer "Oscar Niemeyer") (1907–2012). Costa had the lead and Niemeyer collaborated on the Ministry of Education and Health in Rio de Janeiro (1936–43) and the Brazilian pavilion at the 1939 World's Fair in New York. Following the war, Niemeyer, along with Le Corbusier, conceived the form of the [United Nations Headquarters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Headquarters "United Nations Headquarters") constructed by Walter Harrison.

Lúcio Costa also had overall responsibility for the plan of the most audacious modernist project in Brazil; the creation of new capital, [Brasília](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bras%C3%ADlia "Brasília"), constructed between 1956 and 1961. Costa made the general plan, laid out in the form of a cross, with the major government buildings in the center. Niemeyer was responsible for designing the government buildings, including the palace of the President;the National Assembly, composed of two towers for the two branches of the legislature and two meeting halls, one with a cupola and other with an inverted cupola. Niemeyer also built the cathedral, eighteen ministries, and giant blocks of housing, each designed for three thousand residents, each with its own school, shops, and chapel. Modernism was employed both as an architectural principle and as a guideline for organizing society, as explored in *[The Modernist City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Modernist_City "The Modernist City").*[^92]

Following a military coup d'état in Brazil in 1964, Niemeyer moved to France, where he designed the modernist headquarters of the French Communist Party in Paris (1965–1980), a miniature of his United Nations plan.[^93]

Mexico also had a prominent modernist movement. Important figures included Félix Candela, born in Spain, who emigrated to Mexico in 1939; he specialized in concrete structures in unusual parabolic forms. Another important figure was [Mario Pani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mario_Pani "Mario Pani"), who designed the [National Conservatory of Music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservatorio_Nacional_de_M%C3%BAsica_\(Mexico\) "Conservatorio Nacional de Música (Mexico)") in Mexico City (1949), and the [Torre Insignia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torre_Insignia "Torre Insignia") (1988); Pani was also instrumental in the construction of the new [University of Mexico City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ciudad_Universitaria,_Mexico_City "Ciudad Universitaria, Mexico City") in the 1950s, alongside [Juan O'Gorman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_O%27Gorman "Juan O'Gorman"), [Eugenio Peschard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugenio_Peschard "Eugenio Peschard"), and [Enrique del Moral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enrique_del_Moral "Enrique del Moral"). The [Torre Latinoamericana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torre_Latinoamericana "Torre Latinoamericana"), designed by [Augusto H. Alvarez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augusto_H._Alvarez "Augusto H. Alvarez"), was one of the earliest modernist skyscrapers in Mexico City (1956); it successfully withstood the [1985 Mexico City earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1985_Mexico_City_earthquake "1985 Mexico City earthquake"), which destroyed many other buildings in the city center. [Pedro Ramirez Vasquez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedro_Ramirez_Vasquez "Pedro Ramirez Vasquez") and [Rafael Mijares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rafael_Mijares "Rafael Mijares") designed the Olympic Stadium for the 1968 Olympics, and Antoni Peyri and Candela designed the Palace of Sports. [Luis Barragan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luis_Barragan "Luis Barragan") was another influential figure in Mexican modernism; his raw concrete residence and studio in Mexico City looks like a blockhouse on the outside, while inside it features great simplicity in form, pure colors, abundant natural light, and, one of his signatures, a stairway without a railing. He won the [Pritzker Architecture Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pritzker_Architecture_Prize "Pritzker Architecture Prize") in 1980, and the house was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2004.[^94]

Japan, like Europe, had an enormous shortage of housing after the war, due to the bombing of many cities. 4.2 million housing units needed to be replaced. Japanese architects combined both traditional and modern styles and techniques. One of the foremost Japanese modernists was [Kunio Maekawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kunio_Maekawa "Kunio Maekawa") (1905–1986), who had worked for Le Corbusier in Paris until 1930. His own house in Tokyo was an early landmark of Japanese modernism, combining traditional style with ideas he acquired working with Le Corbusier. His notable buildings include concert halls in Tokyo and Kyoto and the International House of Japan in Tokyo, all in the pure modernist style.

[Kenzo Tange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenzo_Tange "Kenzo Tange") (1913–2005) worked in the studio of Kunio Maekawa from 1938 until 1945 before opening his own architectural firm. His first major commission was the [Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hiroshima_Peace_Memorial_Museum "Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum"). He designed many notable office buildings and cultural centers. office buildings, as well as the [Yoyogi National Gymnasium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoyogi_National_Gymnasium "Yoyogi National Gymnasium") for the [1964 Summer Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1964_Summer_Olympics "1964 Summer Olympics") in Tokyo. The gymnasium, built of concrete, features a roof suspended over the stadium on steel cables.

The Danish architect [Jørn Utzon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%B8rn_Utzon "Jørn Utzon") (1918–2008) worked briefly with [Alvar Aalto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alvar_Aalto "Alvar Aalto"), studied the work of Le Corbusier, and traveled to the United States to meet [Frank Lloyd Wright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright "Frank Lloyd Wright"). In 1957 he designed one of the most recognizable modernist buildings in the world; the [Sydney Opera House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sydney_Opera_House "Sydney Opera House"). He is known for the sculptural qualities of his buildings, and their relationship with the landscape. The five concrete shells of the structure resemble seashells by the beach. Begun in 1957, the project encountered considerable technical difficulties making the shells and getting the acoustics right. Utzon resigned in 1966, and the opera house was not finished until 1973, ten years after its scheduled completion.[^95]

In Iran, [Dariush Borbor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dariush_Borbor "Dariush Borbor") introduced a unique combination of modern architecture in unison with the local vernacular, manifested in the [Arjomand House](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arjomand_House&action=edit&redlink=1 "Arjomand House (page does not exist)") (1960) in [Kerman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerman "Kerman") and the [Nahavandi House](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nahavandi_House&action=edit&redlink=1 "Nahavandi House (page does not exist)") (1965) in [Tehran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tehran "Tehran").[^96]

In India, modernist architecture was promoted by the postcolonial state under Prime Minister [Jawaharlal Nehru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru "Jawaharlal Nehru"), most notably by inviting Le Corbusier to design the city of [Chandigarh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandigarh "Chandigarh").[^88] Although Nehru advocated for young Indians to be part of Le Corbuiser's design team in order to refine their skills whilst building their city, the team included only one female Indian architect, [Eulie Chowdhury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eulie_Chowdhury "Eulie Chowdhury").[^88]

Post independence architecture in Pakistan is a blend of Islamic and modern styles of architecture with influences from Mughal, indo-Islamic and international architectural designs. The 1960s and 1970s was a period of architectural Significance. [Jinnah's Mausoleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mazar-e-Quaid "Mazar-e-Quaid"), [Minar e Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minar-e-Pakistan "Minar-e-Pakistan"), [Bab e Khyber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bab-e-Khyber "Bab-e-Khyber"), [Islamic summit minar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summit_Minar,_Lahore "Summit Minar, Lahore") and the [Faisal mosque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faisal_Mosque "Faisal Mosque") date from this time.

## Preservation

Several works or collections of modern architecture have been designated by [UNESCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO "UNESCO") as [World Heritage Sites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites "World Heritage Sites"). In addition to the early experiments associated with Art Nouveau, these include a number of the structures mentioned above in this article: the [Rietveld Schröder House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rietveld_Schr%C3%B6der_House "Rietveld Schröder House") in Utrecht, the [Bauhaus structures in Weimar, Dessau, and Bernau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bauhaus_World_Heritage_Site "Bauhaus World Heritage Site"), the [Berlin Modernism Housing Estates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Modernism_Housing_Estates "Berlin Modernism Housing Estates"), the [White City of Tel Aviv](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_City_\(Tel_Aviv\) "White City (Tel Aviv)"), the city of [Asmara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asmara "Asmara"), the city of [Brasília](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bras%C3%ADlia "Brasília"), the [Ciudad Universitaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_City_of_Mexico "University City of Mexico") of [UNAM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNAM "UNAM") in Mexico City and the [University City of Caracas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_City_of_Caracas "University City of Caracas") in Venezuela, the [Sydney Opera House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sydney_Opera_House "Sydney Opera House"), and the [Centennial Hall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hala_Stulecia_\(Wroc%C5%82aw\) "Hala Stulecia (Wrocław)") in Wrocław, along with select works from [Le Corbursier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Architectural_Work_of_Le_Corbusier "The Architectural Work of Le Corbusier") and [Frank Lloyd Wright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_20th-Century_Architecture_of_Frank_Lloyd_Wright "The 20th-Century Architecture of Frank Lloyd Wright").

Private organizations such as [Docomomo International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Docomomo_International "Docomomo International"), the [World Monuments Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Monuments_Fund "World Monuments Fund"), and the Recent Past Preservation Network are working to safeguard and document imperiled Modern architecture. In 2006, the World Monuments Fund launched [*Modernism at Risk*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Monuments_Fund#Modernism_at_Risk "World Monuments Fund"), an advocacy and conservation program. The organization [MAMMA.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MAMMA. "MAMMA.") is working to document and preserve modernist architecture in Morocco.[^99]
