The **Pritzker Architecture Prize** is an international award presented annually "to honor a living [architect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architect "Architect") or architects whose built work demonstrates a combination of those qualities of talent, vision and commitment which has produced consistent and significant contributions to humanity and the built environment through the art of architecture". Founded in 1979 by [Jay A. Pritzker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jay_Pritzker "Jay Pritzker") and his wife [Cindy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cindy_Pritzker "Cindy Pritzker"), the award is funded by the [Pritzker family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pritzker_family "Pritzker family") and sponsored by the [Hyatt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyatt "Hyatt") Foundation. It is considered to be one of the world's premier architecture prizes, and is often [referred to as the Nobel Prize of](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_prizes_known_as_the_Nobel_or_the_highest_honors_of_a_field "List of prizes known as the Nobel or the highest honors of a field") architecture.[^1] [^2] [^3] [^4] [^5]

## Criteria and proceedings

The Pritzker Architecture Prize Jury says it is awarded "irrespective of nationality, race, creed, or ideology".[^6] The recipients receive US$100,000, a citation certificate, and, since 1987, a bronze medallion.[^1] The designs on the medal are inspired by the work of architect [Louis Sullivan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Sullivan "Louis Sullivan"), while the Latin inspired inscription on the reverse of the medallion— *firmitas, utilitas, venustas* (English: [firmness, commodity and delight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Firmness,_commodity_and_delight "Firmness, commodity and delight"))—is from [Ancient Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Roman "Ancient Roman") architect [Vitruvius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitruvius "Vitruvius"). Before 1987, a limited edition [Henry Moore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Moore "Henry Moore") sculpture accompanied the monetary prize.[^1]

The executive director of the prize, Manuela Lucá-Dazio,[^7] solicits nominations from a range of people, including past Laureates, academics, critics and others "with expertise and interest in the field of architecture".[^6] Any licensed architect can also make a personal application for the prize before November 1 every year. (In 1988 [Gordon Bunshaft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gordon_Bunshaft "Gordon Bunshaft") nominated himself for the award and eventually won it.) [^8] The jury, consisting of five to nine "experts... recognized professionals in their own fields of architecture, business, education, publishing, and culture", deliberates and early in the following year announce the winner.[^6] The prize chair is the 2016 Pritzker laureate [Alejandro Aravena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alejandro_Aravena "Alejandro Aravena"); earlier chairs were [J. Carter Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._Carter_Brown "J. Carter Brown") (1979–2002), the [Lord Rothschild](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacob_Rothschild,_4th_Baron_Rothschild "Jacob Rothschild, 4th Baron Rothschild") (2003–2004), the [Lord Palumbo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Palumbo,_Baron_Palumbo "Peter Palumbo, Baron Palumbo") (2005–2015), [Glenn Murcutt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glenn_Murcutt "Glenn Murcutt") (2016–2018) and [Stephen Breyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Breyer "Stephen Breyer") (2019–2020).[^9]

## Laureates

Inaugural winner [Philip Johnson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Johnson "Philip Johnson") was cited "for 50 years of imagination and vitality embodied in a myriad of museums, theaters, libraries, houses, gardens and corporate structures".[^10] The 2004 laureate [Zaha Hadid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zaha_Hadid "Zaha Hadid") was the first female prize winner.[^11] [Ryue Nishizawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ryue_Nishizawa "Ryue Nishizawa") became the youngest winner in 2010 at age 44.[^12] Partners in architecture (in 2001, [Jacques Herzog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Herzog "Jacques Herzog") and [Pierre de Meuron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Meuron "Pierre de Meuron"), in 2010, [Kazuyo Sejima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazuyo_Sejima "Kazuyo Sejima") and [Ryue Nishizawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ryue_Nishizawa "Ryue Nishizawa"), in 2020, [Yvonne Farrell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvonne_Farrell "Yvonne Farrell") and [Shelley McNamara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shelley_McNamara "Shelley McNamara"), and in 2021, [Anne Lacaton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anne_Lacaton "Anne Lacaton") and [Jean-Philippe Vassal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Philippe_Vassal "Jean-Philippe Vassal")) have shared the award.[^13] In 1988, [Gordon Bunshaft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gordon_Bunshaft "Gordon Bunshaft") and [Oscar Niemeyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscar_Niemeyer "Oscar Niemeyer") were both separately honored with the award.[^14] The 2017 winners, architects [Rafael Aranda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rafael_Aranda "Rafael Aranda"), [Carme Pigem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carme_Pigem "Carme Pigem"), and [Ramón Vilalta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_Vilalta "Ramón Vilalta") were the first group of three to share the prize.[^15] [^16]

## Criticism

In 2013, the student organization "Women in Design" at the [Harvard Graduate School of Design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_Graduate_School_of_Design "Harvard Graduate School of Design") started a petition arguing [Denise Scott Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denise_Scott_Brown "Denise Scott Brown") should receive joint recognition with her partner, [Robert Venturi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Venturi "Robert Venturi"), who won the award in 1991.[^70] The petition, according to *[The New York Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_York_Times "The New York Times")*, "reignited long-simmering tensions in the architectural world over whether women have been consistently denied the standing they deserve in a field whose most prestigious award was not given to a woman until 2004, when [Zaha Hadid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zaha_Hadid "Zaha Hadid") won".[^71] Scott Brown told [CNN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNN "CNN") that "as a woman, she had felt excluded by the elite of architecture throughout her career," and that "the Pritzker Prize was based on the fallacy that great architecture was the work of a 'single lone male genius' at the expense of collaborative work." [^72] Responding to the petition, the 2013 prize jury said that it cannot revisit the decisions of past juries, either in the case of Scott Brown or that of [Lu Wenyu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lu_Wenyu "Lu Wenyu"), whose husband [Wang Shu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Shu "Wang Shu") won in 2012.[^73] The 2020 Pritzker jury said in its citation awarding the prize to [Yvonne Farrell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvonne_Farrell "Yvonne Farrell") and [Shelley McNamara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shelley_McNamara "Shelley McNamara") – making them the fourth and fifth women to ever be awarded the prize – that they were, "pioneers in a field that has traditionally been and still is a male-dominated profession \[and\] beacons to others as they forge their exemplary professional path." [^74]

In 2026, weeks before the year's Laureate was announced, [Thomas Pritzker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Pritzker "Thomas Pritzker"), the director of the Hyatt foundation and son of [Jay A. Pritzker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jay_A._Pritzker "Jay A. Pritzker") resigned from his role as the Hyatt Hotels Corporation executive chairman following the release of the [Epstein files](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epstein_files "Epstein files") which contained communications between himself and [Jeffrey Epstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeffrey_Epstein "Jeffrey Epstein"), saying he "exercised terrible judgment in maintaining contact with" Epstein. However, Pritzker remains the director and vice president of the Hyatt foundation, and a Hyatt Hotels Corporation executive. [^75]
