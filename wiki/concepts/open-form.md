## Definition
An architectural and spatial theory developed primarily by Oskar Hansen. It postulates that architecture is not a closed, monumental, finished work of art (Closed Form), but rather a flexible "background" or "passé-partout" for the events of daily human life. The space is meant to be completed and shaped by its users.

**PL:** *Teoria architektoniczna i przestrzenna opracowana głównie przez Oskara Hansena. Zakłada, że architektura nie jest zamkniętym, monumentalnym, gotowym dziełem sztuki (Formą Zamkniętą), lecz elastycznym "tłem" (passé-partout) dla zdarzeń codziennego życia ludzi. Przestrzeń ma być dopełniana i kształtowana przez jej użytkowników.*

## Historical Context
Formulated in the late 1950s and presented by Hansen at the CIAM congress in Otterlo (1959). It became a highly influential approach in Polish architectural education (especially at the Warsaw Academy of Fine Arts) and informed large-scale housing estates, though it often collided with the rigid, top-down realities of the communist centrally planned economy.

## Key Proponents
[[Oskar Hansen]], [[Zofia Hansen]]

## Key Examples
- [[Osiedle Słowackiego (Lublin)]] – widely considered the most complete built realization of the Open Form concepts.
- [[Teatr Formy Otwartej]] – an amphitheater inside Osiedle Słowackiego designed explicitly as an unprogrammed, sculptural background for community activities.
- Przyczółek Grochowski (Warsaw)

## Critiques & Alternatives
While deeply humanist in theory, the Open Form was often compromised in execution by poor building materials and the lack of actual agency given to tenants under state socialism. In the 21st century, these open spaces are vulnerable to over-regulation, where simple things like non-standard stairs or lack of railings (essential to the open sculptural form) are deemed illegal safety hazards.
