## Definition

Earth-sheltered housing is a building type in which the structure is embedded in or covered by earth, using the soil as thermal mass, weatherproofing, and structural envelope. In low-rise, high-density contexts, earth-sheltered dwellings are typically organised around sunken courtyards that provide light, air, and access to subterranean rooms. The most extensive historic examples are the yaodong (cave dwellings) of the Chinese loess plateaux, where hundreds of thousands of people still live in homes dug into hillsides and grouped around underground courtyards.

W kontekście niskopiętrowej zabudowy wysokogęstościowej mieszkania ziemne są zazwyczaj zorganizowane wokół zanurzonych dziedzińców dostarczających światła, powietrza i dostępu do podziemnych pomieszczeń. ## Historical Context

Earth-sheltered construction is as old as human habitation — the earliest caves were natural earth shelters. The innovation of the Chinese yaodong was to create artificial caves in the soft, stable loess soil, carving rectangular rooms 6–8 metres below ground and grouping them around sunken courtyards. This technique, developed over millennia, produces dwellings with remarkable thermal stability: the thick earth layer keeps interiors cool in summer and warm in winter, reducing heating and cooling energy to near zero. The courtyards serve as light wells, ventilation shafts, and social spaces; the roofs are used for drying crops and as additional outdoor area. Earth-sheltered housing has seen modern revivals in the 1970s energy crisis (Malcolm Wells' "Gentle Architecture") and in contemporary sustainable design, where it is valued for its minimal environmental footprint.

**PL:** Konstrukcja ziemna jest tak stara jak ludzkie osadnictwo — najwcześniejsze jaskinie były naturalnymi schronieniami ziemnymi. ## Key Proponents

- **Malcolm Wells** (American architect, 1926–2009) — Advocate of underground and earth-covered architecture in the 1970s–1990s
- **Chinese vernacular builders** — Millennia of anonymous yaodong construction

## Key Examples

- **Henan Underground Dwellings** (China) — Yaodong caves around sunken courtyards [[Henan Underground Dwellings]]
- **Mesa Verde** (USA) — Ancestral Puebloan cliff dwellings, natural earth-shelter
- **Matmata** (Tunisia) — Troglodyte houses dug into sandstone pits
- **Coober Pedy** (Australia) — Opal-mining town with modern dugout homes

## Critiques & Alternatives

Earth-sheltered housing faces challenges of waterproofing, drainage, and psychological resistance (the "cave" stigma). In dense urban contexts, excavation costs and soil contamination often prohibit new underground construction. Alternatives include bermed earth-sheltering (earth pushed against above-ground walls), green roofs (vegetation on conventional structures), and earth-bermed courtyard houses that combine sunken courts with conventional construction. The yaodong remains unmatched for its zero-energy performance and minimal material use.
