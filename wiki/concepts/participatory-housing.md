## Definition

Participatory housing is an approach to residential production in which future residents are co-authors of the design, planning, and often construction of their own homes, rather than passive recipients of housing delivered by a developer or the state. The defining principle is that communities have the right to shape their own built environment — not merely to be consulted about it — and that this involvement produces both higher-quality outcomes and stronger social cohesion than top-down provision. Participatory housing typically involves resident cooperatives, architect-facilitated design processes, and some form of community self-organisation as a prerequisite for access.

A key related concept is **Incremental Housing** (*budownictwo oparteo na stopniowym wzroście*): the strategy of designing housing for future expansion and modification by residents, so that initial construction is affordable and the building grows with the family's needs and resources. Incremental Housing reduces capital costs, respects residents' autonomy, and acknowledges that housing is a process rather than a product.

## Historical Context

Participatory housing has roots in 1960s–70s debates about the "right to the city" (Lefebvre, 1968) and the critique of functionalist mass housing estates that destroyed existing social networks while providing shelter. Key intellectual precedents include:

- **Aldo van Eyck's Otterlo Circles** (1959, last CIAM congress): the "twin phenomena" of architecture built *by us* (institutional/professional) and *for us* (serving genuine social needs) — a critique of modernist housing that excluded communities from its own production.
- **John Turner's housing advocacy** (Peru, 1960s–70s): observation that self-built *pueblos jóvenes* in Lima produced more socially coherent environments than state housing estates, because residents had agency over form and process.
- **German IBA Berlin** (1984–87): international building exhibition in which participatory housing methodologies developed in SAAL were imported; included Álvaro Siza's Bonjour Tristesse housing block.

The most significant single implementation was **SAAL** (*Serviço de Apoio Ambulatório Local*), Portugal, 1974–1976, conceived by Nuno Portas following the Carnation Revolution. SAAL operationalised participatory housing at national scale: six principles (community self-organisation, preservation of social-spatial ties, reduced bureaucracy, resident co-financing, decentralisation, Incremental Housing strategy); three regional delegatures; Brigadas de Construção (field teams of leading architects); 170 projects designed in 26 months.

## Key Examples

- **SAAL Bouça, Porto** (1975–2006): Álvaro Siza; 131 duplex row-house units; ilha typology adapted to modernist formal language; influenced by Alvar Aalto (Sunila) and Bruno Taut (Britz); redefines public/private boundary through articulated threshold spaces; completed in full 2006 after 30-year interruption.
- **SAAL São Victor, Porto** (1974–1975): Álvaro Siza; 102 row-house units; building A1 retains ruins of original ilha as historical counterpoint; most politically committed SAAL project; never completed due to programme's termination.
- **Quinta da Malagueira, Évora** (1977–): Álvaro Siza; 1,200 low-rise row houses on semi-rural land; infrastructure (services duct) as organisational armature; incremental growth over decades.
- **IBA Berlin, Kreuzberg** (1984–87): international building exhibition incorporating SAAL methodology; Álvaro Siza's Bonjour Tristesse (1984) and other participatory projects; imported Portuguese approach to West Berlin social housing.

## Critiques & Alternatives

- **Self-help as exploitation**: SAAL Lisbon residents protested in February 1975 against the self-help requirement, calling it "double exploitation" — workers expected to build their own homes after a full working day. SAAL Lisboa e Centro-Sul abandoned self-help in May 1975.
- **Political instrumentalisation**: SAAL was created by a provisional government as a response to post-revolutionary political pressure; its stated neutrality was a strategic fiction. Participatory housing programmes are inherently political.
- **Scale and duration**: SAAL operated for only 26 months and served a fraction of the projected need (375 homes completed vs. 40,000 families targeted). Participatory processes are slow and expensive relative to conventional production; political windows close before completion.
- **Class capture**: Bouça's 2006 completion reprogrammed the project for the middle class (underground parking, redesigned units). As participatory housing ages and market conditions change, its social targeting tends to drift upward.
- **Architect's autonomy vs. residents' demands**: Álvaro Siza explicitly refused to become "the hand of the people" — he offered skilled design proposals for communal deliberation rather than executing residents' requests literally. This is the productive tension at the heart of participatory housing.
