## Definition
The architectural and regulatory practice of dividing public toilet facilities into two categories — male and female — typically through separate rooms, signage, and fixtures. This binary partition is increasingly critiqued as a spatial enforcement of heteronormative gender ideology that fails to accommodate non-binary, transgender, and intersex individuals, while also ignoring the full range of social, care, and bodily activities (feeding, changing, resting, intimate communication) that actually occur in these spaces.

**PL:** *Architektoniczna i regulacyjna praktyka dzielenia publicznych toalet na dwie kategorie — męskie i damskie — zazwyczaj poprzez oddzielne pomieszczenia, oznaczenia i armaturę. Ten binarny podział jest coraz częściej krytykowany jako przestrzenne narzucanie heteronormatywnej ideologii płciowej, które nie uwzględnia osób niebinarnych, transpłciowych i interpłciowych, a jednocześnie ignoruje pełen zakres aktywności społecznych, opiekuńczych i cielesnych (karmienie, przewijanie, odpoczynek, intymna komunikacja) faktycznie odbywających się w tych przestrzeniach.*

## Historical Context
Gender-separated public toilets emerged in the 19th century alongside the "separate spheres" ideology, initially as a way to protect "respectable" women from male public space. The design quickly became a global default, encoded in building codes and plumbing regulations. Contemporary critiques note that the binary model assumes a universal user (cisgender, able-bodied, without childcare needs) and creates safety risks for transgender and gender-nonconforming people.

## Key Proponents
Critiqued by [[Noviki]]; design alternatives advocated by queer-feminist urbanists and disability-rights campaigners.

## Key Examples
- **Stabilna dyfuzja toalet publicznych** (Noviki, 2023) — AI-generated visual manifesto against binary WC design.
- **All-gender restroom retrofits** — increasingly common in universities, museums, and transport hubs in North America and Northern Europe, typically replacing binary rooms with fully enclosed cubicles and shared hand-washing areas.

## Critiques & Alternatives
- **Safety**: Opponents of de-gendering often claim that mixed toilets endanger women; proponents respond that fully enclosed cubicles and better surveillance of common areas increase safety for all.
- **Care work**: Standard male toilets rarely include baby-changing stations, distributing the burden of childcare unevenly. Non-gendered designs can centralise care infrastructure.
- **Transgender exclusion**: Binary signage forces trans and non-binary people into unsafe choices; inclusive design removes this coercion.
