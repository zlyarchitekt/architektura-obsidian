## Definition
Housing designed to minimise private dwelling space and maximise shared facilities — kitchens, dining halls, laundries, nurseries, recreation rooms — on the premise that collective provision of domestic services can free individuals (especially women) from unpaid reproductive labour and foster new forms of social solidarity.

**PL:** *Mieszkalnictwo zaprojektowane tak, by minimalizować prywatną przestrzeń mieszkaniową i maksymalizować zaplecze wspólne — kuchnie, stołówki, pralnie, żłobki, sale rekreacyjne — na założeniu, że kolektywna organizacja usług domowych może uwolnić jednostki (szczególnie kobiety) od nieodpłatnej pracy reprodukcyjnej i sprzyjać nowym formom solidarności społecznej.*

## Historical Context
The idea flourished in early Soviet Constructivism (Narkomfin, 1930) and interwar European modernism, often linked to feminist demands for the socialisation of housework. Lenin explicitly stated that socialism was impossible without liberating women from domestic slavery. Most experiments were reversed under Stalin or by post-war welfare states that privatised domesticity into the nuclear family flat. Co-living spaces today represent a voluntary, market-driven revival of some collective principles, but without the political ambition to abolish private property or gendered labour.

## Key Proponents
[[Moisei Ginzburg]], [[Ignaty Milinis]], Ernst May, Hannes Meyer

## Key Examples
- **Narkomfin, Moscow** (1930) — communal canteen, no private kitchens; reversed under Stalin.
- **Co-living spaces** (21st c.) — shared kitchens and lounges, but private bedrooms; usually market-rate and individual-choice driven.

## Critiques & Alternatives
- **Elite capture**: Narkomfin served bureaucrats, not workers.
- **Authoritarian reversal**: Collective housing is vulnerable to political backlash; spatial radicalism requires political protection.
- **Commodification**: Today's co-living often substitutes branding for genuine social infrastructure.
