## Definition

Housing crisis refers to a condition in which access to adequate affordable housing is structurally and systematically denied to a significant portion of the urban population — not as a temporary market fluctuation but as a persistent structural feature of the urban economy. Its proximate cause is supply insufficiency in growing cities, but contemporary urban housing crises are driven primarily by the [[Financialization of Housing]]: the transformation of residential real estate into a financial asset, which structurally aligns landlord incentives with rent maximization and capital appreciation rather than housing provision.

**PL:** Kryzys mieszkaniowy to stan, w którym dostęp do przystępnego cenowo mieszkania jest strukturalnie i systematycznie odmawiany znacznej części miejskiej populacji. Jego bezpośrednią przyczyną jest niedobór podaży, ale współczesne kryzysy napędza przede wszystkim finansjalizacja: przekształcenie nieruchomości mieszkalnych w aktywa finansowe, strukturalnie ukierunkowując landlordów na maksymalizację czynszu.

## Historical Context

Berlin's housing crisis is documented in this wiki primarily through [[Uspołeczniamy Berlin]] (Kusiak, 2022). The structural sequence:

1. **1990s–2000s privatization:** Berlin sold its public housing stock cheaply after reunification, creating the asset base for corporate accumulation. The city gave away its structural "political leverage" over housing at below-market prices.
2. **Corporate landlord model:** Large stock-listed companies (Vonovia, Deutsche Wohnen) replaced traditional private landlords; shareholder primacy structurally drives rent maximization regardless of local market conditions or social need.
3. **Supply failure:** Even in periods of intense private development, private developers built approximately 7,000 apartments per year post-1989 — less than a quarter of the rate achieved under public control in the 1920s (see below).
4. **Regulatory failure:** Mietbremse (rent brake) and Mietendeckel (rent cap) provided partial relief but were either circumvented (Mietbremse — corporate legal departments found loopholes immediately) or struck down (Mietendeckel — Federal Constitutional Court, March 2021, on competence grounds).

Key figure: Berlin rents rose **44% between 2015 and 2020** in a city where **85% of residents rent**.

[[Joanna Kusiak]] identifies the housing crisis as global — "a worldwide phenomenon affecting London, São Paulo, and Berlin alike, though taking a slightly different form in each place." In Warsaw, the local variant is reprywatyzacja: legal mechanisms used to transfer urban buildings from communities to private reprywatyzacja businessmen (the subject of Kusiak's doctoral dissertation *Chaos Warszawa*).

**PL:** Berliński kryzys mieszkaniowy: (1) prywatyzacja lat 90. po zjednoczeniu; (2) korporacyjny landlordyzm; (3) zawodność podaży prywatnej (7 tys. mieszkań/rok vs. 30 tys. lat 20.); (4) zawodność regulacji (Mietbremse obchodzone; Mietendeckel obalony przez FTK). Czynsze +44% (2015–2020) w mieście, gdzie 85% wynajmuje. Globalny wymiar: Londyn, São Paulo, Berlin — ta sama struktura, różne oblicza. Warszawa: reprywatyzacja jako lokalna odmiana.

## Public Control vs. Free Market: Production Data

A key empirical observation from the source, drawing on research by **Andrej Holm**: Berlin's housing production peaked in the **1920s** — the decade of *Rotes Berlin* and large-scale cooperative and municipal housing construction — not in periods of free-market dominance. Post-1989 private development produced approximately **7,000 new apartments per year**; cooperative and municipal construction in the 1920s produced approximately **30,000 per year**.

The explanation is structural: private developers are (1) obligated to seek capital returns on investment, (2) typically do not own the land and must purchase it at market prices, (3) operate on 5–10 year investment cycle timeframes. Public developers are (1) not seeking capital returns, (2) typically own the land already, (3) operate on decade-long timeframes. The result is that private housing production is intrinsically risk-averse and lower-volume than public housing production when public capacity is strong. The supply-side argument for market housing — that removing regulation and building more will solve the crisis — is empirically contradicted by the Berlin production record.

**PL:** Kluczowa obserwacja empiryczna z badań Andreja Holma: berlińska produkcja mieszkań szczytowała w **latach 20. XX w.** (~30 tys./rok pod demokratyczną kontrolą publiczną), nie w dekadach wolnorynkowych (~7 tys./rok po 1989). Wyjaśnienie strukturalne: prywatni deweloperzy muszą kalkulować zwrot z kapitału, kupują ziemię po cenach rynkowych, myślą w cyklu 5–10-letnim. Publiczni deweloperzy: bez presji zwrotu, zazwyczaj właściciele ziemi, myślą w dekadach. Dane obalają argument podażowy za deregulacją.

## Structural Responses: A Taxonomy

The source ([[Uspołeczniamy Berlin]]) documents three categories of response, distinguished by their structural depth:

**1. Regulation (symptom management):**
- Mietbremse (rent brake): circumvented by corporate legal teams
- Mietendeckel (rent cap, 2020): struck down by FCC March 2021 as exceeding Senate competence
- Assessment: insufficient; can always be circumvented or overruled; does not address ownership structure

**2. Market purchase (structural but expensive):**
- Buying controlling stakes in corporate landlords
- Assessment: costly; would reward speculation; the apartments were sold below market value and buying them back at market prices is, as Kusiak notes, "not an art"

**3. Vergesellschaftung (structural remedy):**
- [[Socialization (Vergesellschaftung)]]: Article 15 GG; removes assets from speculative ownership permanently; democratic management; self-financed through rental income bonds
- Assessment: constitutionally the most robust option; permanent structural effect; precedent of global significance

**PL:** Trzy kategorie odpowiedzi: (1) *Regulacja* (zarządzanie symptomami) — Mietbremse (obchodzone), Mietendeckel (obalony); niewystarczające. (2) *Wykup rynkowy* (strukturalny, ale drogi) — nagradza spekulację. (3) *Vergesellschaftung* (lekarstwo strukturalne) — art. 15 GG; trwałe wyjęcie z obwodu spekulacyjnego; samofinansujące się; precedens o globalnym znaczeniu.

## Vienna as Prevention

The [[Vienna Housing Model]] represents a structural alternative that never required remediation: a city whose public land ownership was never substantially privatized maintains the capacity to govern housing development without perpetual crisis management. Vienna's lesson is that housing crisis prevention is structurally simpler than housing crisis remediation: retaining public land ownership across successive governments is the baseline condition for everything else.

**PL:** Model wiedeński jako prewencja zamiast sanacji: miasto, które nie wyprzedało ziemi, zachowuje zdolność zarządzania mieszkalnictwem bez permanentnego zarządzania kryzysami. Wiedeńska lekcja: prewencja kryzysu jest strukturalnie prostsza niż sanacja.
