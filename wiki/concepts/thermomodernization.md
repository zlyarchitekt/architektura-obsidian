## Definition
The process of upgrading an existing building to reduce heat loss and lower energy consumption, most commonly by adding an exterior layer of thermal insulation (like EPS/styrofoam or mineral wool) and replacing windows. 

**PL:** *Proces modernizacji istniejącego budynku w celu zmniejszenia strat ciepła i obniżenia zużycia energii, najczęściej polegający na dodaniu zewnętrznej warstwy izolacji termicznej (styropian lub wełna mineralna) oraz wymianie okien.*

## Historical Context
A mass phenomenon in post-communist countries since the 1990s, aimed at fixing the disastrous energy inefficiency of communist-era prefabricated housing and public buildings. While ecologically and economically necessary, "termomodernizacja" became notorious for "pasteloza" (painting housing blocks in garish pastel colors) and the erasure of exterior architectural features.

## Key Proponents / Critics
- Strongly criticized by architectural historians and preservationists for inducing a "swollen facade" (*opuchlizna*) effect. Post-war modernism often relies on subtle depth contrasts, window setbacks (reveals), and tectonic rhythms which are instantly eradicated when 15-20cm of insulation is glued flat across the entire face of a building.

## Key Examples
- [[Biblioteka UMCS]] – standard insulation thickness ruined window proportions until experts intervened to restore the window-to-wall ratio and original two-tone grey plaster finishes.
- [[Osiedle Słowackiego (Lublin)]] – required the innovative use of varying insulation materials with different conductivity coefficients (λ) to maintain the varied depth of the facade grid without suffocating the balconies.

## Critiques & Alternatives
Best practice requires matching the original plaster textures, maintaining window setbacks by pulling new window frames outward (or insulating reveals very carefully), and using high-efficiency slim insulation panels (like aerogels or resol foams, which are much more expensive) instead of standard thick EPS.
