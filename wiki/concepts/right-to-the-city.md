## Definition
A political and spatial concept originating with Henri Lefebvre (1968) asserting that urban inhabitants — not only property owners or state institutions — have a collective right to appropriate, use, and transform urban space according to their needs. In its contemporary usage, the right to the city encompasses access to housing, public space, infrastructure, and cultural representation. For marginalised groups (LGBTQ+ communities, migrants, the poor), it specifically means the right to be visible, to gather safely, and to imprint their histories and symbols onto the urban fabric.

## Historical Context
Lefebvre formulated the concept during the 1968 uprisings in France as a Marxist critique of capitalist urbanisation, which treats the city as a commodity rather than a shared oeuvre. The idea was revived in the 2000s by the World Social Forum and UNESCO, and has been adopted by social movements from the MST in Brazil to housing activists in Berlin and Warsaw. In Poland, the concept appears in discussions of participatory housing and queer urbanism, though it remains marginal in formal planning education.

## Key Proponents
Henri Lefebvre, David Harvey, urban social movements, [[Michał Kowalski]] (queer adaptation).

## Key Examples
- **Tęcza reclamation, Warsaw** — citizens decorating the burnt rainbow asserted their right to the surface of the city against homophobic arson.
- **Budżet Obywatełski (Warsaw)** — the 2023 citizen-budget proposal to rebuild Tęcza as a hologram is an institutionalised form of the right to the city.

## Critiques & Alternatives
- **Legal vagueness**: The right to the city is a moral-political claim rather than a justiciable legal right; without enforcement mechanisms it risks becoming rhetorical.
- **Gentrification paradox**: When marginalised communities successfully claim space, property values often rise, displacing the very groups who fought for it.
