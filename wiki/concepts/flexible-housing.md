## Definition
Housing design that refuses fixed functional assignments to rooms, instead providing open structures (generous spans, utility access points, neutral surfaces) that allow inhabitants to reconfigure spaces over time according to changing needs, family structures, or cultural preferences. Flexibility can be achieved through technology (movable kitchens, plug-in services), structural strategy (column grids rather than load-bearing walls), or simply the architect's refusal to name rooms.

**PL:** *Projektowanie mieszkaniowe odmawiające sztywnego przypisywania funkcji pomieszczeniom, zapewniające zamiast tego otwarte struktury (duże rozpiętości, punkty dostępu do instalacji, neutralne powierzchnie), które pozwalają mieszkańcom rekonfigurować przestrzeń w czasie zgodnie ze zmieniającymi się potrzebami, strukturami rodzinnymi lub preferencjami kulturowymi. Elastyczność można osiągnąć przez technologię (ruchome kuchnie, instalacje plug-in), strategię strukturalną (siatki słupów zamiast ścian nośnych) lub po prostu odmowę architekta nazywania pokoi.*

## Historical Context
Modernism's free plan (Le Corbusier's Five Points) was an early structural flexibility, but it was usually deployed to impose the architect's programme rather than liberate the user. True flexibility as a user-empowering strategy emerged in the late 20th century with critiques of the nuclear-family flat, and has been revived by architects like Tatiana Bilbao who argue that standard housing serves only 18% of society.

## Key Proponents
[[Tatiana Bilbao]], Alejandro Aravena, John Habraken

## Key Examples
- **Tatiana Bilbao's Monterrey housing** — attempted modular same-size rooms interweaving atypically; compromised to conventional layouts but retained unprogrammed shared spaces.
- **Reconstruir MX indigenous housing** — communities chose layouts; one family refused street-facing windows.
- **Traditional Mexican / Polish rural housing** — seasonal room-use shifts (sleeping in different rooms by season) as pre-modern flexibility.

## Critiques & Alternatives
- **Building codes**: Most jurisdictions mandate kitchens, bathrooms, and bedrooms, making legal flexibility difficult.
- **Developer economics**: Standard layouts are cheaper to build and sell. Flexibility requires either public subsidy or high-end budgets.
- **User burden**: Flexible space shifts design labour onto inhabitants, who may lack the time or skill to reconfigure their homes.
