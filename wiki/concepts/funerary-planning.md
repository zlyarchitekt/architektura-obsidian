## Definition

Funerary planning is the spatial planning of burial and mourning infrastructure — cemeteries, crematoria, funeral homes, and associated transport connections — as a public service with accessibility requirements equivalent to any other critical public infrastructure. The concept foregrounds the political and spatial dimensions of funerary provision: who has the right to bury their dead in their community, who can afford dignified burial, and who can visit graves without a private car. In Poland, funerary planning is governed primarily by the *ustawa o cmentarzach i chowaniu zmarłych* (1959 law on cemeteries and the burial of the dead), which mandates that new cemeteries be located at city margins, that brick graves never be removed, and that every municipality provide a communal (non-denominational) cemetery — a requirement met by only 32% of municipalities.

**PL:** Planowanie funeralne to planowanie przestrzenne infrastruktury pogrzebowej i żałobnej — cmentarzy, krematorów, domów pogrzebowych i powiązanych połączeń transportowych — jako usługi publicznej o wymogach dostępności równoważnych innej infrastrukturze krytycznej. W Polsce reguluje je przede wszystkim ustawa o cmentarzach i chowaniu zmarłych (1959), nakazująca lokowanie nowych cmentarzy na obrzeżach miast, zakazująca likwidacji grobów murowanych i zobowiązująca każdą gminę do posiadania cmentarza komunalnego — wymóg spełniany przez zaledwie 32% gmin.

## Historical Context

Until the late twentieth century, cemetery planning in Poland was dominated by the Catholic Church: 68% of Poland's 12,269 cemeteries are denominational, primarily Catholic. The 1959 communist-era law reflected sanitary concerns that are now technically outdated (requiring marginal location) and cultural assumptions (dominance of brick graves and permanence) that conflict with both space constraints and changing burial practices (cremation, secular burial). The east-west gradient in communal cemetery provision is a direct legacy of post-war forced migration: Western Poland inherited a dense network of former German evangelical cemeteries, which were converted to communal use after 1945; Eastern and Central Poland has very few communal cemeteries because no comparable legacy exists.

Secularization is rapidly creating new demand for communal, non-denominational burial: Sunday mass attendance fell from 41% (2010) to 28.3% (2021); children participating in school religion classes dropped from 93% to 54%; baptisms declined from 374,300 (2016) to 312,100 (2020). As the Catholic Church's cultural dominance weakens, the state's failure to provide adequate communal infrastructure becomes an urgent spatial justice issue.

**PL:** Do końca XX w. planowanie cmentarne w Polsce zdominował Kościół katolicki (68% cmentarzy wyznaniowych). Ustawa z 1959 r. odzwierciedlała przestarzałe względy sanitarne i kulturowe (dominacja grobów murowanych, lokalizacja marginalna). Gradient wschód–zachód to dziedzictwo migracji powojennych: zachodnia Polska przejęła gęstą sieć dawnych cmentarzy ewangelickich. Sekularyzacja gwałtownie zwiększa zapotrzebowanie na cmentarze komunalne.

## Key Examples

- **Cmentarz Komunalny w Białymstoku (Karakule)**: located in Supraśl commune, 7 km from Białystok's city centre (8.5 km by road); managed by Białystok city (illegal under the 1959 law); no public transit; exemplifies urban cemetery pushed beyond municipal boundaries.
- **Cmentarz Komunalny Południowy, Warsaw**: located in Piaseczno commune; managed by Warsaw city (same illegal cross-boundary pattern); two buses daily from city centre; cited in the essay's opening case.
- **Denmark**: cemeteries treated as public parks, open to cyclists, dog-walkers, sports users, picnics; integrated into city fabric; cited as model for secular, accessible funerary infrastructure.
- **Podlaskie voivodeship**: 17 communal cemeteries for 1.169 million residents = 68,901 residents per communal cemetery; 2.4% share of all cemeteries; 10% Orthodox minority (120,000 people) underserved; most cemeteries without any public transit access.

- Województwo podlaskie: 68 901 mieszkańców na cmentarz komunalny; 17 cmentarzy komunalnych; brak komunikacji do większości.

## Critiques & Alternatives

- **Gendered spatial exclusion**: cemetery visitors are predominantly elderly women (men die earlier; women outlive husbands by ~10 years; women have lower pensions and are less likely to own cars). Pushing cemeteries to city margins without public transit selectively excludes the population group with the highest need and lowest mobility.
- **Outdated legislation**: the 1959 law's requirement to locate new cemeteries at city margins is based on sanitary assumptions no longer valid by current standards. The prohibition on removing brick graves (even after 100+ years) causes space shortage and prevents space reuse. Both rules require reform.
- **Danish model**: cemeteries as integrated urban parks, open to everyday uses, managed as civic green infrastructure — challenges the Polish cultural norm of cemeteries as sacred, fenced, and separated from urban life.
- **Legal non-compliance**: 68% of Polish municipalities lack a communal cemetery despite a legal obligation. No enforcement mechanism exists. The NIK (2016) audit flagged the cross-boundary management practice as illegal but no corrective action followed.
- **Secularization gap**: as Poland rapidly secularizes, the gap between growing demand for non-denominational burial and the stagnant supply of communal cemeteries will widen.

**PL:** Płciowe wykluczenie przestrzenne: starsze kobiety (główne odwiedzające) są najbardziej wykluczone transportowo. Przestarzałe prawo: wymóg lokalizacji marginalnej i zakaz likwidacji grobów murowanych wymagają reformy. Model duński: cmentarze jako parki. Niestosowanie prawa: 68% gmin bez komunalnego. Luka sekularyzacyjna: rosnący popyt na pochówek niereligijny vs. stagnacja podaży komunalnej.
