## Definition

Financialization of housing is the process by which residential property ceases to function primarily as a place to live and is restructured as a financial asset — a vehicle for yield generation, balance-sheet appreciation, and shareholder return. In financialized housing markets, the primary "client" of a landlord is not the tenant but the shareholder; rent is not compensation for providing habitation but a revenue stream whose maximization is structurally obligatory. This is structurally distinct from traditional private landlordism ("Pan Müller with two houses"), which operates within the local economy and responds to local market conditions. Financialized housing routes rents through tax havens, pays dividends to global investors, and is insulated from local political pressure by corporate legal structure.

**PL:** Finansjalizacja mieszkalnictwa to proces, w którym nieruchomości mieszkalne przestają pełnić przede wszystkim funkcję miejsca zamieszkania i są restrukturyzowane jako aktywa finansowe — instrumenty generowania zysku i zwrotu dla akcjonariuszy. Klientem pierwotnym landlorda jest akcjonariusz, nie lokator; czynsz nie jest wynagrodzeniem za dostarczanie lokum, lecz strumieniem przychodów, którego maksymalizacja jest strukturalnie obligatoryjna.

## Historical Context

The corporate landlord model emerged at scale in Berlin over the 2010s, driven by the post-reunification privatization of Berlin's public housing stock — buildings sold cheaply in the 1990s and 2000s, creating the asset base for corporate accumulation. By 2020–22, commercial landlords with more than 3,000 apartments each held an estimated 250,000 apartments in Berlin alone; Deutsche Wohnen, Vonovia, and several others were stock-market listed, with shareholders including large global capital managers (BlackRock, Blackstone).

Berlin rents rose **44% between 2015 and 2020**. In a city where 85% of residents rent, this is a direct transfer of income from tenants (local economic actors) to shareholders (global capital, tax havens). The macroeconomic consequence: rent money extracted from Berlin's local economy does not return as local spending or local taxation; it exits the city and the country. The city loses doubly: on foregone taxes and on direct social rent subsidies (*Wohngeld*) that flow from public budgets to corporate landlords when tenants cannot cover market rents.

A further dimension is opacity: investigative journalism by Correctiv revealed that hundreds of apparently independent small landlords in Berlin are in fact subsidiaries of a single corporation (Pears Global) registered in Luxembourg — hundreds of different names on a single letterbox. The true scale of corporate housing ownership is structurally difficult to audit.

[[Saskia Sassen]]'s *Expulsions* (2014) provides the theoretical framework at global scale: sophisticated financial instruments construct a self-perpetuating sector of global land and housing speculation, generating "expelled" subjects — populations priced out of or removed from space — as a structural feature rather than an accidental side-effect.

**PL:** Berliński model korporacyjnego landlordyzmu rozwinął się na bazie prywatyzacji zasobów mieszkaniowych po zjednoczeniu. Czynsze +44% (2015–2020) w mieście, gdzie 85% wynajmuje. Konsekwencja makroekonomiczna: pieniądze wyciekają z lokalnej gospodarki do rajów podatkowych i globalnych akcjonariuszy. Miasto traci podwójnie: na podatkach i na subwencjach czynszowych (*Wohngeld*) przepływających z publicznego budżetu do korporacyjnych landlordów. Ramy teoretyczne: [[Saskia Sassen]], *Expulsions* (2014) — finansjalizacja produkuje "wyrzuconych" (*expelled*) jako cechę strukturalną.

## Key Proponents / Critics

- [[Saskia Sassen]] — *Expulsions* (2014); global theoretical framework; housing financialization as producing "expelled" subjects; loss of state territorial sovereignty
- [[Joanna Kusiak]] — Berlin-specific analysis; connection of financialization to the DWE constitutional remedy; "Pan Müller vs. corporate landlord" as key analytic distinction
- [[Deutsche Wohnen und Co enteignen]] — Vergesellschaftung as the structural response
- **Andrej Holm** — Berlin housing researcher; empirical production data showing public control outperforms free market in housing output

## Key Examples

- **Berlin 2015–2022:** 250,000 apartments owned by corporate landlords (Vonovia, Deutsche Wohnen, Pears Global and others); +44% rent increase; Mietendeckel struck down March 2021; DWE referendum September 2021.
- **Pears Global:** Hundreds of apparently independent small landlords revealed as subsidiaries of one Luxembourg-registered corporation (Correctiv investigation); the letterbox image as emblem of opaque financialized landlordism.
- **BlackRock, Blackstone:** Named in [[Uspołeczniamy Berlin]] as global financial actors with stakes in Berlin corporate housing; identified as responsible for housing crises in multiple cities worldwide.
- **Post-reunification privatization:** Berlin sold off its public housing stock cheaply in the 1990s–2000s — the original act that created the conditions for corporate accumulation. The city gave away its "political leverage."

## Critiques & Alternatives

Regulatory interventions (Mietbremse, Mietendeckel) are insufficient: they address symptoms, not structural causes. Corporate legal departments are specifically organized to find loopholes in each new regulation. Mietendeckel was struck down by Germany's Federal Constitutional Court in March 2021.

The [[Deutsche Wohnen und Co enteignen]] initiative proposes **Vergesellschaftung** (Article 15 GG) as the structural remedy: removing the housing stock from the speculative ownership circuit entirely, enabling democratic management and redirecting rental income toward maintenance, ecological retrofitting, workers' wages, and new construction.

The [[Vienna Housing Model]] represents a structural alternative that never required remediation: a city whose public land ownership was never substantially privatized maintains the capacity to govern housing development without needing to buy back what was sold.

The conceptual connection to agricultural land commodification in Poland ([[Land Commodification]]) is direct: in both cases, the mechanism is the transformation of a socially embedded resource (housing, farmland) into a financial asset extracting value to remote shareholders, with local communities bearing the costs.

**PL:** Regulacyjne interwencje niewystarczające: korporacyjne działy prawne organizowane specjalnie do obchodzenia każdej nowej regulacji; Mietendeckel obalony. DWE proponuje Vergesellschaftung jako lekarstwo strukturalne. Model wiedeński: alternatywa strukturalna nigdy niewymagająca sanacji. Powiązanie konceptualne z utowarowieniem ziemi w Polsce: ten sam mechanizm — zasób społecznie osadzony (mieszkania, ziemia rolna) przekształcony w instrument finansowy ekstrakcji wartości do odległych akcjonariuszy.
