## Definition
The wave of modernist architecture spanning roughly from the late 1950s to the 1970s. In Poland, particularly known as "modernizm poodwilżowy" (post-thaw modernism), it represented a break from Socialist Realism. It is characterized by rationalism, innovative structural logic (concrete frames, prefabrication), bold tectonics, and often monumental public or residential scales.

**PL:** *Fala architektury modernistycznej z lat ok. 1950-1970. W Polsce określana jako modernizm poodwilżowy, stanowiąca zerwanie z socrealizmem. Cechuje się racjonalizmem, innowacyjną logiką konstrukcyjną (żelbet, prefabrykacja), wyrazistą tektoniką i często monumentalną skalą budynków użyteczności publicznej lub osiedli mieszkaniowych.*

## Historical Context
A uniquely vulnerable style today. These buildings are often less than 70 years old: seen by many as outdated, functionally flawed, and ugly ("concrete blocks"), yet recognized by historians as highly coherent spatial visions. They exist in a legal grey area—too old to meet modern technical/safety building codes, but supposedly "too young" or unappreciated to be systematically entered into heritage protection registers.

## Critiques & Alternatives
The main crisis facing this architecture today is its collision with modern building codes (fire safety, thermal insulation). Standard modernization techniques (thick styrofoam, standard PVC windows) completely obscure the subtle relief, tectonics, and proportions that defined the style.
