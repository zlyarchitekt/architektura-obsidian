## Definition
The understanding that engaging with manageable physical risks—such as climbing, balancing, and manipulating unstructured environments—is a crucial developmental need for children, teaching them resilience and spatial judgment.

**PL:** *Przekonanie, że podejmowanie możliwego do opanowania ryzyka (wspinanie się, balansowanie) jest kluczową potrzebą rozwojową dzieci, uczącą odporności i oceny przestrzennej.*

## Historical Context
Generations growing up before the 1990s routinely experienced high-risk, unstructured play (e.g., on construction sites or improvised structural playgrounds). In the 21st century, liability culture and hyper-parenting have led to the systematic removal of risk from designated play areas.

## Key Proponents
[[Maja Brzozowska-Brywczyńska]], [[Mikołaj Smoleński]]

## Key Examples
Hanging upside down from carpet beaters (trzepaki), playing in construction sites, or the high metal framing of older housing estates.

## Critiques & Alternatives
The complete removal of risk leads to "surplus safety," which paradoxically can make children more prone to accidents outside the playground because they lack risk-assessment skills.
