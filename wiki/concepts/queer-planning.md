## Definition

Queer planning (*queerowa planistyka*) is an approach to urban planning and design that takes queer theory as its analytical and ethical framework. Its premise is that conventional planning has been — and remains — heteronormative and cisnormative: it encodes the assumptions, values, and spatial preferences of white, cisgender, heterosexual men, while marginalising or invisibilising other bodies, sexualities, and ways of living. Queer planning foregrounds the *process* of planning (who is included, whose knowledge counts, what power relations are reproduced) over the *product* (the plan or building). Drawing on Judith Butler's formulation of queer as a verb rather than a noun — "always and only redeployed, twisted, toward urgent and expanding political goals" — queer planning understands itself as permanently in motion, refusing to crystallise into a fixed identity or territory.

**PL:** Queerowa planistyka to podejście do urbanistyki i projektowania oparte na teorii queer jako ramie analitycznej i etycznej. Premisa: konwencjonalne planowanie jest i pozostaje heteronormatywne i cisnormatywne. Foregrunduje *proces* planowania (kto jest włączony, czyja wiedza się liczy) ponad *produkt*. Za Judith Butler: queer jako czasownik — "zawsze i tylko ponownie rozmieszczane" — stąd queerowa planistyka permanentnie w ruchu, odmawia skrystalizowania w stałą tożsamość lub terytorium.

## Historical Context

Research connecting urban space and LGBTQ+ identity developed from the 1980s onward (linkage between psychosexual identity and urban space was documented from the early 1980s). Academic formalisations include work by Petra L. Doan, Sue Hendler & Michael Backs (*Queerying Planning: Challenging Heteronormative Assumptions and Reframing Planning Practice*, 2011), and the broader *Queerying Planning* anthology. In 2017, Krystin Wong-Tam edited *Any Other Way: How Toronto Got Queer*, bringing the discourse into civic planning practice. By the 2020s, commercial consultancies (notably ARUP's *Queering Public Space* report) had incorporated queer planning into mainstream practice in high-income countries.

In Poland, engagement with queer planning is essentially absent as of 2022 (Michał Kowalski's RZUT-33 essay is the first Polish-language introduction). Polish urbanists had begun writing about feminist cities and human rights urbanism, but queer-specific planning remained unaddressed.

**PL:** Badania łączące przestrzeń i tożsamość LGBTQ+ rozwijają się od lat 80. Akademicka formalizacja: antologia *Queerying Planning* (Doan, 2011); Hendler & Backs; Krystin Wong-Tam (*Any Other Way: How Toronto Got Queer*, 2017). W 2022 ARUP wydał *Queering Public Space*. W Polsce — nieobecne do 2022 r.; esej Kowalskiego jest pierwszym polskojęzycznym wprowadzeniem.

## Key Examples

- **1QMXPL (Pierwsze Polskie Miastx Queer)**: Kowalski's manifesto vision — five principles: relations over competition; publicized emotions; spatial anti-structure (queer pockets city-wide); economic justice; extreme characters design. Acknowledges it is utopian but frames it as the opening of a necessary discussion.
- **Old Compton Street/Soho (London), Chueca (Madrid), Castro (San Francisco)**: classic queer enclaves; cited by Kowalski as cautionary examples — captured by commercial interests, gentrified, dominated by affluent white cis-men; women and non-binary people were minorities and excluded; "never really queer."
- **ARUP, *Queering Public Space* report**: evidence that queer planning has entered commercial mainstream in high-income countries.

**PL:**
- 1QMXPL: manifest Kowalskiego; pięć zasad; utopia jako punkt wyjścia do dyskusji.
- Castro, Chueca, Old Compton Street: przestrogi — koloniach przez kapitał, wykluczenie kobiet i osób niebinarnych.
- ARUP: dowód na wejście queerowego planowania do mainstreamu komercyjnego.

## Critiques & Alternatives

- **Is it physically possible?** Can spatial design produce the non-normative qualities queer planning aspires to, or is the built environment too inert a medium for such fluid social ambitions? Kowalski acknowledges the challenge but argues that space can at minimum *enable* or *obstruct* queer life.
- **Polish political context**: the essay was written under conditions of intense political hostility to LGBTQ+ rights in Poland (2015–2022 PiS government, anti-LGBTQ+ "ideology-free zones"). Kowalski offers a vision knowing it cannot be implemented immediately.
- **Enclave vs. network**: the queer enclave model (Castro, Chueca) has historically enabled safety and community-building but is vulnerable to commercial capture and gentrification; the distributed network model Kowalski proposes is theoretically more resilient but lacks physical anchorage.
- **Who is "queer"?** The category is deliberately open, but this creates the risk that "queer planning" becomes either so inclusive as to lose specificity or so narrowly LGBTQ-focused as to miss the broader structural critique of heteronormativity.
- **Extreme characters design**: Kowalski cites this design principle (designing for atypical/extreme users improves outcomes for all) without fully developing its implications for queer space.

**PL:** Czy przestrzeń może produkować nieheteronormatywność? Polskie realia polityczne (antagonizm PiS). Enklawa vs. sieć — każde rozwiązanie ma wady. Kto jest "queer"? Zasada extreme characters design — nierozwinięta.
