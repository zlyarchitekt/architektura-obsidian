## Definition
A compact, fitted kitchen designed by Margarete Schütte-Lihotzky in 1926 for Ernst May's Frankfurt housing programme. At 6.5 m², it unified aluminium drawers, an electric oven, oak worktops, and standardised cabinetry into a single efficient workstation. It became the prototype for modern fitted kitchens, rolled out as standard in 10,000 Frankfurt units with costs amortised through rent.

**PL:** *Kompaktowa, zabudowana kuchnia zaprojektowana przez Margarete Schütte-Lihotzky w 1926 r. dla programu mieszkaniowego Ernsta Maya we Frankfurcie. O powierzchni 6,5 m², łączyła aluminiowe szuflady, elektryczny piekarnik, dębowe blaty i standaryzowaną zabudowę w jedną efektywną stację roboczą. Stała się prototypem nowoczesnych kuchni zabudowanych, wdrożonym jako standard w 10 tys. frankfurckich mieszkań z kosztami rozłożonymi na czynsz.*

## Historical Context
Emerging from Weimar-era rationalist housing reform, the Frankfurt Kitchen applied industrial time-motion studies to domestic labour. It was simultaneously a design breakthrough and a tool of social engineering: the city used it to demonstrate bourgeois prosperity to working-class tenants, while the compact size fixed women within a single optimised circuit of unpaid work.

## Key Proponents
[[Margarete Schütte-Lihotzky]], Ernst May

## Key Examples
- **Frankfurt Kitchen** (1926) — canonical prototype.
- **Barbara Brukalska's WSM Żoliborz kitchen** (1927) — Polish response: cheaper, open to the living room, rejecting isolation.

## Critiques & Alternatives
Feminist critics note that the Frankfurt Kitchen perfected domestic efficiency without questioning who performed the labour. Brukalska's open annex and later kitchen-island typologies challenged this isolation by reintegrating cooking into social space.
