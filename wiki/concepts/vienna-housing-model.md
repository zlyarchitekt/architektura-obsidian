## Definition

The Vienna housing model refers to the system by which Vienna has maintained structural affordability and political leverage over its built environment by retaining public ownership of significant portions of its urban land across successive governments, economic periods, and ideological shifts. Unlike most European capital cities, Vienna never substantially privatized its public housing land base — a choice accumulated across more than a century of consistent municipal governance. The consequence is that the city can decide what is built where, for whom, and at what density, in ways that cities that sold their land cannot. In [[Joanna Kusiak]]'s formulation: "land ownership is the real possibility of practising politics."

**PL:** Wiedeński model mieszkaniowy to system, w którym Wiedeń utrzymał strukturalną dostępność cenową i polityczną kontrolę nad środowiskiem zabudowanym, zachowując publiczną własność znacznej części ziemi miejskiej przez kolejne rządy, okresy ekonomiczne i zmiany ideologiczne. W ujęciu Kusiak: "własność ziemi to realna możliwość uprawiania polityki."

## Historical Context

Vienna's public housing tradition is one of the longest and most celebrated in the world. The foundational period is ***Rotes Wien*** (*Red Vienna*, 1918–1934): the social-democratic municipal government launched a mass housing programme financed by a progressive "luxury tax" on private wealth. Between 1923 and 1934, the city built approximately **64,000 new apartments** in large communal complexes (*Gemeindebauten*), most famously the **Karl-Marx-Hof** (1930, Karl Ehn) — a 1,382-apartment complex running 1,100 metres along Heiligenstädter Strasse, conceived as a city-within-a-city with communal laundries, baths, a kindergarten, a dental clinic, and cooperative retail. The Gemeindebauten were not propaganda; they were functional, durable, and embedded in the city fabric.

Critically, the Gemeindebauten survived the end of Red Vienna — the Austro-fascist coup of 1934 that ended municipal social democracy — and then survived the Nazi annexation (1938–45) and the postwar political transitions. They were maintained and expanded by governments across the ideological spectrum because they had become the structural backbone of Viennese housing. Today, approximately **60% of Vienna's population lives in subsidized or municipal housing**, making Vienna the largest municipal housing provider in Europe.

**PL:** Fundamentalny okres: *Rotes Wien* (Czerwony Wiedeń, 1918–1934) — program masowego budownictwa komunalnego finansowany progresywnym "podatkiem od luksusu". Karl-Marx-Hof (1930): 1382 mieszkań na 1100 metrach, z pralnią, żłobkiem, kliniką stomatologiczną i kooperatywnym handlem. Gemeindebauten przeżyły austro-faszyzm (1934), aneksję nazistowską (1938–45) i powojenne przemiany. Dziś ok. **60% wiedeńczyków** mieszka w subsydiowanym lub komunalnym mieszkalnictwie — Wiedeń jest największym komunalnym dostawcą mieszkań w Europie.

## The Structural Mechanism: Land as Political Capital

What distinguishes Vienna from Berlin, London, or Warsaw is not merely the quantity of social housing but the **structural basis** that makes it sustainable and continually renewable: the city still owns large land reserves, which it leases (*Erbbaurecht*-like instruments) to non-profit housing developers at below-market rates. The land cost advantage — often 40–60% of total development cost — enables rents well below the private market without ongoing subsidy requirements. The land is not sold; only the right to build on it is transferred. Ownership remains municipal.

This structure is significant for three reasons:
1. **Permanence:** Socialised apartments cannot be re-privatised so long as the land remains public, because the land lease is conditional on non-profit use.
2. **Self-financing:** Rental income from municipal housing funds maintenance, ecological retrofitting, and new development — the city does not depend on annual budget allocations for housing provision.
3. **Political capacity:** Land ownership means the city can decide what is built at every development cycle — it is not dependent on negotiating with private landowners who may hold out, speculate, or choose not to develop.

The conceptual parallel with [[Romea Muryń]]'s lease model for Polish agricultural land ([[Land Commodification]]) is direct: in both proposals, separating use rights from ownership removes land from the speculative circuit while enabling productive use. In both cases, the aim is that the public sector captures land value increments rather than private capital.

**PL:** Mechanizm strukturalny: Wiedeń posiada duże rezerwy gruntów, które dzierżawi deweloperom non-profit poniżej stawek rynkowych. Koszt gruntu — często 40–60% kosztów inwestycji — zostaje wyeliminowany z kalkulacji, co umożliwia czynsze poniżej rynku bez stałego dotowania. Ziemia nie jest sprzedawana. Trzy konsekwencje: (1) trwałość — usocjalizowane mieszkania nie mogą być reprywatyzowane; (2) samofinansowanie — dochody z najmu finansują utrzymanie i nową budowę; (3) zdolność polityczna — własność ziemi = możliwość decydowania o każdym nowym budynku.

## The Double Face of Vienna

Kusiak notes in [[Uspołeczniamy Berlin]] that Vienna has a "double face" (*podwójna twarz*): on one side, a city associated with imperial aesthetics, fin-de-siècle architecture, and an apparent immutability since the 19th century; on the other, a city that experiments aggressively with social housing innovations and pro-ecological urban interventions. In 2021, Vienna's city council ordered the removal of asphalt from several streets, converting them into small urban parks — a gesture of urban ecological practice that is possible precisely because the city owns the land under those streets. No negotiation with private landowners, no compensation claims, no years of planning permission disputes: the city decided, the city acted.

The double face is not a paradox but a product of the structural foundation: public land ownership enables political experimentation regardless of the aesthetic conservatism of the existing urban fabric. The city can be simultaneously traditional in appearance and radical in governance.

Vienna's post-2000 housing experiments extend the Red Vienna tradition into new territory: the **Baugruppen** model (cooperative self-organised housing groups that collectively commission and finance residential buildings on city-leased land), **Wohnfonds Wien** (the city's housing fund, which manages land allocation and non-profit developer selection), and large-scale mixed urban developments on former industrial sites, most notably **Seestadt Aspern** — a planned new district of 20,000 apartments on the eastern edge of the city, entirely on municipal land, with a tram line extended before the first residents moved in.

**PL:** "Podwójna twarz" Wiednia: z jednej strony miasto tradycyjne, cesarskie; z drugiej — agresywnie eksperymentujące. W 2021 r. magistrat nakazał zerwanie asfaltu z kilku ulic i przebudowę na parki — możliwe, bo miasto jest właścicielem ziemi. Brak negocjacji z prywatnymi właścicielami, brak roszczeń odszkodowawczych. Eksperymenty po 2000 r.: model Baugruppen (spółdzielnie na dzierżawionych gruntach), Wohnfonds Wien, Seestadt Aspern (20 tys. mieszkań na obrzeżach, całkowicie na gruncie komunalnym, linia tramwajowa przed pierwszymi mieszkańcami).

## Land Ownership as Political Capacity: The Core Principle

Kusiak's formulation in [[Uspołeczniamy Berlin]] makes the general principle explicit:

> "Every city is simply land — a collection of plots with buildings and people on them. If we want to govern any city, we must have control over that land. Then we can decide what can be built where. Through architecture and land law, we also have influence over people and social behaviour — this is the difference between whether a theatre, a shopping centre, a square, or a car park is built in a given place. Selling these plots is selling the real possibility of practising politics."

Vienna did not sell this possibility. Berlin did. The Vienna model's value is not primarily the architectural legacy of the Gemeindebauten or even the 60% subsidized housing figure — it is the structural capacity that remains intact because the city retained its land. Every urban governance decision — transport, ecology, culture, housing affordability — is conditioned on whether the city owns the land on which it is to be implemented.

**PL:** "Każde miasto jest po prostu ziemią — zbiorem działek, na których znajdują się budynki i ludzie. Jeżeli chcemy zarządzać jakimkolwiek miastem, to musimy mieć kontrolę nad tą ziemią. [...] Wyprzedawanie tych działek jest wyprzedawaniem realnej możliwości uprawiania polityki." Wiedeń nie wyprzedał tej możliwości. Berlin — tak. Wartość modelu wiedeńskiego to nie architektura Gemeindebauten ani nawet 60% mieszkań subsydiowanych — to nienaruszona zdolność strukturalna, która pozostaje dlatego, że miasto zachowało ziemię.

## Limits and Transferability

The Vienna model is not directly transferable to other cities or countries:
- It is the product of a specific historical trajectory (Red Vienna 1918–34; postwar political consensus; uninterrupted municipal continuity) that cannot be replicated by policy decision
- It required no structural break: Vienna never needed to socialize housing because it never privatized the basis for social housing
- It operates within Austrian federal law; instruments available to Vienna (Erbbaurecht-like land leases, Wohnfonds, non-profit cooperative framework) are not universally available
- As Kusiak explicitly warns: "there is no point in copying models one-to-one." Vienna's context is unique

But the model is valuable as a **proof of concept**: it demonstrates that public land ownership + democratic governance = structural housing affordability without perpetual crisis management. For cities that have already privatized their land — Berlin, London, most Polish cities — the lesson is that returning to structural control requires a structural instrument: [[Socialization (Vergesellschaftung)]] (DWE's Art. 15 GG approach) or a lease model ([[Romea Muryń]]'s proposal for post-PGR agricultural land in Poland). Prevention was simpler than remediation, but remediation is still possible — it just requires a constitutional mechanism rather than a regulatory one.

**PL:** Model wiedeński nie jest bezpośrednio przenoszalny: jest produktem konkretnej trajektorii historycznej i nie wymagał żadnego strukturalnego przełomu. Kusiak explicite ostrzega przed kopiowaniem "jeden do jednego". Ale jest wartościowy jako *dowód słuszności koncepcji*: publiczna własność ziemi + demokratyczne zarządzanie = strukturalna dostępność mieszkań. Dla miast, które już sprzedały swoją ziemię: odzyskanie kontroli strukturalnej wymaga instrumentu strukturalnego — Vergesellschaftung (DWE) lub modelu dzierżawy (Muryń). Prewencja była prostsza niż sanacja, ale sanacja jest możliwa — wymaga mechanizmu konstytucyjnego, nie regulacyjnego.
