## Definition

Vacant property (*pustostany*) refers to buildings or plots that have been completed (or were previously occupied) but remain unoccupied and unoperational — generating no economic output, no social use, and no tax revenue while degrading the surrounding environment and requiring maintenance costs from owners. In urban planning terms, vacancy represents both a resource failure (materials, energy, and land embedded in the building are wasted) and a spatial dysfunction (the dead building suppresses street life, signals decline, and generates neighbourhood stigma). The concept encompasses residential vacancies (empty flats, abandoned houses), commercial vacancies (empty offices, retail units), and large-scale structural vacancies (permanently closed shopping centres, unlet speculative developments).

**PL:** Pustostany to budynki lub działki ukończone (lub wcześniej użytkowane), które pozostają niezamieszkałe i nieoperacyjne — bez przychodów, użytku społecznego ani dochodów podatkowych. Reprezentują zarówno marnotrawstwo zasobów (materiały, energia, ziemia zawarte w budynku), jak i dysfunkcję przestrzenną (martwy budynek tłumi życie ulicy, sygnalizuje upadek i stygmatyzuje okolicę).

## Historical Context

Vacancy is endemic to market economies — it appears in cycles of speculative development, functional obsolescence, demographic decline, and demand mismatch. In Poland, the phenomenon has several distinct origins: post-transformation industrial collapse (factory sites, PGR complexes); speculative retail construction in the 2000s–2010s (a wave of shopping centres with supply far exceeding demand); post-pandemic footfall collapse in enclosed malls; and demographic shrinkage in smaller cities whose largest employers are closing (mining, energy).

The structural crisis of the enclosed shopping centre typology is the dominant contemporary driver of large-scale vacancy in Poland and across Europe. Post-pandemic footfall decline of ~49% (vs. March 2019), the rise of e-commerce, and the competition from open-format retail parks (discount-anchored, car-accessible, lower operating costs) have left many enclosed malls structurally unviable. The response — transformation into mixed-use/hybrid buildings (residential, office, logistics, leisure, public use) — creates complex adaptive reuse challenges.

**PL:** Pustostany są endemiczne dla ekonomii rynkowych: pojawiają się w cyklach spekulacyjnego budownictwa, funkcjonalnej obsolescencji, demograficznego spadku i niedopasowania popytu. W Polsce: tereny poprzemysłowe po transformacji; spekulacyjne galerie handlowe lat 2000–2010; post-covidowy kryzys frekwencji; demograficzny skurcz małych miast. Dominant: kryzys strukturalny zamkniętych galerii handlowych.

## Key Legal Frameworks

**Netherlands — Leegstandswet (Vacancy Act, 2010)**: Considered the most developed European vacancy legislation. Key provisions:
- Municipalities may adopt "vacancy regulations" requiring owners to register properties vacant for >6 months.
- After 12 months of vacancy, the municipality may designate a user (physical or legal person).
- Owner must offer a usage contract to the designated user within 3 months.
- Applies to residential, commercial, and office properties.

**Poland**: No equivalent vacancy legislation exists. Polish municipalities lack legal instruments to require vacancy registration, impose users, or negotiate development conditions with private owners. The Centre for Revitalisation Advisory Services (IMRiR) has proposed converting municipal-owned vacancies into affordable housing, but privately owned large vacancies (such as [[Galeria Bawełnianka]]) remain outside any formal regulation.

**PL:** Holandia: Leegstandswet (2010) — obowiązek zgłoszenia pustostanu >6 mies., prawo gminy do wyznaczenia najemcy po 12 mies. Polska: brak odpowiednika; gminy bez narzędzi regulacyjnych dla prywatnych pustostanów.

## Key Examples in the Wiki

- **[[Galeria Bawełnianka]]** (Bełchatów, Poland): 35,000 m² shopping centre completed 2012, never opened; went bankrupt 2015; Poland's most prominent structural vacancy case.

## Critiques & Alternatives

- **Property rights vs. public interest**: in liberal property law frameworks, a private owner's right to leave a building vacant is difficult to override without constitutional reform — vacancy acts create regulatory tension with property rights.
- **Adaptation complexity**: converting enclosed malls to mixed-use/hybrid buildings is technically and financially complex — these buildings were designed with internal circulation systems, deep floor plates, and infrastructure unsuited to residential or office conversion.
- **Shrinking city paradox**: in cities losing population (Bełchatów, formerly industrial towns), vacancy is a symptom of structural decline, not a solvable planning problem — adding uses to a declining market may only shift vacancy from one building to another.
- **Circular economy of architecture**: the most productive frame for vacancy is material and resource circularity — an empty building embeds millions of euros of materials and energy; its demolition destroys that value; its conversion preserves it. This frame shifts the question from "how to fill it" to "how to maintain embedded resource value."

**PL:** Napięcie prawo własności vs. interes publiczny. Złożoność adaptacji technicznej (głębokie płyty, wewnętrzna cyrkulacja galerii = trudna do mieszkania/biur). Paradoks kurczącego się miasta: vacancy to symptom strukturalnego upadku, nie problem planistyczny. Gospodarka cyrkularna: pustostan = wartość zasobu, demolka = niszczenie tej wartości.
