## Summary
In this essay from Kwartalnik RZUT, Dominika Trybe explores the role of urban nature, using the red fox as a focal point to highlight the "third landscape" of London. The text argues for a shift in perspective where landscape architects act as "landscape physicians" (adhering to *primum non nocere*) and cities are understood as shared ecosystems encompassing human and non-human stakeholders.

**PL:** *W eseju z Kwartalnika RZUT Dominika Trybe bada rolę miejskiej przyrody, wykorzystując rudego lisa jako punkt wyjścia do zbadania „trzeciego krajobrazu” Londynu. Tekst postuluje zmianę perspektywy, w której architekci krajobrazu działają jako „lekarze krajobrazu” (stosując zasadę primum non nocere), a miasta są rozumiane jako wspólne ekosystemy obejmujące ludzkich i nieludzkich interesariuszy.*

## Key Points
- London's status as a "forest": with 21% tree cover and over 8 million trees, London meets the UN definition of a forest.
- The concept of the "third landscape" (from Gilles Clément) applies to London's urban ecosystem, where abandoned and marginal spaces act as vital ecological corridors.
- Animals like the urban fox and potentially reintroduced beavers act as crucial components for urban resilience.
- Daniel Raven-Ellison advocates for London as a "National Park City".
- Landscape architects should adopt a medical ethos (*primum non nocere*), diagnosing and supporting the landscape rather than merely reshaping it.
- The shift towards the "Naturocene" (a term by Periscope studio) envisions urbanization and sustainable development as mutually supportive.
