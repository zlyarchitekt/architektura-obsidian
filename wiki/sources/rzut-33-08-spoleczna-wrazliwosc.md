## Summary

Article 8 of RZUT-33 is an interview with Professor Marek Krajewski, sociologist at the Faculty of Sociology at Adam Mickiewicz University (UAM) in Poznań, conducted by Igor Łysiuk. Krajewski acts as an outside expert voice — a social scientist evaluating the new Warsaw Studium from a sociological standpoint. His overall verdict is strongly positive, particularly regarding the quality of the diagnostic work, but he identifies one significant blind spot: the document's insufficient sensitivity to class differences and the risks of gentrification.

Krajewski praises the Studium as an exceptional diagnostic document — a "textbook mapping the basic dilemmas contemporary cities face." He highlights the recognition of the following urban pathologies: sprawl and uncontrolled growth; car dominance; degradation of public spaces through neoliberal over-exploitation; deep social differentiation (including migration and aging); and rising **singularization** (the growing proportion of people living alone). He is especially appreciative of the Studium's recognition of **non-human urban actors**: the document acknowledges that cities have taken space from nature, and it plans to make coexistence more harmonious by preserving wild urban enclaves and attending to urban wildlife.

The sociologist endorses the **polycentric city** model ("wygodna lokalność") — a hierarchical network of neighbourhood centres where basic needs can be met without crossing the whole city. He notes, however, that strengthening local identity is a double-edged sword: it builds solidarity and civic responsibility but also generates local rivalries. He also underlines the Studium's attention to the **aging society** — not as a demographic statistic but as a spatial design problem requiring proximity to medical services and everyday facilities.

On mobility, Krajewski notes that the Studium's assumptions are "sometimes revolutionary" for the city centre: reducing traffic lanes, privileging pedestrians and cyclists, pushing cars out of central areas. He highlights a problem unique to cities like Warsaw, Kraków, and Gdańsk — the **temporal conflict** between the rhythms of residents and those of visitors: the bulwary wiślane (Vistula embankments) are his example of a space where the needs of local residents and incoming users are in chronic tension. The Studium addresses this by planning spaces that mediate between service functions and residential needs.

## Key Points

- **Diagnostic quality**: the Studium's analytical groundwork is of textbook quality — Krajewski rates it as one of the best urban diagnostics he has encountered in the Polish context.
- **Non-human actors**: the explicit inclusion of urban wildlife and the commitment to wild urban enclaves is a sociologically significant step — it operationalises an ecological ethics rarely present in Polish planning documents.
- **Polycentric city risk**: strengthening local identity and neighbourhood self-sufficiency also creates neighbourhood-level competition, potentially reinforcing spatial inequalities between better- and worse-served districts.
- **Class blindness**: Krajewski's main critique — the Studium is sophisticated on ecology, aging, and mobility, but insufficiently attentive to how its proposals (densification, greening, accessibility improvements) may drive gentrification and displace lower-income residents.
- **Aging society as spatial problem**: proximity to medical services and basic retail is the decisive planning variable for an aging population — the Studium registers this but does not fully operationalise it as a design standard.
- **Temporal conflicts in public space**: Warsaw's "zmorą" (plague) is the conflict between resident rhythms and visitor rhythms. The bulwary wiślane exemplify how the same space can be experienced as home by some and leisure destination by others, generating chronic conflict.
- **Revolutionary mobility**: reducing central traffic lanes is "counter-intuitive" given congestion, but the Studium correctly identifies it as the necessary direction; the inverted transport pyramid is the right framework.
- **Singularization**: the growing proportion of single-person households is named as a key challenge — it changes the spatial and service demands of the city fundamentally (smaller dwellings, more shared spaces, proximity to services).

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Krajewski's class-sensitivity critique is serious and unresolved: the Studium's quality push (greenery, mixed-use, public space) may make treated areas more expensive, not more inclusive. This echoes the critique in [[rzut-uspoleczniamy-berlin]] and the gentrification dynamics described in Deutsche Wohnen und Co enteignen activism.
- The "wild urban enclaves" idea ("w formie zenklawizowanej") is vague — how large, where, with what governance? Article 10 ([[rzut-33-10-niescisla-ochrona-przyrody]]) presumably addresses this.
- Temporal conflict as a planning problem is identified but no concrete mediation instrument is offered; this is a gap in the Studium acknowledged by Krajewski without resolution.
