## Summary

Artur Brzozowski's essay applies spatial planning analysis to a subject rarely addressed in architecture and planning discourse: cemeteries. Starting from a concrete human story (an elderly woman in Warsaw crossing the city weekly to visit her husband's grave, a practice her sister in a rural village could not maintain at all), Brzozowski maps the structural inequality in Poland's funerary infrastructure — arguing that cemeteries are a form of public infrastructure with accessibility requirements that current law and planning practice systematically fail to meet.

The essay diagnoses four interlocking problems: (1) a 1959 law (*ustawa o cmentarzach i chowaniu zmarłych*) that mandates new cemeteries at city margins, forbids the removal of brick graves, and allows earth graves to be cleared only after 20 years — a framework that fills cemeteries, pushes new ones further from cities, and reflects sanitary assumptions that are no longer valid; (2) the legal obligation for every municipality to have a communal (non-denominational) cemetery, which is met by only 32% of municipalities (798 of 2,477); (3) a sharp east-west spatial gradient, with western Poland inheriting former German evangelical cemeteries (converted to communal status after 1945) while central and eastern Poland has almost none; (4) the routine illegal practice of large cities locating communal cemeteries in neighbouring municipalities' territory (Warsaw's Cmentarz Komunalny Południowy in Piaseczno; Białystok's new cemetery in Supraśl) — which pushes cemeteries even further from city centres.

The Podlaskie voivodeship case study is chosen for its combination of severe shortage (17 communal cemeteries, 2.4% of all cemeteries, 68,901 residents per communal cemetery) and specific contextual factors: a significant Orthodox Christian minority (120,000 people — 76.6% of all Polish Orthodox), rapid ageing (25.4% of population over 60, some municipalities with 15% over 80), and rural depopulation. The near-total absence of public transport to most communal cemeteries (only Białystok and Łomża have transit access) means that elderly women — statistically the main cemetery visitors, since women outlive men by ~10 years — are frequently excluded from accessing burial sites by spatial and transport infrastructure designed around car ownership they often lack.

Brzozowski closes with the Danish model as a counter-example: cemeteries integrated into city fabric, treated as parks, open to cyclists, dog-walkers, and sports users. He frames this not as a radical reimagination but as the logical outcome of shifting cultural attitudes toward death and secularizing society — a shift already underway in Poland (Sunday mass attendance down from 41% in 2010 to 28.3% in 2021; children in religion class from 93% to 54%), which will inevitably increase demand for communal, non-denominational burial space.

## Key Points

- **Structural diagnosis**: Poland has 12,269 cemeteries (8,387 = 68% denominational, mostly Catholic). The 1959 *ustawa o cmentarzach* requires all new cemeteries at city margins; brick graves can never be removed; land for new cemeteries only designated via local spatial plans (*mpzp*). These rules fill urban cemeteries and push new ones further away.
- **Communal cemeteries**: every municipality legally required to have one, but only 798 of 2,477 municipalities (32%) do. Only 1,879 communal cemeteries exist in total.
- **East-West gradient**: western Poland has many communal cemeteries because former German evangelical cemeteries were converted after 1945. Eastern/central Poland has very few. Mazowieckie: 26 communal; zachodniopomorskie: 476.
- **Illegal cross-boundary management**: cities locate cemeteries in neighbouring municipalities' territory to avoid land costs → illegal under the 1959 law, flagged by NIK (Supreme Audit Office) 2016 report. Examples: Warsaw (Piaseczno), Białystok (Supraśl/Karakule — 7 km from city centre in forest).
- **Secularization**: Sunday mass attendance 41% (2010) → 28.3% (2021); children in religion class 93% (2010) → 54% (2021); baptisms 374,300 (2016) → 312,100 (2020); seminary first-year students halved 2015–2021. Demand for communal (non-Catholic) burial space will increase.
- **Podlaskie case study**: 710 total cemeteries, only 17 communal (2.4%); 68,901 residents per communal cemetery (national worst: łódzkie 207,110; warmińsko-mazurskie only 856); 10% Orthodox (120,000 people, 76.6% of all Polish Orthodox); 25.4% over 60 years old; 48% of municipalities saw >10% population decline since 2011.
- **Transport exclusion**: communal cemeteries have public transit access only in Białystok and Łomża; most are 3–4 km from city centres (reachable only by car); the furthest (Karakule) is 7km straight-line from Białystok's centre, 8.5km by road. The nearest communal cemetery for Suwałki residents is in Grajewo, 85 km away.
- **Gender dimension**: men die younger (8-year gap); wives outlive husbands by ~10 years; 56% of dying Polish men have their wife present, only 22.7% of dying women have a husband present. Cemetery visitors are predominantly elderly women — who are disproportionately car-less and public-transport-dependent.
- **Funeral costs vs. subsidy**: Warsaw burial costs up to 14,680 PLN; state funeral subsidy only 4,000 PLN. Rural areas: ~8,000 PLN. Communal cemeteries are cheaper and have transparent pricing (required by law); denominational pricing is set unilaterally by parish.
- **Danish model**: cemeteries integrated as public parks; cycling, dogs, sports, picnics accepted. Cited as evidence that the barrier is cultural rather than sanitary or technical.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The essay does not address how to legally force municipalities to build communal cemeteries — enforcement mechanisms for the existing legal obligation are absent from the analysis.
- The Danish model of cemetery-as-park is presented as desirable but the cultural shift required is not analysed beyond citing secularization statistics.
- The east-west gradient is partially a legacy of forced migration and Polonization policies — the essay notes the context but does not fully engage with the political history of how Germany's cemeteries became Poland's.
- Rising burial costs and demand for communal space are noted, but the economic model for expanding communal cemetery infrastructure (who pays?) is not addressed.
