## Summary

Michał Kowalski's essay-manifesto opens with a question: what privileges does the planner carry into their work? Planning is not a technically neutral field but an exercise of power — and historically, the order desired by planners has been "strictly heterosexist, favouring white, cis-male, and heteronormative concepts" (Petra L. Doan). Queer planning (*queerowa planistyka*) has been part of planning discourse in high-income countries for decades (including ARUP's commercial *Queering Public Space* report), but Polish urbanism has yet to engage with it. Kowalski's text introduces the concept and proposes a vision — acknowledged as potentially utopian — of *Pierwsze Polskie Miastx Queerowe* (1QMXPL), the First Polish Queer City.

The noun "miastx" uses gender-neutral orthography advocated by inclusive Polish linguistics (zaimki.pl). "Queer" is defined expansively: people who do not fix their gender, psychosexual, or romantic identity; people who resist cultural corsets and systemic classifications; and, drawing on Jan Kochanowski, "an open society that is cured of totalitarian impulses to make everyone the same." Judith Butler's formulation is cited: queer is not a stable identity but a verb — "always and only redeployed, twisted, from prior usage toward urgent and expanding political goals." This makes queer planning a process-focused discipline rather than a product-focused one, parallel to queer theory's emphasis on *becoming* over *being*.

The 1QMXPL is built on five spatial-organisational principles: (1) Relations over competition — mutual aid, sharing, co-operation as the city's social substrate; (2) Publicized emotions — emotional and sexual expression in public space as legitimate as political expression; Ann Cvetkovich's "politically depressed" project (Chicago 2007 pyjama protest) as example of how private affects gain collective-political dimension; (3) Spatial anti-structure — polycentric, porous, permeable urban form with transformable uses and "queer pockets" (*kieszonki queerowe*) distributed across the city; (4) Economic justice — queer planning accounts for the intersection of sexuality, poverty, race, disability, and the "moral" economic discipline of capitalism (Krystin Wong-Tam: "we live in boring, oppressive cities oriented toward production and consumption"); (5) Extreme characters design — designing for extreme cases (queer people, people with disabilities, atypical users) improves the city for everyone.

Kowalski explicitly rejects the model of a compact queer neighborhood or gay village (Castro, Chueca, Old Compton Street/Soho): these enclaves were quickly captured by commercial interests, gentrified, and dominated by affluent white cis-men, excluding women, non-binary, and economically marginalised queer people. Poland's political climate makes replicating them impossible anyway — but this is positioned as an opportunity to build something better: dispersed, transit-connected, low-budget, cooperative, resistant to gentrification by virtue of its networked rather than territorial structure.

Buduje je na pięciu zasadach: relacje ponad rywalizację, upublicznienie emocji, antystruktura przestrzenna, sprawiedliwość ekonomiczna, projektowanie dla skrajnych użytkowników. Odrzuca model getta queer (Castro, Chueca) jako skolonizowanego przez kapitał i białych cismężczyzn — proponuje rozproszoną, sieciową, niskokwotową alternatywę.

## Key Points

- Polish planning discourse lacks engagement with queer planning; international discourse (including ARUP's commercial report) has treated it for decades.
- "Miastx" — gender-neutral Polish noun form, aligned with inclusive language advocacy (zaimki.pl); the article introduces this linguistic politics into architecture/urbanism.
- Queer planning (queer urbanism) is process-focused, not product-focused; parallels queer theory's emphasis on *human doing* over *human being* (Butler, Hendler & Backs).
- Judith Butler on queer: "queer will have to remain what it is in the present never fully owned, but always and only redeployed, twisted, toward urgent and expanding political goals."
- Jan Kochanowski: "an open society, cured of totalitarian impulses to make everyone the same; a society of open difference, not closed identity."
- **1QMXPL principle 1 — Relations over competition**: mutual aid, sharing, and co-operation as social substrate; contrasted with competitive, accumulative, segregating cities.
- **1QMXPL principle 2 — Publicized emotions**: all emotions and sexualities are permitted and treated with respect in public space; Ann Cvetkovich's *Public Feelings* project (Chicago 2007 pyjama demonstration: "Depressed? It might be political"); Maria Dębińska on trans politics of suffering; emotional expression as foundation for alternative public sphere.
- **1QMXPL principle 3 — Spatial anti-structure**: polycentric, porous, non-compulsively dense city; free open spaces as transformative zones; queer pockets (*kieszonki queerowe*) distributed across the whole city; flexible, multi-functional, fluid in form and purpose; property structure fluidity and "hyper-elasticity" of uses.
- **1QMXPL principle 4 — Economic justice**: queer planning accounts for multi-dimensional economic, racial, class, and disability oppression; rejects the capitalist "moral" production-consumption model; co-operatives, collectives, patchwork families, micro-communities, social mix (Tomasz Sikora); Krystin Wong-Tam: "boring, oppressive cities oriented toward production and consumption."
- **1QMXPL principle 5 — Extreme characters design**: designing for queer people (atypical, extreme cases) improves the city for all.
- Critique of existing queer enclaves (Castro, Chueca, Old Compton Street/Soho): captured by commercial interests, gentrified, dominated by wealthy white cis-men; women and non-binary people were minorities and often excluded; "they were never really queer."
- Polish alternative: dispersed, transit-connected, walkable/cycleable, low-budget, cooperative, multi-generational, independent of age and cultural capital; networked structure more resistant to commercialisation and gentrification than territorial enclave.
- ARUP's *Queering Public Space* report cited as evidence that queer urbanism has entered commercial mainstream in high-income countries; Poland remains behind.
- Planning profession's critique: planners must question their own biases (Petra L. Doan); "Who is the object and who is the subject of post-modern planning?"
- Question left open: "Can you, reader, imagine pushing people beyond the everyday comfort zone of normative life? Looking beyond the hegemony? Giving up your own privilege?"

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Is queer planning possible under Poland's current political climate? Kowalski acknowledges the vision is potentially utopian but frames it as a starting point for discussion, not a finished plan.
- The tension between queer planning's emphasis on non-territoriality (queer as verb, not place) and the practical need for safe physical spaces remains unresolved.
- Kowalski explicitly opens the discussion rather than closing it: "I don't have a ready answer for you on how to transform Polish cities into an enclave of happy diversity and non-binariness."
