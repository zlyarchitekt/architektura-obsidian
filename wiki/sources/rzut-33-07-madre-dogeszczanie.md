## Summary

Article 7 of RZUT-33 is an interview with urbanist and researcher Kuba Snopek, conducted by Igor Łysiuk. Snopek participated in the Warsaw Studium process as an external consultant advising on project communication, and he had previously worked with the Real Estate Development and Design faculty at UC Berkeley. The article centres on the concept of *mądre dogęszczanie* — "smart" or "intelligent densification" — as the organizing principle for Warsaw's future spatial development within the framework of the new Studium.

Snopek argues that the Studium's central value is its **approach** rather than any single thematic priority: it takes Warsaw seriously as a complex organism, absorbs international trends without copying them, and translates good practices into a Warsaw-specific spatial identity. The most important overarching idea is *comfort* — interpreted broadly as microclimatic comfort (delivered by [[Blue-Green Infrastructure]]), visual and spatial comfort (ordered urban fabric, upgraded public spaces and key transport nodes), and mobility comfort (multi-modal transport choice).

On densification specifically, Snopek distinguishes three planks of the Studium's strategy: (1) exclusion zones — not all land is buildable, preventing suburban sprawl; (2) activation of underused urban reserves — industrial sites, rail yards, shopping-centre plots, and service zones are unlocked for intensive mixed-use development; (3) intelligent infill within the existing urban fabric, calibrated to each morphological type. Panel-block estates (*wielka płyta*) have abundant greenery but lack services; densification there means adding retail and community facilities while *preserving* the greenery. 1990s and 2000s developments are the opposite case: dense built fabric but degraded public space — densification must work differently.

The era of "carpet densification" — mass development on empty greenfield sites — is ending. Warsaw is running out of simple plots. The next decade will bring smaller, more complex projects combining multiple functions, negotiated between public and private actors. Snopek emphasises that [[Mixed-Use Development]] and [[Public-Private Partnership]] will define Warsaw's development pattern. He draws on the California experience: UC Berkeley's Real Estate Development and Design programme exists precisely because California cities suffer from unaffordable housing, fragmented public spaces, and poor transit — the same pathologies Warsaw is trying to prevent. The lesson is not to copy Californian solutions but to learn the habit of complex cross-sector collaboration.

## Key Points

- **Approach over content**: the Studium's greatest contribution is its analytical and synthetic methodology — deep reading of Warsaw's existing fabric combined with creative import of world trends.
- **Three-plank densification**: (1) exclusion zones preventing sprawl, (2) activation of brownfield/rail/mall reserves for intensive development, (3) typology-specific infill within existing fabric.
- **Typological sensitivity**: panel estates need *more services and less greenery* (retain green); 1990s developments need *more public space* (already dense). Same word ("densification") means different things in different morphological contexts.
- **End of easy development**: Warsaw is running low on straightforward development plots; the next decade forces complex mixed-use PPP projects on small, constrained sites.
- **Berkeley parallel**: California cities share Warsaw's pathologies (housing crisis, poor transit, fragmented public space); UC Berkeley's interdisciplinary approach is a model for collaborative development practice, not a set of solutions to import.
- **Studium as "spatial constitution"**: a coordinating legal document, not an architectural vision — local plans must conform to it, giving it binding effect.
- **Quality imperative**: the Studium pushes towards quality at every turn — development only where economically and ecologically viable; mixed programmes that reinforce rather than degrade public space.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Snopek praises the Studium's quality push but acknowledges that lex developer and PPP negotiations depend on legal tools that may not yet exist — a gap between aspiration and instrument.
- The "no carpet densification" position conflicts with the reality that market pressure will continue to seek greenfield plots at the urban periphery; the Studium's exclusion zones are only as strong as their enforcement.
- The Berkeley parallel is instructive but the institutional context differs: Polish planning law does not yet offer the value-capture or mandatory contribution mechanisms that make US PPPs effective.
