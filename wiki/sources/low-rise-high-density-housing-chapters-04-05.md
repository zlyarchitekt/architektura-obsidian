## Summary

These two chapters shift the *Low-Rise, High-Density Housing* survey from historic antecedents to the 20th century. Chapter 4 frames the modern era as a period where permanent compositional principles (identified in Chapters 1–3) are subjected to "minor deformations" driven by location, period, and cultural context. The authors identify three forces that shaped 20th-century low-rise, high-density housing: (1) the functionalist break from stylistic canons, improving standards but often producing sterile environments; (2) Dutch Structuralism's critique of modernist cellular fragmentation and its attempt to restore wholeness through labyrinthine order and functional mix; and (3) the enduring necessity of "presence" and "garden" as design categories, now reframed through issues of atomisation, collective responsibility, and participatory design.

**PL:** Te dwa rozdziały przenoszą przegląd *Low-Rise, High-Density Housing* z historycznych poprzedników do wieku XX. Rozdział 4 ujmuje epokę nowoczesną jako okres, w którym stałe zasady kompozycyjne (zidentyfikowane w rozdziałach 1–3) podlegają "drobnym deformacjom" napędzanym przez lokalizację, epokę i kontekst kulturowy. Autorzy wskazują trzy siły kształtujące dwudziestowieczną niską zabudowę o wysokiej gęstości: (1) funkcjonalistyczne zerwanie z kanonami stylistycznymi, poprawiające standardy, ale często produkujące sterylne środowiska; (2) krytyka holenderskiego strukturalizmu wobec modernistycznej fragmentacji komórkowej i próba przywrócenia całości przez porządek labiryntowy i mieszankę funkcjonalną; (3) trwała potrzeba "obecności" i "ogrodu" jako kategorii projektowych, obecnie przeformułowanych przez problemy atomizacji, odpowiedzialności zbiorowej i projektowania partycypacyjnego.

Chapter 5 presents 14 international projects from the first half of the 20th century, organised chronologically and geographically. The selection covers:
- **British garden cities**: Letchworth (Parker & Unwin, 1903) and Hampstead Garden Suburb (Unwin, 1905) — blending rural and urban advantages through strict regulations, integrated green networks, and vernacular-modern synthesis.
- **Amsterdam School**: Michael de Klerk's Spaarndammerplantsoen "The Ship" (1917) — expressionist brick maisonettes with communal gardens, breaking from standardised functionalism through urban-contextual massing.
- **Dutch Modernism**: J.J.P. Oud's Mathenesse (1922), Weissenhof row house (1927), and Kiefhoek (1928–30) — poetic functionalism, crystallised white forms, and precisely calculated minimal dwellings with garden access.
- **American modernism**: R.M. Schindler's Pueblo Ribera Court (1923, San Diego) — concrete courtyard housing with climatic pergolas and U-shaped garden enclosures; Frank Lloyd Wright's Suntop Homes (1938–42, Pennsylvania) — rotating quadruple units creating pinwheel privacy.
- **German modernism**: Bruno Taut's Onkel-Toms-Hütte (1925–27, Berlin) — row-house richness within modernist economy; Walter Gropius's Törten Siedlung (1926–27, Dessau) — prefab aerated-concrete cubes with ribbon windows; Ernst May's Praunheim (1926–30, Frankfurt) — garden-city-inspired flexible row houses with split-level gardens.
- **Austrian modernism**: Werkbundsiedlung Vienna (1931–32) — 31 architects, 70 houses, pavilion-like experiments in interior-nature relationships and level-shifted differentiation.
- **Nordic modernism**: Alvar Aalto's Kauttua Terrace Houses (1939, Finland) — contour-adhering, level-shifted volumes with roof-garden terraces and shared forest.
- **Swiss modernism**: Werkbundsiedlung Zürich (1939) — cooperative-built row houses and multi-apartment blocks on axes, with vertical shifting for garden intimacy.

**PL:** Rozdział 5 prezentuje 14 międzynarodowych projektów z pierwszej połowy XX wieku, zorganizowanych chronologicznie i geograficznie. Wybór obejmuje: angielskie miasta-ogrody (Letchworth, Hampstead), szkołę amsterdamską (de Klerk), modernizm holenderski (Oud), modernizm amerykański (Schindler, Wright), modernizm niemiecki (Taut, Gropius, May), modernizm austriacki (Werkbundsiedlung Wiedeń), modernizm nordycki (Aalto) i szwajcarski (Werkbundsiedlung Zurych).

## Key Points

- The 20th century introduced a permanent housing crisis (industrialisation, urban overcrowding, post-war shortage) that made low-rise, high-density housing a mass-scale necessity rather than a vernacular accident.
- Ebenezer Howard's *Garden Cities of To-morrow* (1902) reframed the garden city not as rural retreat but as an integrated foundation for efficient urban life.
- Modernism's functional break improved housing standards but produced "anatomically specialised, lifeless cells"; Dutch Structuralism responded by seeking "labyrinthine order" and functional mix borrowed from indigenous settlement patterns.
- The "presence" concept reappears in 20th-century examples as responsiveness to climate (Schindler's pergolas, Aalto's contour adherence), social change (disappearance of servant's quarters, integration of household appliances), and the need for collective meaning against atomisation.
- The garden evolves from vegetable plot to multi-functional space: terrace, climatic buffer, social venue, symbolic enclosure. In Dutch floating estates (Ijburg), it even becomes water itself.
- Key technical innovations of the period: prefabricated aerated concrete (Gropius), flexible/mergeable floor plans (May), level-shifted differentiation (Werkbundsiedlung Vienna), rotation for privacy (Wright).

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The authors celebrate modernism's functional improvements while simultaneously critiquing its "lifeless cells" and "frozen white sculptures." Is this a productive dialectic or an unresolved ambivalence?
- Dutch Structuralism is praised for restoring "wholeness" by borrowing from indigenous settlements (kasbahs, bazaars), yet these same indigenous forms were classified in Chapters 1–3 as vernacular "growth" types without architects. Can modernist authorship authentically replicate non-authored spatial logics?
- The claim that "participatory ways of thinking and collective design work" are today's most important duties sits uneasily with the fact that most 20th-century examples in Chapter 5 are top-down, architect-led estates. Where is the participatory evidence?
- Frank Lloyd Wright's Suntop Homes were never fully built (only one quadruple unit realised). Does including an unbuilt project weaken the survey's empirical claims, or strengthen its typological ones?
