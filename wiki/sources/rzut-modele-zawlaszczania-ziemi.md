## Summary

Romea Muryń traces three phases of Polish agricultural land transformation: communist nationalization under the *plan sześcioletni* (1950–1955) and the PGR system; post-1989 privatization through the state land agency (AWRSP → ANR → KOWR), which sold off the bulk of the state portfolio without social safeguards; and the current condition in which foreign capital — primarily Dutch (35%) and German (38%) corporations — controls 73% of all foreign-held Polish agricultural land. By 2016, private ownership had risen from 76.3% (1990) to 91.4%, leaving the state with only 8.6% of agricultural land, overwhelmingly concentrated in the formerly PGR-dominated western and northern regions.

**PL:** Muryń śledzi trzy fazy transformacji polskiej ziemi rolniczej: komunistyczną nacjonalizację w ramach planu sześcioletniego i system PGR; prywatyzację po 1989 r. przez agencję skarbu państwa (AWRSP → ANR → KOWR), która wyprzedała większość zasobu bez zabezpieczeń społecznych; oraz obecny stan, w którym kapitał zagraniczny — głównie holenderski (35%) i niemiecki (38%) — kontroluje 73% wszystkich zagranicznych udziałów w polskiej ziemi rolniczej.

The article argues that the 2016 land trade act — designed to limit foreign acquisition — is structurally too late: it applies only to the 8.6% state land still under KOWR management, leaving the 91.4% already privatized beyond its reach. Rural municipalities suffer from the lowest local spatial plan coverage in the country, making them particularly vulnerable to speculative and extractive land use. A comparative spatial analysis of three village types — small-scale (Osnowo, Brzozowo), medium-scale (Michałówek), and large-scale former-PGR (Bieganów) — demonstrates how ownership structure directly determines spatial form and quality of rural life.

**PL:** Artykuł argumentuje, że ustawa o obrocie ziemią z 2016 r. jest strukturalnie spóźniona: dotyczy jedynie 8,6% ziemi państwowej pod zarządem KOWR, pozostawiając 91,4% już sprywatyzowanej ziemi poza jej zasięgiem. Gminy wiejskie mają najniższe pokrycie miejscowymi planami zagospodarowania przestrzennego, co czyni je szczególnie podatnymi na spekulacyjne i ekstraktywne użytkowanie ziemi.

Muryń proposes a "new agricultural reform": state buyback of land to restore the public portfolio, a flexible lease model (10–60 years) separating the right to use from ownership in order to eliminate land speculation, and decentralisation of management away from the central KOWR/Ministry structure. She frames the argument through Saskia Sassen's global analysis of land commodification and reduced state sovereignty, concluding: "land is neither a typical commodity nor a typical means of production — it cannot be subjected exclusively to market mechanisms."

**PL:** Muryń proponuje "nową reformę rolną": odkup ziemi przez Skarb Państwa w celu odbudowy zasobu publicznego, elastyczny model dzierżawy (10–60 lat) oddzielający prawo użytkowania od własności w celu wyeliminowania spekulacji, oraz decentralizację zarządzania. Argumentację opiera na globalnej analizie Saskii Sassen dotyczącej utowarowienia ziemi.

## Key Points

- In 1990, 76.3% of agricultural land was privately owned; by 2016: 91.4% — state retains only 8.6%.
- The state land agency (ANR, now KOWR) sold off land from 1991 onward without area limits or social criteria; family farm priority clauses were added only in 2003.
- Foreign capital acquired 73% of all foreign-held agricultural land: Dutch companies 35%, German 38%. Largest concentrations in Zachodniopomorskie (32%) and Warmińsko-Mazurskie (19%).
- The 2016 land trade act applies only to remaining state land (~8.6%); the 91.4% already in private hands is unaffected.
- PGR territories were equipped with housing and social infrastructure funded by the state, which then passed to private buyers without any compensation to local governments or residents.
- Rural municipalities have the lowest local plan (*mpzp*) coverage in Poland; purely agricultural communes have the least of all.
- Three village typologies by land scale: small-scale (individual farms, Kujawsko-Pomorskie), medium-scale (balanced, Opolskie), large-scale post-PGR (Bieganów, Lubuskie — fully captured by foreign agribusiness).
- Proposed reform: stop state land sales immediately; long-term state buyback programme; lease model to replace ownership transfer; decentralise management from KOWR to regional/local level.
- Core thesis (citing Polish Constitution, Art. 5): land is a common good requiring public stewardship, not a market asset.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The proposed "new agricultural reform" risks comparison to PRL nationalization — Muryń acknowledges the tension but does not fully resolve it, arguing that the new model differs through decentralization and lease-not-ownership.
- The article focuses exclusively on agricultural land; urban land commodification (which intersects with urban sprawl and the WZ instrument) is not addressed, leaving a gap between this source and [[rzut-ustanowiony-chaos]].
- The decentralization proposal is structurally at odds with the existing KOWR/Ministry framework; no transition mechanism is specified.
- Statistics are drawn from reports up to 2018 — the legal landscape may have evolved further since the 2022 land reform discussions began.
- Animex and Cargill Poland are named as the largest agribusiness operators in Poland, both foreign-owned — but their specific land area is not given.
