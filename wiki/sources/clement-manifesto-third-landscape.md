## Summary

The *Manifesto of the Third Landscape* is Gilles Clément's distilled theoretical statement on the ecological and political significance of spaces neglected by human activity — roadsides, wastelands, urban voids, abandoned industrial sites — which he calls collectively the Third Landscape (*Tiers Paysage*). Originally published in 2004 and reissued here in 2022 by Trans Europe Halles, the manifesto is structured as twelve analytical sections (Definition, Extent, Character, Status, Stakes, Mobility, Evolution, Scale, Representation, Relationship to Time, to Society, to Culture) followed by a set of policy prescriptions organised around those same themes.

The central claim is that the Third Landscape — alongside primary sets (virgin ecosystems never exploited) and reserves (administratively protected areas) — constitutes the main planetary refuge for biodiversity. Everywhere else, in intensively managed agricultural, urban, or tourism spaces, diversity is "driven out." The manifesto frames this not merely as an ecological observation but as a political and cultural argument: the Third Landscape occupies the same position in relation to organised territory as the Third State in Sieyès's 1789 pamphlet — "Everything. Has done nothing. Aspires to be Something." It is space expressing neither power nor submission to power.

The urban dimension is systematically addressed: urbanisation creates a "mesh" of built fabric, and the degree of mesh closure determines whether biological corridors remain open. Closed meshes eliminate exchanges between the "vacuoles" of biodiversity within the urban membrane; open meshes and biological corridors (hedges, roadsides, riverbanks) sustain them. The manifesto also theorises the temporal dimension of the Third Landscape as inherently inconstant and Lamarckian — not evolving toward a fixed climax but continuously adjusting, making it more resilient than constant (Darwinian) systems. The manifesto ends with a strong critique of heritage conservation as applied to natural spaces: fixing a Third Landscape fragment as a heritage object condemns it to disappearance, because its ecological value lies precisely in its freedom to evolve.

## Key Points

- **Three-part taxonomy**: Third Landscape = primary sets (virgin climax ecosystems) + neglected lands (abandoned from prior use) + reserves (administratively protected). Managed spaces (agriculture, city, tourism) drive out diversity. Unmanaged spaces host it.
- **Political analogy**: Third Landscape = Third State (Sieyès 1789). "Space expressing neither power nor submission to power." Indecision as positive political category.
- **Planetary Garden**: The planet understood as a garden; ecological finiteness = the enclosure ("Garten" etymologically = enclosure). The biosphere is the garden's wall.
- **Urban mesh and vacuoles**: Urbanisation creates mesh; closed meshes cut biological continuity; open meshes sustain biological corridors ("vacuoles" = pockets of Third Landscape within urban fabric).
- **Biological corridors**: Linear fragments of Third Landscape — hedges, field edges, roadsides, riverbanks — connecting larger reserves. Edges and interfaces are themselves richer in species than interior zones.
- **Against heritage fixation**: "The fixation of the model set up as heritage condemns the Third Landscape to its own disappearance." Ecological value resides in freedom to evolve, incompatible with heritage stasis.
- **Lamarckian vs. Darwinian evolution**: Third Landscape favours Lamarckian (inconstant, transformative, recovers without hiatus) over Darwinian (constant, leads to collapse under environmental pressure). Inconstancy = resilience.
- **Demography as taboo**: Diversity is directly linked to human population; Clément names demography as the unspoken constraint underlying all Third Landscape dynamics.
- **Non-doing as political principle**: "Instruct the spirit of non-doing as one instructs the spirit of doing." Non-spatial organisation as a vital design and policy principle.
- **Fragmentation reduces diversity**: Fragmented Third Landscape hosts fewer species than contiguous Third Landscape of the same total area. Urban sprawl increases fragmentation while increasing total number of fragments.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Clément's explicit equation of Third Landscape with Lamarckian evolution is a controversial scientific claim — Lamarckism as a mechanism has been largely rejected in favour of neo-Darwinian synthesis; Clément uses it as an analogy more than a literal biological claim.
- The manifesto's position on reserves is paradoxical: reserves are Third Landscape, but reserve status (heritage protection) can "fix" the landscape and destroy its ecological value. Clément navigates this by distinguishing administrative delineation (acceptable) from prescriptive fixation (destructive).
- Demography is named as the central taboo subject but not developed — the manifesto stops at naming it rather than proposing demographic policy.
- The 2022 edition is a TEH reprint/translation (Michele Bee and Raphaël Fèvre, approved by author) — the text appears unchanged from the 2004 original; no updated commentary or postface accounts for two decades of subsequent urbanism discourse.
