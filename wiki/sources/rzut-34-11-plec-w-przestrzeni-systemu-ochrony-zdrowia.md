## Summary

Essay by architect Marta Czachorowska (m+design) based on 11 years of observation (2011–2022) in voivodeship hospitals in Warsaw and Łódź regions, examining how biological and social gender shape medical space design. The text distinguishes gender-neutral, co-educational, and gender-specific hospital wards; documents the feminisation of care spaces (oncology, maternity) through personalisation, rituals, and communal activities; and argues that "soft" spatial solutions from women's wards should be transferred to men's wards and gender-neutral departments as empathetic, inclusive practice.

**PL:** Esej architektki Marty Czachorowskiej (m+design) oparty na 11 latach obserwacji (2011–2022) w szpitalach wojewódzkich w Warszawie i regionie łódzkim, analizujący wpływ płci biologicznej i społecznej na projektowanie przestrzeni medycznych. Tekst rozróżnia oddziały neutralne płciowo, koedukacyjne i dedykowane konkretnej płci; dokumentuje feminizację przestrzeni opieki (onkologia, położnictwo) poprzez personalizację, rytuały i aktywności zbiorowe; oraz argumentuje, że "miękkie" rozwiązania przestrzenne z oddziałów kobiecych powinny być przenoszone na oddziały męskie i neutralne płciowo jako empatyczna, inkluzywna praktyka.

## Key Points

- **Gender-neutral medical spaces**: High-specialist, expensive-equipment departments with "ad hoc" care — PET, diagnostic imaging (MR, CT, X-ray, ultrasound), operating theatres, ER. Exceptions: angiography (mainly male) and mammography (female).
- **Co-educational wards**: General internal medicine, outpatient clinics, dental, rehabilitation — basic care without sex-specific therapy.
- **Gender-specific wards**: Gynaecological oncology (female), prostate/testicular oncology (male), cardiology and stroke (male majority), endocrinology (sex-specific hormones), maternity/gynaecology (female), trauma (male majority).
- **Yentl syndrome**: Historical "sex-blind" treatment of patients led to life-threatening misdiagnoses and inappropriate therapies for women. The term comes from the 1983 film where Barbra Streisand plays a Polish Jewish woman disguised as a man to get education. Caroline Criado Perez documents this extensively in *Invisible Women*.
- **Transgender healthcare barriers**: Avoidance or refusal of care, problems obtaining referrals, lack of information from doctors, uncomfortable interpersonal interactions. Trans people use all areas of healthcare and are often not immediately recognised as trans by medical staff.
- **Feminisation of women's wards**: Observed at ICZMP Łódź (maternity) and WWCOiT im. M. Kopernika Łódź (oncology) — personalisation through hanging pictures, photos of babies born there, flowers at nurse stations. Patients on women's wards show greater readiness for non-medical activities (theatre, dance, photo sessions for breast-cancer survivors — "amazons"). This requires universal, easily rearrangeable multi-purpose spaces — often basements, former hospital chapels, or corridor fragments.
- **Spatial analogies**: Women's behaviour in the "medical town" mirrors their urban navigation — hairdresser, office, shop, nursery, after-school activities. They explore the hospital more, use non-medical services (waiting rooms, halls, hairdressers, wig shops, hospital cafés).
- **Rituals**: Celebration of chemotherapy completion by striking a "victory gong" — symbolic start of new life. Clubs of "amazons" enrich the hospital's functional layout.
- **Men's wards**: Lack the communal and personalised spaces characteristic of women's wards. The question arises whether this is tied to women's social role as performers of domestic rituals, or whether it simply reflects that stereotypical gender roles are a superficial key to designing care spaces that exclude men from well-designed spaces of care.
- **Hospital cafés**: Once stereotypically seen as "female" spaces for long coffee conversations, their function has been expanded to become hospital common rooms for both sexes — neutral zones for non-hierarchical meetings between patients, families, and doctors, and for interdisciplinary medical exchange. Design should include diverse seating: multi-station tables, individual seats at varying heights, sofas, standing places.
- **Transferable solutions**: Many solutions from women's wards can be implemented on men's wards — communal activity rooms, individualisation opportunities, transition ceremonies (e.g. 1 m² "farewell space" for symbolic departure from the ward). Post-mortem rooms on men's wards could be modelled on newborn farewell rooms, with wooden cladding and family seating.
- **Maggie's Centres**: Charles Jencks's *The Architecture of Hope* describes buildings offering emotional support, art therapy, drama therapy, movement therapy, and horticultural therapy.
- **Architectural elements supporting soft competencies**: Nurse station as social hub; ward kitchen for informal tea interactions; bathrooms with seats, bidets, grab bars, rain showers, mobile bath tubs for body care in cosmetic not medical context; intra-ward rehabilitation rooms for favourite activities and mirror-neuron exercises; empathetic newborn farewell rooms without religious symbols, for psychologist conversations and trauma processing within medical space.
- **Conclusion**: Feminist architecture of care does not cure, but can support medical staff. Appropriately designed, friendly, "soft", "female" spaces can encourage patients to seek diagnosis and rehabilitation. These solutions should gradually transfer from women's to men's wards.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- The author initially assumed biological sex would most shape medical space, but found social/cultural gender (gender) most expressive in spatial arrangement. This raises questions about how far biological sex-specific needs (e.g. different disease profiles, body sizes) are currently under-addressed in hospital design.
- The transfer of "feminine" spatial solutions to men's wards is proposed as universally beneficial, but could it be read as imposing a feminised norm on male patients who may resist it? The author does not address potential patient resistance.
- The observation that one-third of WSM Żoliborz residents employed servants (from article 07, contextually relevant) suggests class dynamics may override gender innovations in spatial design — a tension not explicitly discussed for hospital spaces but potentially relevant (private rooms vs. communal wards).
