## Summary

The East Beach Café in Littlehampton, West Sussex is a small commercial building by [[Heatherwick Studio]], commissioned by two local residents — Jane Wood and Sophie Murray — to replace a seafront chip-and-burger kiosk and help revitalize a declining British seaside town. The text, drawn directly from Heatherwick Studio's own website and reproduced on Divisare, is a first-person account of the design problem and process. It is promotional in tone but unusually explicit about design reasoning, making it a useful document of the studio's method.

The design was generated almost entirely by a single functional constraint: the need to protect forty metres of sea-facing glass with roller shutters. Rather than treating the shutters as appendages, Heatherwick wrapped the building in long undulating ribbons of steel at shutter-box width, forming the entire roof and wall structure. The angled geometry of the ribbons across the building gives articulation to the rear façade — necessarily windowless, facing the sewage line. The entire structure was fabricated locally by two craftsmen at Littlehampton Welding.

The source is notable for its explicit rejection of the conventional British seaside aesthetic — the Art Deco steamship, the white-sailed yacht — in favour of something that engages more honestly with the actual British beach experience: damp shingle, found objects, eroded forms. Heatherwick wanted the building to "sit in the shingle like any other interesting seaside object." This places the project in a tradition of architecture that derives its formal language from found and eroded natural objects rather than from historical building types.

Projekt powstał niemal w całości z jednego ograniczenia funkcjonalnego: potrzeby ochrony czterdziestu metrów szklanych powierzchni skierowanych na morze roletami. Budynek miał „siedzieć w żwirze jak każdy inny interesujący nadmorski obiekt."

## Key Points

- **Site**: 40-metre-long cigarette-shaped strip, Littlehampton; bounded front by promenade, rear by high-pressure sewage line that cannot be built over
- **Client**: Jane Wood and Sophie Murray — two private local residents (not a developer or institution)
- **Design generator**: roller shutters → entire building wrapped in undulating steel shutter-box ribbons that form roof and walls
- **Dual challenge**: maximise sea views (full glazing, sea-facing) while creating an articulated rear with no windows — solved by the same ribbon geometry
- **Material**: raw weathered steel exterior; interior sprayed with rigid insulating foam
- **Fabrication**: entire steel structure fabricated in sections by Littlehampton Welding (two men, local)
- **Aesthetic position**: explicitly rejects Art Deco/nautical pastiche; building should read as an eroded found object on the beach
- **Urban context**: British seaside town decline post-1970s; café as part of Littlehampton regeneration effort; now draws visitors from across England

**PL:**
- **Lokalizacja**: 40-metrowy pas kształtu papierosa, Littlehampton; od przodu promenada, od tyłu rurociąg ściekowy wysokiego ciśnienia, nad którym nie można budować
- **Klient**: Jane Wood i Sophie Murray — dwie prywatne lokalne mieszkanki (nie deweloper ani instytucja)
- **Generator projektu**: rolety → cały budynek owinięty falistymi wstęgami stalowych skrzynek roletowych tworzącymi dach i ściany
- **Podwójne wyzwanie**: maksymalizacja widoków na morze (pełne przeszklenie od frontu) + artykułowana tylna część bez okien — rozwiązane tą samą geometrią wstęg
- **Materiał**: surowa stal kortenowska na zewnątrz; wnętrze natryskiwane sztywną pianką izolacyjną
- **Wykonawstwo**: cała stalowa konstrukcja wykonana w sekcjach przez Littlehampton Welding (dwóch mężczyzn, lokalnie)
- **Pozycja estetyczna**: odrzuca pastisz art deco/morski; budynek powinien czytać się jak erodowany znaleziony obiekt na plaży
- **Kontekst miejski**: upadek brytyjskiego miasteczka nadmorskiego po 1970; kawiarnia jako część rewitalizacji Littlehampton; przyciąga dziś odwiedzających z całej Anglii

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Source is Heatherwick Studio's own description — entirely promotional; no independent critical assessment of whether the building performs as claimed or how the "eroded object" reading reads in person.
- Raw weathered steel on a marine seafront is a borderline case for the material: salt air can prevent the stable protective patina from forming correctly, leading to ongoing corrosion rather than stabilisation. Source does not address this.
- Date of completion not stated in source. (From external knowledge: c. 2007 — not confirmed here.)
- Local fabrication by two men is an attractive narrative but gives no structural or specification detail.

- Źródło to własny opis Heatherwick Studio — całkowicie promocyjne; brak niezależnej oceny krytycznej, czy budynek funkcjonuje zgodnie z zapowiedziami ani jak odczytanie „erodowanego obiektu" wygląda w rzeczywistości.

## Quotes

> "The convention for modern seaside architecture is to evoke white-sailed yachts and a bygone era of Art Deco steamships but, to us, the British seaside did not conjure up images of twinkling sea, golden sand and blue skies. Our associations were with stumbling around on damp brown shingle, spotting the magically eroded objects that the sea has offered up."

> "It made us wonder how to make a connection with the texture and richness of a British beach and whether this building could sit in the shingle like any other interesting seaside object."

> "The design was led by the roller shutters that the building would need to protect its windows. Rather than sticking them on like eyelids, we began to think about making the whole building out of its steel shutter boxes."

> "The entire metal structure was fabricated in sections by Littlehampton Welding by just two men."
