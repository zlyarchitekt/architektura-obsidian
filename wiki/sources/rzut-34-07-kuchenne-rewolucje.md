## Summary
An essay tracing the kitchen as a gendered political space across the 20th century. Zofia Piotrowska shows how the kitchen disappeared from public discourse with industrialisation, becoming a privatised, feminine zone even though professional cooking had historically been male. The text moves through several key episodes: Christine Frederick's taylorist "New Housekeeping" (1913) which measured women's movements with a thread to optimise appliance layout; the Frankfurt Kitchen by Margarete Schütte-Lihotzky (1926) — a 6.5 m² "Mercedes among kitchens" that standardised fitted cabinetry but still imprisoned women in domestic labour; Erna Meyer's training kitchens and feminist programme of intellectualising housework; Barbara Brukalska's radically open kitchen annex for WSM Żoliborz (1927) which tried to reintegrate cooking into family life but was later walled off because one-third of residents still employed servants; and the Soviet Narkomfin (Ginzburg + Milinis, 1930) which abolished private kitchens entirely in favour of a communal canteen — only to have miniature kitchens forcibly inserted by Stalinist backlash. The essay concludes that only the gradual, unforced return of Brukalska's annex idea — evolving into the kitchen island and the open-plan living-cooking-dining zone — finally dissolved the kitchen's isolation, turning food preparation from domestic labour into social pleasure.

## Key Points
- **Kitchen as political battlefield**: The 1959 "Kitchen Debate" between Nixon and Khrushchev made the kitchen a Cold War propaganda stage, but the deeper struggle was over who performed unpaid reproductive labour.
- **Taylorism at home**: Christine Frederick applied factory motion-study (thread-tracing women's paths) to domestic space. The kitchen was designed to female hand-scale; the living room remained male-comfort territory.
- **Frankfurt Kitchen (1926)**: Margarete Schütte-Lihotzky's 6.5 m² fitted kitchen was a design masterpiece and a propaganda tool for bourgeois prosperity, rolled out in 10,000 Frankfurt units. Yet it locked the user into a single efficient circuit of domestic work.
- **Erna Meyer's feminist kitchens**: Meyer's 1926 Stuttgart exhibition featured training kitchens where women learned to use new appliances. Her programme intellectualised housework, treating it as equivalent to business management.
- **Brukalska's open annex (1927)**: For WSM Żoliborz, Barbara Brukalska designed an open kitchen annex flowing into the living room — a social revolution that treated cooking as part of family life, not isolated laboratory work. Only luxury was a ventilated food-storage cabinet (budget precursor to the refrigerator). The idea was later compromised by doors and walls because residents still employed cooks.
- **Narkomfin (1930)**: Moisei Ginzburg and Ignaty Milinis eliminated private kitchens entirely, replacing them with a communal canteen. Stalinist backlash forced miniature kitchens into the unadapted flats, reversing the experiment.
- **The slow evolution**: Technological progress (refrigerators) changed habits but not spatial layouts. Only the late-20th-century return of Brukalska's annex — via the kitchen island into the open-plan living-cooking-dining zone — finally dissolved the kitchen's gendered isolation.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- If the Frankfurt Kitchen was hailed by contemporaries as a feminist breakthrough (someone finally paid attention to women's needs), was it emancipation or a more efficient cage?
- Brukalska's open annex failed in practice because social reality (domestic servants) outran architectural ideology. Does this prove that spatial design cannot precede social structure, or simply that the WSM Żoliborz community was not the intended user?
- Narkomfin's kitchen abolition was reversed by political decree. What does this say about the autonomy of architectural experimentation under authoritarian regimes?
