## Summary

Article 9 of RZUT-33 is an essay by Magdalena Krzoska-Hołody presenting original research — a 2022 pilot participatory-observation study of Warsaw cultural institutions' activities in green spaces. The article argues that cultural institutions and NGOs collectively form **sieci kultury** (cultural networks) that have become an increasingly important actor in mapping, valuing, and ultimately shaping the city's [[Blue-Green Infrastructure]].

The core thesis is that cultural institutions, initially confined to their designated premises, have been expanding into urban public space — parks, wildflower meadows, community gardens, railway edges, brownfields, forgotten squares. This "going around the corner" (*wychodzenie za róg*) is not incidental: it reflects a structural transformation in the role of culture in the city. In recent years, cultural institutions have increasingly taken on the role of ecological educators — a function previously held by specialist institutions (zoos, botanical gardens, research institutes). By telling and co-creating the environmental history of their neighbourhoods, cultural networks become a source of ground-level intelligence about BGI that top-down planning cannot easily replicate.

Krzoska-Hołody conducted participatory observation of 20 events in Warsaw in 2022 — 10 in Targówek and Praga Północ, 10 across other city districts. She analysed nature walks led by expert guests, community gardens, creative projects, movement and mindfulness practices, and foraging activities. Locations included historic parks, forests, urban parks, community gardens, a historic cemetery, Vistula riverbanks, railway edges, re-wilded urban squares, and urban wastelands. From this fieldwork she develops a **typology of cultural nature events** in Warsaw's sieci kultury.

A recurring observation is the unexpected emotional and cognitive value of **undefined, "architecture-free" spaces**: railway margins, roadsides, forgotten greenlets, overgrown brownfields. These "spaces of discomfort" — transit zones without obvious landscape value — were experienced by participants with great generosity and often particular sentiment, especially by older residents who recalled them as childhood play spaces. The desire to preserve their wild character while removing rubbish and improving safety and paths was a consistent pattern, with formal park amenities explicitly rejected as unnecessary. This resonates with Clément's [[Third Landscape]] concept and his critique of heritage-fixation (turning neglected land into designed park destroys the value).

The article also discusses the loss and restoration of **embodied and collective knowledge**: tacit skills — identifying wild plants, composting, urban beekeeping, observing bird behaviour — that were marginalized by a knowledge economy that favours easily digitizable, marketable information. Cultural events create spaces where this knowledge circulates across generations. The author cites Andrzej W. Nowak's concept of "lost knowledge" (*wiedza utracona*): when a skill vanishes, an entire "horizon of meaning" vanishes with it. Cultural networks are restoring this horizon, one community garden and foraging walk at a time.

The case study of the **Łąki Golędzinowskie** protests (late 2022) illustrates how cultural-network-shaped citizen attitudes generate concrete planning resistance. When Warsaw city authorities announced plans to convert the wild Vistula-bank meadows into a "natural urban park," residents — many of whom had developed relationships with the site through cultural activities — organized resistance, insisting on preserving the existing wild character rather than formalizing it.

## Key Points

- **Sieci kultury as planning actors**: cultural networks (institutions + NGOs) can participate in spatial planning as partners with a ground-level view of resident preferences and actual spatial dynamics — a legitimately deliberative source of knowledge distinct from formal surveys.
- **Wild > designed**: participants in cultural-nature events consistently preferred "spaces of discomfort" (transit zones, brownfields, railway edges) over manicured park infrastructure. Formal amenities were often actively rejected.
- **Naming as empowerment**: participants who learned the professional name of a plant, animal, or fungus subsequently photographed it, noted it, and sought more information — naming is a cognitive act of engagement that formal ecology education leverages but community events democratize.
- **Embodied knowledge gap**: tacit skills (wild-plant identification, composting, urban beekeeping, permaculture, bird-watching) are being lost not just as skills but as entire meaning-horizons (Nowak). Cultural events are the primary vehicle for their intergenerational restoration.
- **Ecological references in cultural events**: participants cited Arne Naess's deep ecology, James Lovelock (Earth as living subject), Peter Wohleben (forest intelligence), and Piet Oudolf (garden naturalism) — indicating that ecological philosophy is entering urban cultural programming.
- **Succession failure**: the main weakness of cultural-network initiatives is lack of succession; when founders leave, gardens and projects collapse. Long-term commitment is the difference between ephemeral activation and lasting infrastructure.
- **Łąki Golędzinowskie**: the wildflower meadows on the right bank of the Vistula (between Grota-Roweckiego and Gdański bridges) became a protest site when city authorities announced a "natural urban park" conversion in late 2022; culturally-engaged residents resisted formalization, wanting wild character preserved with minimal intervention.
- **Free access as structural requirement**: almost all analysed events were open and free; the absence of free communal spaces is itself identified as a problem of contemporary cities.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The article's main tension is between *leaving wild spaces alone* and *activating communities around them*: the very cultural programs that build attachment to an undefined space also bring it to attention, potentially accelerating its formalization or development.
- The Łąki Golędzinowskie protest is presented as a success story of cultural-network influence; the long-term outcome of that planning dispute is unresolved in the text (events of late 2022).
- Krzoska-Hołody's typology of events is observational, not normative: it describes what Warsaw's cultural networks are doing, not what they should do. A prescriptive follow-up — how to integrate sieci kultury formally into BGI planning processes — is an open question.
- The paper's scope (Warsaw, 2022) may not generalise: in smaller Polish cities, cultural institutions may lack the critical mass to constitute a functional planning-relevant network.
