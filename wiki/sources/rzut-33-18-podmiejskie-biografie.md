## Summary

*Podmiejskie Biografie* is an interview conducted by RZUT editor Zofia Piotrowska with Katarzyna Kajdanek, Polish urban and community sociologist whose research focuses on suburbanisation. Kajdanek is the author of *Powrotnicy* (*The Returners*), a study of people who moved back from suburbs to cities after discovering the suburban dream had failed to deliver.

The interview opens with a linguistic disambiguation: *suburbanizacja* (suburbanisation) denotes the *process*, while *przedmieścia* (suburbs) denotes the *result* — but "przedmieście" also refers historically to areas just outside old city walls, now often within administrative boundaries (e.g. Przedmieście Świdnickie in Wrocław). Contemporary Polish suburbia should be called *suburbia* — monofunctional, primarily residential spaces, often without basic infrastructure. A further Polish statistical problem: the country has no category between "city" and "village"; functional urban areas, widely used elsewhere in Europe, do not exist in Polish public statistics.

Kajdanek identifies two phases of Polish suburbanisation. The first, during socialism: state-directed urbanisation had its own logic, but parallel processes included second homes as party-activist perks, aspiration homes for the better-off, and periurban settlement by rural migrants denied *meldowanie* (official city residence). The second phase, from the late 1990s: the first mortgages, the developer market, affordable land in outer zones, all large Polish cities following the liberal American-style sprawl model simultaneously — not because it was inevitable (German cities managed to stop it), but because they lacked the political will or planning capacity to prevent it.

The cultural logic of the detached house is crucial. Kajdanek argues that it was partly a genuine cultural value — the house as assertion of autonomy, separation from the unwanted social mix of socialist blocks, a family achievement ratified by parents and in-laws. But this cultural dimension was superimposed on an economic compulsion: the state abdicated responsibility for housing policy, leaving the market to produce only unaffordable city housing and affordable suburban land. "Urban sprawl became, in existing political conditions, virtually the only response to the very painful fact of the state's abdication from shaping housing policy." The house as compulsion, not just as choice.

The book *Powrotnicy* tracks the suburban life-cycle. The initial experience is sometimes euphoric — the satisfaction of having managed the bank, the builder, the entire difficult process. But a growing dissonance builds between the dream (space, nature, community) and the reality (commute time, infrastructure failure, boredom, social incompatibility). The dissonance sharpens in crises: a child's asthma from poor air quality; value clashes in an intensely religious neighbourhood; a partner's death revealing the physical and financial burden of an unmaintainable house. The essay invents a figure: the *podmiejski nomad* — the suburban teenager who, denied urban life by geography and parental logistics, builds an elaborate daily survival system (planning departure and return times, packing a full life in a backpack, sleeping and studying on suburban trains, developing social networks across the city to avoid going home).

The interview notes that Poland's air quality inversion has proceeded: suburbs, once marketed as "clean countryside," now have worse air than city centres (thanks to urban heating system upgrades and anti-smog programs). Shopping centres have become a substitute public space for suburban teenagers — "a relative safe, well-lit waiting room, a form of day-care centre." Private schools are selected not only for educational quality but as the only institutions aligning school hours with working parents' schedules.

## Key Points

- *Suburbanisation* (suburbanizacja) = process; *suburbs/suburbia* (przedmieścia/suburbia) = result; Polish "przedmieście" also has a historical meaning (extra-mural settlement, now often within city boundaries).
- Polish statistics have no category between "city" and "village" — functional urban areas (used in most European countries) don't exist in Polish public statistics.
- Two phases of Polish suburbanisation: (1) Socialist — state urbanisation + parallel processes (party dachas, periurban migrants without *meldowanie*); (2) Liberal from late 1990s — first mortgages, developer market, liberal American-style sprawl, all major cities simultaneously.
- Suburbanisation was not inevitable: nearby German cities managed to prevent it with political will and planning capacity.
- The detached house carries cultural weight — rejection of socialist block-living, assertion of autonomy, social aspiration ratified by family. But the deeper cause is economic: the state abdicated housing policy, making urban housing unaffordable.
- **Central argument**: "Urban sprawl became, in existing political conditions, virtually the only response — and one generated from below — to the painful fact of the state's abdication from shaping housing policy."
- High infrastructure cost: municipalities cannot afford the roads, pipes, cables, and internet for unlimited dispersed development; this is why suburban homes often still use wells, cesspits, and unreliable internet.
- Polish suburban specificity: development built on old village cores (not greenfield like American Levittown) because individual builders want proximity to existing water/sewage infrastructure; creates a specific "quasi-intercultural contact" between old rural communities and new suburban settlers.
- Life-cycle of the suburb (from *Powrotnicy*): initial euphoria (survived the bank, the builder, the process) → gradual dissonance (commute, boredom, infrastructure) → crisis (health, values, widowhood/divorce) → reurbanisation.
- **Podmiejski nomad** (*suburban nomad*): the teenager who builds an elaborate survival strategy — calculating departure/return times, packing a full life in a backpack, sleeping and studying on commuter trains, mapping urban social networks — to spend maximum time in the city and minimum time at home.
- Reurbanisation triggers (from *Powrotnicy* research): children's asthma and air quality; social incompatibility with hyper-religious or politically hostile neighbours; divorce or partner's death in old age revealing the unsustainability of maintaining the house alone.
- **Air quality inversion**: cities that were dirty are now (after heating reforms, anti-smog programs) cleaner than their suburban surroundings. The "clean countryside" marketing premise of suburban development is now frequently false.
- **Shopping centres as substitute public space**: suburban teenagers use malls as safe, lit, warm waiting rooms — a form of youth day-care. Private schools function similarly: chosen not just for educational quality but for matching parents' work hours.
- **Minister Uściński anecdote**: in Piotrowska's interview with the minister, all staff present lived in communes outside Warsaw — illustrating how the suburb-preference of decision-makers shapes housing policy.
- The book excised a passage about suburban "galerianki" — a socially loaded term (previously associated with teenage girls selling sexual services); Kajdanek's use referred to teenagers spending time in malls out of necessity, not choice; excised for fear of misreading.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The cultural vs. economic causation of suburbanisation: Kajdanek avoids condemning single-family housing as a way of life, but insists the systemic cause is state abdication not cultural preference. This is compatible with politicians (like Uściński) who claim the house is a legitimate housing aspiration.
- The *Powrotnicy* research describes the suburban experience primarily through the lens of eventual returnees — those who stayed in the suburbs are systematically absent from the study. Kajdanek acknowledges this sampling constraint.
- The "urban nomad" phenomenon raises questions about whose responsibility it is: the parent who made the move (Kajdanek explicitly defends parents, saying the anger should become political); the planning system that generated no alternatives; or the children themselves, who developed extraordinary resilience.
