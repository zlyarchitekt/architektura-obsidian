## Summary

Comprehensive Wikipedia biography covering [[Le Corbusier]]'s full arc from his 1887 birth in La Chaux-de-Fonds, Switzerland to his 1965 death at Roquebrune-Cap-Martin. The article documents his self-education, his formative stints under Auguste Perret (reinforced concrete) and Peter Behrens (alongside Mies van der Rohe and Walter Gropius), and the development of his signature theoretical positions: Purism, the Five Points of Architecture, the Plan Voisin, Ville Radieuse, and the Modulor proportional system. Key built works are described in detail: Villa Savoye (1928–1931), Unité d'Habitation (1947–1952), Notre-Dame du Haut at Ronchamp (1950–1954), and the Chandigarh governmental sector (1950s). 17 of his buildings in 7 countries were inscribed as UNESCO World Heritage Sites in 2016.

Artykuł dokumentuje samokształcenie, praktyki u Perreta i Behrensa, i rozwój kluczowych pozycji teoretycznych: Puryzmu, Pięciu Punktów Architektury, Plan Voisin, Ville Radieuse i Modulora. 17 jego budynków w 7 krajach zostało wpisanych na listę UNESCO w 2016 r.

The source is also the fullest single account of Le Corbusier's controversies. In the 1920s he co-founded and contributed to the French fascist journals *Plans*, *Prélude*, and *L'Homme Réel*, and had connections to *Le Faisceau* (a short-lived fascist party). During World War II he actively sought commissions from the Vichy regime and was appointed by eugenicist surgeon Alexis Carrel to a Vichy eugenics committee. He wrote to his mother in October 1940: "The Jews are having a bad time. I occasionally feel sorry. But it appears their blind lust for money has rotted the country." The gap between his rhetorical humanism — *soleil, espace, verdure* — and these political positions is one of the central unresolved tensions in his legacy.

## Key Points

- Born 6 October 1887, La Chaux-de-Fonds, Switzerland; self-taught; trained under Auguste Perret (reinforced concrete) and Peter Behrens (alongside Mies van der Rohe and Gropius).
- Founded **Purism** (1918) with Amédée Ozenfant; manifesto *Après le Cubisme*; journal *L'Esprit Nouveau* (1920); pseudonym adopted 1920.
- **Dom-Ino House** (1914–15): patented structural system of three concrete slabs on six columns + staircase; separates structure from walls; precursor to the Five Points.
- **Five Points of Architecture** (1927): pilotis, roof terrace (*toit jardin*), free plan (*plan libre*), ribbon window (*fenêtre en longueur*), free façade (*façade libre*).
- **Promenade architecturale**: architecture to be experienced in movement through ramps and sequence, not static viewpoints; perfected at Villa Savoye.
- **Plan Voisin** (1925): proposal to bulldoze central Paris north of the Seine and replace with 60-storey cruciform towers in a park grid; never realised but decisive in triggering debates about urban renewal.
- **Ville Radieuse** (1935): revised utopian city; housing assigned by family size (not class) on pilotis in parkland; became the model for postwar European public housing.
- **CIAM** (*Congrès International d'Architecture Moderne*): co-founded 1928 at Château Lake Leman; founding members included Gropius, Mies, Perret, Aalto, Sert; **Athens Charter** (1943, drafted on a cruise ship Athens–Marseille) became the master document of postwar city planning.
- **Modulor** (introduced c.1927–1943): proportional system based on human figure, golden ratio, and Fibonacci numbers; applied to Villa Stein at Garches (1927) and Unité d'Habitation; intended to mediate metric/imperial systems.
- **Open Hand** (*La Main Ouverte*): recurring symbol; largest version at Chandigarh (26 m high) — "open to give and open to receive."
- **Awards**: AIA Gold Medal 1961; Légion d'honneur (Chevalier 1937, Grand Officier 1964); honorary degree, Cambridge 1959; no Pritzker (established 1979, after his death).
- **UNESCO 2016**: 17 buildings in 7 countries — Villa Savoye, Unité d'Habitation, Chandigarh Capitol Complex, Ronchamp, etc.
- **Controversies**: fascist journals 1920s; Le Faisceau connections 1925–28; Vichy commissions sought; eugenics committee 1942; antisemitic letter Oct 1940; proposed redesign of Algiers "after its Jewish population had been forcefully removed"; Jane Jacobs (*The Death and Life of Great American Cities*) is the most influential critical voice.
- **Witold Rybczynski** (1998, *Time*): "authoritarian, inflexible and simplistic... wherever it was tried—in Chandigarh by Le Corbusier himself or in Brasilia by his followers—it failed."

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The Wikipedia source confirms specific details of Le Corbusier's fascist associations that the current entity page describes vaguely: *Plans*, *Prélude*, *L'Homme Réel* journals named; Le Faisceau connections 1925–28; Vichy eugenics committee via Alexis Carrel; verbatim antisemitic letter Oct 1940.
- The claim in the current entity page that Le Corbusier "received the RIBA Gold Medal (1953)" is not confirmed by this Wikipedia article, which only lists the AIA Gold Medal (1961) under Awards — possible error to investigate.
- Jane Jacobs as a critical voice is mentioned but her full counter-argument (*The Death and Life of Great American Cities*, 1961) deserves its own source ingest given its importance for the [[City]] concept.
- The relation between Le Corbusier's urbanism and Polish postwar housing (Pruitt–Igoe is American, but the tower-in-a-park model was extensively applied in Poland) is an open research thread connecting this entity to [[Spatial Planning (Poland)]].
