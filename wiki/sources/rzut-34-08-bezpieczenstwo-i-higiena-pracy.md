## Summary
An architectural-urbanist essay by Iga Mazur and Marta Wróblewska examining sex work through the lens of spatial design and occupational safety. The authors — explicitly identifying as pro-sex feminists and architects, not activists or sex workers themselves — survey a wide typology of sex-work spaces across Europe: the Zurich sex drive-in (2013), Amsterdam's window brothels, Basel's Laufhaus and Toleranzzone, Cologne's Pascha tower, Antwerp's Villa Tinto, and the proposed Moke Architecten erotic centre for Amsterdam. The text documents how each spatial model produces distinct safety conditions: windows with inter-door alarms, drive-ins with emergency buttons, clubs with bouncer protocols, and private escorting with spatial surveillance (mirrors, monitored entrances). A central tension runs through the essay: criminalisation (Polish abolitionism) removes labour rights and makes workers invisible to planners, while legalisation (Switzerland, Netherlands) creates safety infrastructure but also stigma through special permits and medical surveillance. The Amsterdam case study is the most detailed: mayor Femke Halsema's four scenarios for the red-light district (curtains, fewer windows, more windows, or total relocation to a suburban high-rise) reveal a conflict between resident quality-of-life, tourism, and worker safety. The authors note that architects rarely admit to designing sex spaces publicly, and that workers themselves are seldom consulted in planning consultations.

## Key Points
- **Zurich sex drive-in (2013)**: Wooden LED-lit shelters with safety buttons, driver-door lock design. Substituted street prostitution after a city referendum. No sex worker was consulted in the design; no architect publicly credited.
- **Terminology as politics**: The authors adopt "sex worker" (pracownik/pracownica seksualna) over "prostitute" as an act of recognising labour dignity, following Juno Mac and Molly Smith.
- **Feminist split**: Abolitionist feminism treats sex work as patriarchal violence; pro-sex feminism treats it as labour needing rights and safe working conditions. Polish left parties largely align with abolitionism.
- **Legal models**: Legalisation (CH, NL, DE) → special permits, medical surveillance, stigmatising residency rules. Decriminalisation (BE, 2022) → sex work treated as ordinary service labour. Abolitionism (PL, CZ, IT, ES) → selling sex not punished, but related activities (pimping, organising) criminalised; workers have no labour rights.
- **Amsterdam window brothels**: ~350 ground-floor windows. Alarm buttons summon police and alert neighbouring workers; interconnecting doors allow mutual aid. Tourist nuisance (non-paying gawkers) is a major workplace problem. 2020 ban on guided tours.
- **Mayor Halsema's four scenarios**: (1) curtains — reduces visibility and therefore demand; (2) fewer windows + new workplace elsewhere — forces landlords to choose who stays; (3) more windows to spread tourists; (4) total relocation to suburban erotic centre. The authors note that isolation increases danger for workers.
- **Architectural shame**: Architects who design sex spaces rarely publicise the work. Moke Architecten's Amsterdam tower (5,000 m² with looping corridors) is a rare public commission, yet workers were not consulted.
- **Spatial safety devices**: Mirrors on walls and ceilings (full situational awareness), monitored building entrances, second person behind a wall, emergency buttons, interconnecting doors, street-lighting design.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- Does spatial design (alarm buttons, interconnecting doors) genuinely improve safety, or does it merely manage risk within an inherently dangerous legal framework?
- The Zurich drive-in was designed without worker input; the Amsterdam erotic centre is stalled because residents reject visibility. Can sex-work space ever be co-designed with its users under conditions of stigma and criminalisation?
- If sex work is labour, should it be regulated like any other commercial activity (zoning, hygiene inspections, tax), or does the intimacy involved demand exceptional spatial and legal treatment?
