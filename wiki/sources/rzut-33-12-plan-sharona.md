## Summary

Urszula Prokop reconstructs the National Plan of Israel (1949–1952), designed by [[Arieh Sharon]] and a multidisciplinary team of 80–170 architects, urban planners, engineers, economists, and sociologists. The essay is both an architectural history and a critical political reading: the same plan that established the spatial structure of the modern Israeli state also served as an instrument of Palestinian displacement, ethnic sorting of Jewish immigrants, and military territory-consolidation.

The context is extraordinary: Israel declared independence on 14 May 1948 in the midst of the first Arab-Israeli war; approximately 759,000 Palestinians fled or were expelled; and roughly 700,000 Jewish immigrants from Asia and North Africa arrived within three years, doubling the Jewish population. As of 1948, 82% of the population lived in the coastal strip between Tel Aviv and Haifa. The Sharon Plan addressed this concentration with five interdependent pillars: **agriculture** (food self-sufficiency for 600,000 workers, 75% of national food demand); **industry** (decentralised from Tel Aviv to new peripheral towns); **roads and water** (infrastructure connecting the distributed settlement pattern); **landscape protection** (national parks, nature reserves, and the Hebraization of Arabic place names); and **New Towns** (*Nowe Miasta*) — 20 masterplans for communities of 20–50,000 residents, each combining garden city and kibbutz spatial models.

The New Towns were the plan's most controversial instrument. Modelled on European garden cities and filtered through the existing kibbutz tradition, they were designed as compact, functionally segregated settlements with service and administrative centres, 6–10,000-person residential clusters (each with a school and green space), car-free interiors, and industry on the periphery. The 750-metre maximum distance from home to all essential services anticipates later "15-minute city" thinking. The plan was implemented immediately and with extraordinary speed because: emergency "crisis regulations" bypassed parliamentary procedures; a special development fund (financed by donations, loans, and war reparations) could be spent without parliamentary approval; and ~90% of land was nationalised via the 1950 "Abandoned Property Act," which transferred land vacated by Palestinian refugees to the Israeli state.

The critical dimension of the essay is unflinching. New Towns housed primarily Mizrahi Jews (from the Middle East, North Africa, and Central Asia) in functionalist buildings they did not own and had not chosen — while Ashkenazi Jews settled with family in cities or joined existing kibbutzim. The modern, hygienic, collectivist settlements intended to produce social integration produced instead deeper ethnic stratification. Accumulated frustration became the social base for the Likud victory of 1977, which shifted Israel's spatial policy toward private homeownership ("build your own home") while *radicalising* the decentralisation logic into West Bank colonisation. The socialist ideology was stripped out; the territorial expansion mechanism remained.

## Key Points

- Sharon Plan (1949–1952): national spatial plan for Israel, directed by Arieh Sharon (Department of Planning, from 1949). Team: 80–170 specialists.
- Five pillars: agriculture, industry, roads/water, landscape protection, New Towns.
- New Towns: 20 masterplans; hybrid of garden city + kibbutz; service/admin centre + residential colonies (6–10k); car-free interior; industry on periphery; 750m max radius to essential services.
- Land confiscation: ~90% of Israel's territory nationalised; Abandoned Property Act (1950) transferred Palestinian refugee land to the state. The "making the desert bloom" slogan obscured that much of the claimed land was cultivated by Palestinian farmers (*fellaheen*).
- Decentralisation served military as well as planning goals: dispersed settlements = defensive positions; New Towns located near or on Palestinian village sites to balance ethnic composition and deter refugee return.
- Hebraization: Arabic place names systematically replaced with Hebrew names claimed as "ancient origins."
- Implementation speed: emergency regulations bypassed parliamentary procedures; development fund bypassed parliamentary approval.
- Social outcome: Mizrahi Jews segregated in peripheral New Towns; Ashkenazi Jews in cities and kibbutzim. Modernist housing promoted integration but produced stratification.
- Political consequence: accumulated Mizrahi frustration → Likud victory 1977 → "build your own home" program (private homeownership) + radical continuation of territorial decentralisation as West Bank colonisation (stripped of socialist ideology, used for settler expansion).
- The plan was praised internationally (published in *The Town Planning Review* in 1952) but criticised internally for over-optimism and sacrificing quality for speed.
- Key published sources: Arieh Sharon, "Planning in Israel," *Town Planning Review* 1952; Sharon, *Kibbutz+Bauhaus*, 1975; Zvi Efrat, *The Object of Zionism*, Princeton 2014; Sharon Rotbard, *Białe miasto, czarne miasto* (Polish translation 2022).

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The essay focuses on the plan's design logic and its social outcomes without fully reconciling these: Sharon himself appears to have believed in the plan's egalitarian intent, while the outcome was segregation. The gap between planning intent and social reality is identified but not fully theorised.
- The relationship between the Sharon Plan's spatial logic and later Palestinian erasure is documented but raises questions about whether the plan was inherently colonial or was colonially instrumentalised.
- The 750m service radius in New Towns anticipates "15-minute city" thinking — but the article does not draw this connection explicitly. It would be productive to compare the New Towns model with contemporary compact-city proposals.

## Quotes

> "Plan Sharona realizował nie tylko utopijne wizje planistów, lecz także strategiczne cele rządzących."

(The Sharon Plan served not only the utopian visions of planners, but also the strategic goals of the governing authorities.)

> "Zmiana polityki była jedynie pozorna. Pomysł decentralizacji zabudowy jako metody zawłaszczania kolejnych terenów nie tylko nie został wycofany, lecz także się zradykalizował."

(The policy change was merely apparent. The idea of spatial decentralisation as a method of territorial appropriation was not abandoned — it was radicalised.)
