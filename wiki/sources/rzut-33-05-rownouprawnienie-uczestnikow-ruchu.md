## Summary

An interview with [[Bartosz Rozbiewski]], BAiPP deputy director for spatial policy, by Zofia Piotrowska. The article frames transport and mobility as an inseparable dimension of spatial planning — not a separate technical domain — reflecting a key organisational shift in BAiPP under [[Marlena Happach]].

The central argument: Warsaw's mobility transformation requires simultaneous action on three fronts — building high-capacity rail infrastructure (metro lines M3–M5, expanded rail network), redesigning streets for equal treatment of all users (the *inverted transport pyramid*, with the private car at the bottom), and reforming parking policy via dynamic, transit-quality-based ratios rather than fixed minimums.

The article is notable for naming Warsaw's mobility paradox: 800 cars per 1,000 residents (among the highest in European capitals), yet public transit remains heavily used — because 20–30% of registered vehicles are fleet cars that travel across Poland, not Warsaw. The policy aim is not to overcome a car culture but to build transit to the point where car ownership becomes unnecessary for daily life.

## Key Points

- **[[Inverted Transport Pyramid]]** (*odwrócona piramida transportowa*): pedestrian → bicycle → public transport → carpooling/carsharing → private car; this hierarchy now informs all street and planning decisions in the Studium.
- **80% rail access target**: by 2050, 80% of Warsaw residents within a 10-minute walk (1 km) of a tram, metro, or rail station; coverage across 17 of 18 city districts.
- **Metro strategy**: M3 (to Gocław, planned), M4 (highest projected ridership — more than M1), M5 (western section possibly surface light rail, not underground, given lower density); corridor selection based on peak-hour flows exceeding 5,000 passengers.
- **Rail integration**: Warsaw is unique as the only Polish municipality with its own rail operator (SKM); the rail node is underutilised; new underground cross-city line in concept development; Warsaw coordinates with PKP PLK.
- **Mobility merged with planning**: key organisational shift — BAiPP integrates mobility within spatial planning (previously separate municipal units), which catalysed changes on Marszałkowska and Aleje Jerozolimskie.
- **Street redesign**: *zielone aleje* (tree-lined avenues with cycling lanes and transit), *zielone wjazdy do miasta* (green city entry corridors), *ulice z parkami linearnymi* (linear park streets), *zielone pierścienie* (cycling-pedestrian green rings) — street typologies linking BZIW with mobility.
- **Parking reform**: ratios not fixed in Studium but set by presidential decree, dynamically adjusted by local transit-quality algorithms; equal treatment of bicycle parking required — covered, accessible from building exit, not hidden in cellars or attics.
- **Warsaw's car paradox**: 800 cars per 1,000 residents, yet transit heavily used; 20–30% of registered vehicles are fleet cars (Poland-wide, not Warsaw-local); even accounting for this, Warsaw significantly exceeds Amsterdam or Berlin.
- **Car-dependent peripheries**: post-transformation (1990s) piecemeal peripheral development created zones systemically dependent on cars; low density and dispersed layout make public transit economically unviable — named as a planning failure that the Studium cannot retroactively fix.
- **Developer reluctance**: minimum 1 parking space per flat remains a "psychological barrier" even at well-connected sites like Browary Warszawskie and Towarowa — status symbol dimension explicitly named.
- **Local mobility zones** (*strefy ruchu lokalnego*): access allowed, through-traffic excluded; planned for major district centres; requires street reconstruction to implement.
- **Transit value-capture gap** (Spanish model): in Spain, transit investment enables development without compulsory purchase — landowners surrender corridor land in exchange for the value uplift transit creates. This mechanism does not exist in Polish law, forcing the city to buy out land for every road or transit corridor.
- **"Planning chasing the rabbit"**: Poland's planning always reacts to what has already been built; proactive planning only possible on city-owned sites (brownfields, post-industrial lands).

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Polish law lacks transit value-capture mechanisms (Spanish barter model), yet the Studium's polycentric compact city vision depends on metro extensions driving densification in peripheral corridors — without value capture, who funds this?
- Developer reluctance to reduce parking below 1:1 even in central, transit-served locations directly contradicts the compact city logic — reveals the market-vs-plan tension the Studium names but cannot resolve.
- *Strefy ruchu lokalnego* are planned for district centres but require street reconstruction — no timeline or funding mechanism is specified.
- Rozbiewski himself does not own a car but was forced to buy a parking space with his flat, which he now rents out — an illustration of the structural mismatch between policy aspiration and market reality even for the Studium's own authors.
