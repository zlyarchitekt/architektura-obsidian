## Summary

A historical survey by [[Joanna Majczyk]] and [[Agnieszka Tomaszewicz]] (researchers at Politechnika Wrocławska) tracing 101 years of women's presence in Polish architecture — from the first two female graduates in 1922 to the contemporary landscape of structural gender inequality. The article's governing argument is that the **feminization of architecture as a field of study** has not translated into the **feminization of architectural power** — and that this gap has structural roots that repeat across every era regardless of formal equality provisions.

The opening historical moment is precisely dateable: on 8 October 1919, the Ministry of Religious Denominations and Public Enlightenment opened Politechnika Lwowska to women; Politechnika Warszawska had already been admitting women for a fourth year of studies. In 1922, [[Jadwiga Dobrzyńska]] (1898–1940) and [[Wanda Wierzbicka-Nettowa]] (1892–1975) became the first women in Poland to receive architecture diplomas. In the same year, the X International Congress of Architects convened in Brussels with "la femme-architecte" on the agenda — and concluded that "it is not necessary to officially recognize the woman-architect." The same year, the draft statute of the Polish Architects' Chamber included a clause that "an architect may be a citizen of the Polish State regardless of sex" — remarkable gender-neutral language, given that exactly two women were licensed at the time.

The article documents the interwar barriers with Barbara Brukalska's own testimony: university ostracism (nationalist fraternities removing women's drawing boards from studios), financial exploitation ("some think women should be paid less or nothing"), investor mistrust, higher standards demanded for equal work, and the persistent assumption that an architectural couple's success belonged to the husband alone. The most extreme case documented is that of **Stanisława Manikowska** (1907–1939), a city planning official at the Łódź municipal office who was driven from her post by systematic harassment; her letter to the city administration — preserved in the Łódź State Archive — is quoted as evidence that the institutional failure to protect her was deliberate and documented.

Post-war Poland brought formal equality declarations under socialism, and the creation of state design offices in 1949 (which removed the need to compete for commissions) genuinely helped women architects maintain continuous practice. The case study is Politechnika Wrocławska's Faculty of Architecture: from its founding in 1948, it admitted women and men equally; by 1981, over 700 women had graduated (~40%); by 1971, women outnumbered men for the first time — a trend that has continued to this day. But throughout 1948–1989, not a single woman served as dean, vice-dean, or department head. The first female dean was Prof. [[Elżbieta Trocka-Leszczyńska]] (from 1993). The first female professor at WA PWr was Barbara Stępniewska-Janowska (1996, 48 years after the faculty opened). At Wrocław's Miastoprojekt state studio in 1989, women constituted 59% of the workforce — yet none had served as director (12 men did) or chief engineer (8 men) in the period 1948–1981.

The contemporary data is stark: women constitute ~45% of licensed architects in IARP (below parity); the SARP Pałac Saski competition jury had a 2:10 female-to-male ratio; in 57 years (1966–2023), the prestigious SARP Honorowa Nagroda was awarded to 6 women and 68 men, with only 3 women receiving it for independent work (Jadwiga Grabowska-Hawrylak 1974, Halina Skibniewska 1978, [[Ewa Kuryłowicz]] 2021). Poland ranked 21st out of 27 EU states on the Gender Equality Index (2022).

## Key Points

- **1922 as founding date**: first two female architecture graduates in Poland — [[Jadwiga Dobrzyńska]] and [[Wanda Wierzbicka-Nettowa]]; simultaneously, international congress fails to recognise women architects.
- **Interwar barriers**: ostracism (boards removed from studios), financial exploitation, investor distrust, higher standards, attribution erasure in architectural couples.
- **Stanisława Manikowska** (1907–1939): extreme case of institutional violence; documented letter in Łódź State Archive.
- **Socialist formalism**: post-war formal equality declarations + state offices helped women maintain practice but did not produce leadership.
- **WA PWr statistics**: women majority since 1971; zero female deans 1948–1989; first female professor 1996.
- **Wrocław Miastoprojekt 1989**: 59% female workforce; zero female directors (12 men); zero female chief engineers (8 men).
- **Key interwar pioneers**: [[Barbara Brukalska]], [[Helena Syrkusowa]], [[Adrianna Górska]] (Paris), Anatolia Piotrowska.
- **Contemporary SARP data**: Honorowa Nagroda: 6 women / 68 men in 57 years; only 3 independently.
- **IARP licensing**: ~45% women among licenced architects (below parity despite educational majority).
- **EU ranking**: Poland 21st/27 on Gender Equality Index (2022).
- **Core paradox**: feminization of education ≠ feminization of decision-making, income, or prestige.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The article focuses on Politechnika Wrocławska as its institutional case study — how representative is this of other Polish faculties? WA PW (Warsaw) had female professor Barbara Brukalska already in 1948, which the authors note in a footnote as an exception.
- The "socialist improvement" narrative is partially confirmed by the quantitative data but complicated by the absence of women in any leadership role through the same period — formal equality coexisted with structural exclusion.
- The "archidziadocen" framing of the editorial implies generational change in progress; the data (6 vs. 68 SARP Honorowa Nagroda; 21st in EU gender equality) suggests the pace is far slower than the rhetoric.
