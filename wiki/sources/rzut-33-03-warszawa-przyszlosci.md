## Summary

A long-form interview with [[Marlena Happach]], Architect of the City of Warsaw and director of [[BAiPP]], conducted by the RZUT editorial team. Happach explains the philosophical and methodological basis of Warsaw's new Studium, positions it as a climate-first "spatial constitution" for a 30-year horizon, and addresses the full range of planning challenges: demographic forecasting, [[Blue-Green Infrastructure]], compact city, developer collaboration, legislative instability, and heritage protection.

The interview opens with Happach's articulation of what the Studium is: a record of values, a plan of action, a profound shift in urban thinking. The primacy of climate over aesthetic concerns ("climate axes, not visual axes and spectacular squares") marks a break from the Studium of 2006. Formally, the document consists of two volumes (Conditions and Directions), supported by an Environmental Impact Assessment, some 300+ pages, 19 large maps, and dozens of diagrams. Happach describes its preparation as "titanic work" supported by external experts in sociology, demography, economics, housing, transport, and environmental protection — including riding the planned green ring routes on bicycles to verify their spatial reality.

A key methodological contribution is the demographic bilans (*bilans*): a land-capacity accounting exercise that compares projected need against available reserves and determines whether new reserves must be designated. The bilans showed Warsaw needs more brownfield housing (multi-family), universal services, and logistics land — but no new single-family or commercial/production reserves. The war in Ukraine transformed theoretical demographic projections: by early 2022 Warsaw had already reached mid-century population levels in combined temporary and permanent residents.

## Key Points

- Studium is the "spatial constitution of Warsaw" — binding on all new local plans; 30-year horizon; two volumes + environmental impact assessment + 19 maps.
- Climate primacy over aesthetics: "climate axes, not visual axes; spectacular squares" — resilience as survival condition, not aesthetic preference.
- Demographic precision: real population 1.97M (mobile phone data) vs. 1.79M (registry); projected +280K by 2050 to ~2.25M. War in Ukraine collapsed decades of growth into months.
- **Bilans** methodology: land-capacity accounting confirms brownfield priority; Warsaw needs multi-family housing + services + logistics reserves; does NOT need new single-family or commercial reserves.
- 25,000 public submissions received during initial consultation; majority concerned with allotment gardens and green space — this shaped the document's priorities.
- Internal coordination: 18 Warsaw districts, each with partial autonomy; plan-making process involves extended negotiation.
- **Developer cooperation**: positive model — Centrum Praskie Koneser, Browary Warszawskie (site of Warsaw's first woonerf), Elektrownia Powiśle, Fabryka Norblina, Towarowa 22. Developers increasingly invest in greenery, water retention, car-free courtyards.
- **Air ventilation corridors** (*korytarze wymiany i regeneracji powietrza*): formerly "wedges" (*kliny nawietrzające*) — now degraded by suburban sprawl on the SW approach; Studium strengthens them to reduce the urban heat island.
- **Heritage**: 50+ post-WWII buildings identified for protection in the Studium; 4 new cultural parks proposed on both banks of the Vistula.
- Post-1989 buildings now at risk: Supersam was lost; biurowce from the 1990s being demolished for larger, optimised buildings. Studium + local plans + heritage register hoped to stem this.
- Legislative constraint: 2003 Planning Act was being revised simultaneously with Studium preparation; new planning act introduces the *plan ogólny*, requiring the Studium to be converted — Happach expresses confidence the vision is universal and will survive the format change.
- Służewiec Przemysłowy: a cautionary example of monofunctional office zoning that has failed; owners want mixed uses but the rigid local plan blocks it, paradoxically forcing reliance on the *specustawa mieszkaniowa* (housing act) to bypass planning.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Happach praises developer-led mixed-use regeneration (Browary, Koneser) while lamenting market-driven demolitions of modernist and 1990s buildings — the same market is simultaneously praised and blamed. "The invisible hand of the market is very visible in Warsaw."
- The Studium's list of 50+ post-war buildings for protection is a recommendation, not a binding order — the interview acknowledges Supersam was already lost.
- Simultaneous creation of the Studium and the new planning act creates a document that must be immediately redrafted as a *plan ogólny* — Happach's confidence in the vision's universality may be tested by format constraints.
- The democratic consultation process (25K submissions) is presented as successful, but the majority concerned allotment gardens — a specifically middle-class concern not representative of all Varsovians.
