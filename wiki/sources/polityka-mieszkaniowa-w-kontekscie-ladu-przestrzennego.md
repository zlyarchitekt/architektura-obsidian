## Summary

This peer-reviewed article by Agata Twardoch (Silesian University of Technology) analyses the relationship between Polish housing policy and spatial order (*ład przestrzenny*) across national, regional, and local administrative levels. The study combines a quantitative evaluation of the National Housing Programme (NPM, 2016) and its legislative tools with a qualitative assessment of three recently built housing estates: Nowy Nikiszowiec in Katowice (public-private, Mieszkanie Plus), Nowe Żerniki in Wrocław (public-private partnership with master planning), and Bliska Wola in Warsaw (purely private developer-led). Twardoch constructs a detailed analytical grid derived from the statutory definition of spatial order (functional, socio-economic, environmental, cultural, and compositional-aesthetic requirements) and uses it to evaluate both policy documents and built outcomes.

**PL:** Artykuł naukowy Agaty Twardoch (Politechnika Śląska) analizuje związki między polską polityką mieszkaniową a ładem przestrzennym na trzech szczeblach administracyjnych. Badanie łączy ilościową ewaluację Narodowego Programu Mieszkaniowego (NPM, 2016) i jego narzędzi legislacyjnych z jakościową oceną trzech nowo wybudowanych osiedli: Nowego Nikiszowca w Katowicach (publiczno-prywatne, Mieszkanie Plus), Nowych Żerników we Wrocławiu (partnerstwo publiczno-prywatne z master planem) oraz Bliskiej Woli w Warszawie (czysto deweloperskie). Autorka konstruuje szczegółową siatkę analityczną wynikającą z ustawowej definicji ładu przestrzennego (wymagania funkcjonalne, społeczno-gospodarcze, środowiskowe, kulturowe oraz kompozycyjno-estetyczne) i stosuje ją do oceny zarówno dokumentów politycznych, jak i zrealizowanych efektów.

The core finding is that Polish housing policy does not guarantee spatial order. The NPM mentions spatial order only four times, often narrowly equating it with landscape aesthetics. The 2018 Act on Facilitating Residential Investments severely weakened local planning tools (MPZP) by allowing residential development outside plans and up to 14 storeys regardless of plan provisions. Among the three estates, only Nowe Żerniki performed well — but this was due to exceptional public-sector involvement (master plan, city architect, workshops) rather than systemic policy. Bliska Wola represents a market-failure case of extreme density without social or environmental consideration. The article concludes by surveying successful international instruments: urban growth boundaries, mandatory housing quotas (Paris, Berlin, Vienna), anti-vacancy taxes, and binding urban design standards.

**PL:** Główny wniosek: polska polityka mieszkaniowa nie gwarantuje zachowania ładu przestrzennego. NPM wspomina ład przestrzenny zaledwie czterokrotnie, często wąsko utożsamiając go z estetyką krajobrazu. Ustawa z 2018 r. o ułatwieniach inwestycji mieszkaniowych drastycznie osłabiła lokalne narzędzia planistyczne (MPZP), zezwalając na zabudowę mieszkaniową poza planem i do 14 kondygnacji niezależnie od zapisów planu. Spośród trzech osiedli tylko Nowe Żerniki uzyskały dobrą ocenę — ale był to efekt wyjątkowego zaangażowania sektora publicznego (master plan, architekt miejski, warsztaty), a nie systemowej polityki. Bliska Wola to przykład rynkowej katastrofy: skrajna gęstość bez uwzględnienia wymogów społecznych i środowiskowych. Artykuł kończy przeglądem skutecznych instrumentów międzynarodowych: urban growth boundaries, obowiązkowe prowizje mieszkaniowe (Paryż, Berlin, Wiedeń), podatki antypustostanowe i wiążące standardy urbanistyczne.

## Key Points

- The statutory definition of spatial order (2003 Planning Act) comprises five requirement categories: functional, socio-economic, environmental, cultural, and compositional-aesthetic. These are operationalised into measurable housing-estate indicators.
- The National Housing Programme (2016) addresses functional requirements most frequently, mentions environmental issues only via energy efficiency, and largely ignores socio-economic mixing, cultural facilities, and compositional quality.
- The 2018 Act on Facilitating Residential Investments undermined MPZP by permitting housing on non-housing plots and overriding height limits, effectively removing local spatial planning from housing location decisions.
- Regional level (voivodeship) has almost no housing-policy tools; there is no regional coordination of settlement patterns or housing demand between municipalities.
- Nowy Nikiszowiec (Katowice): good compositional references to historic Nikiszowiec, but poor location (forest, no services), weak public transport, and low functional diversity. Built under Mieszkanie Plus by BGK Nieruchomości / PFR Nieruchomości.
- Nowe Żerniki (Wrocław): best practice. Master plan first, then MPZP, then plots sold to developers. Mixed tenure (ownership + rental + TBS), city architect involvement, workshops with 40 local practices, cultural facilities planned from the start. Positive social and environmental scores.
- Bliska Wola (Warsaw): worst practice. Extreme density (up to 92 m / 27 floors), 420 dwellings/ha, minimal green space (20–25% biologically active), poor pedestrian/cycle connectivity, no social housing, no cultural facilities, monotonous architecture. Approved under MPZP but MPZP itself was highly controversial.
- International comparisons show that spatial-order-compatible housing policy requires: (1) limits on greenfield development (UGBs); (2) mandatory affordable-housing quotas in every development; (3) anti-vacancy instruments; (4) regional coordination of land release; (5) binding urban design standards linked to infrastructure obligations.
- Main barriers in Poland: quantitative focus overriding quality, very strong property rights blocking public-interest overrides, weak city agency against capital, and low social valuation of common good.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The NPM explicitly states that housing policy should support spatial order, yet the 2018 Act (introduced under the same programme) directly contradicts this by weakening local planning control. How can a single policy framework contain such contradictory instruments?
- Nowe Żerniki succeeded because of exceptional public-sector leadership (master plan, city architect, workshops), not because of any systemic requirement. Is this replicable without a fundamental legal mandate for master planning?
- Poland has no statistical housing deficit (13.6 million dwellings vs 13.5 million households in 2011), yet policy remains quantity-driven. Why does the political discourse ignore the quality-access gap?
- Strong property rights are cited as the main barrier to mixed-tenure quotas and anti-vacancy taxes. Can the Polish constitutional and political context ever accommodate the "public interest overrides private property" principle that underpins Western European housing tools?
- Twardoch's own prior work (*System do mieszkania*) advocates accessible housing. Does the article's emphasis on spatial order risk privileging aesthetic/formal concerns over social accessibility for low-income households?
