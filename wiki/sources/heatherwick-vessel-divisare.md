## Summary

This Divisare project description is Heatherwick Studio's own promotional account of the Vessel. It is an insider source — written by the designers in the language of design intent — and should be read as such: it presents the design logic and the conceptual genealogy (Indian stepwells → amphitheatre → open lattice), but makes no mention of the suicides, the closure, or any controversy. The wiki already has a more critical account via the Thomas Heatherwick Wikipedia source.

**PL:** *Ten opis projektowy z Divisare to własny materiał promocyjny Heatherwick Studio — pisany językiem intencji projektowej. Prezentuje logikę projektu i genealogię konceptualną (indyjskie studnie schodkowe → amfiteatr → otwarta kratownica), ale nie wspomina o samobójstwach, zamknięciu ani żadnej kontrowersji. Wiki posiada już bardziej krytyczne ujęcie przez źródło Wikipedii.*

The source is valuable for two distinct reasons: (1) it provides the **design rationale** in Heatherwick's own words — particularly the move from stepwell (inward-focused) to open lattice (inward + outward), and the structural logic of the steel spine between each pair of staircases; (2) it describes the **fabrication process** in detail: 75 huge steel components manufactured in Venice by specialist fabricator Cimolai, transported in six shipments from Italy, carried across the Hudson River by barge, and assembled on site over three years.

**PL:** *Źródło wartościowe dla: (1) uzasadnienia projektowego własnymi słowami Heatherwicska — przejście od studni schodkowej (skupionej wewnętrznie) do otwartej kratownicy (wewnątrz + zewnątrz) oraz logika strukturalna stalowego kręgosłupa; (2) opisu procesu fabrykacji: 75 dużych elementów stalowych wyprodukowanych w Wenecji przez Cimolai, transportowanych w sześciu partiach z Włoch, przywiezionych przez rzekę Hudson barkami, montowanych przez trzy lata.*

## Key Points

- Design brief: a "centrepiece" for Hudson Yards Public Plaza — memorable single object, not dispersed sculpture; social + active, not static.
- Formal genealogy: Roman Spanish Steps (staircase as congregation point) → Indian stepwells (intricate stone network, accessible at all water levels) → hybrid: stepwell's complexity made outward-looking by opening voids between steps.
- Key structural device: steel spine between each pair of staircases, creating a natural up/down division; exposed raw welded steel for "clarity and integrity."
- Cladding: underside of staircases in deep copper-toned metal — differentiates the stairs from the surrounding architecture.
- Scale: 16 storeys, 2,465 steps, 80 landings, more than 1 mile of routes, 154 interconnecting flights of stairs.
- Fabrication: 75 steel components; specialist fabricator Cimolai (Venice, Italy); 6 maritime shipments; assembled on site over 3 years.
- Bespoke elements: every joint, every handrail designed individually.
- Intent: human scale despite size — designed to be "climbed, explored and enjoyed; a simple structure, animated by people."

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The "human scale" ambition versus the structure's actual harm record is the central contradiction. The source frames the open lattice as socially inviting; the Wikipedia source reveals it as a foreseeable danger. Together they document the gap between design intent and real-world consequence.
- Fabricator Cimolai (Venice) is a world-class specialist in complex steel structures (also worked on the Beijing National Stadium). The 3-year on-site assembly is unusual for a structure of this type — the complexity of bespoke connections is the hidden cost of the "simple structure" claim.
- No structural engineer is credited in the source.

## Quotes

> "Rather than an inert, static sculpture, it should be a social encounter, which encourages activity and participation — it should be fun."

> "By opening up voids between the steps to create a three-dimensional lattice, the public square could be stretched upwards, creating more than a mile of routes that could be explored in different ways."

> "Despite the size of the Vessel, it has been designed at a human scale, to be climbed, explored and enjoyed by New Yorkers and visitors — a simple structure, animated by people and the reflections of the square beneath."
