## Summary

Anna Halek's essay traces the history, spatial logic, and afterlife of Albania's Projekti Bunkerizimit — the totalitarian bunkerization programme launched by Enver Hoxha in the 1970s. Between 100,000 and 750,000 reinforced concrete bunkers (the exact figure is unknowable; all maps and documents were lost after the fall of communism) were distributed across the country's entire landscape: beaches, city centres, alpine ridges, rural fields. The stated purpose was to provide shelter for both soldiers and civilians against foreign invasion — a function never tested, since no attack came.

Halek argues that the bunkers functioned not as military infrastructure but as a psychological instrument of the regime: materialized fear, deployed to justify Hoxha's role as protector of the nation and to sustain a state of permanent collective anxiety. Construction consumed roughly 2% of Albania's GDP annually; the cost of a single bunker equalled that of a small apartment. The materials used met no genuine military standards, as surviving soldiers attested. The "Special Structures" (*Struktura Speciale*) — the largest type, built for political elites and submarine bases — were the only ones with anti-nuclear tunnels; ordinary citizens received only the illusion of protection.

After communism's fall (1991), the bunkers remained: indelible, indestructible, legally and logistically impossible to remove at scale. They have since migrated from symbol of oppression to element of everyday landscape — adopted as storerooms, restaurants, beach bars, and informal infrastructure. Halek uses two theoretical lenses: Foucault's concept of *heterotopia* (a space that holds multiple, contradictory social roles simultaneously) and Turner's concept of *liminality* (objects "neither here nor there", suspended between function and non-function). The bunkers' failure to fulfil their designed purpose places them permanently in a liminal state — useless as defence, indestructible as landscape, and gradually indistinguishable from the natural formations they were camouflaged to resemble.

## Key Points

- **Projekti Bunkerizimit** (1970s–1991): Hoxha's programme to build between 100,000 and 750,000 reinforced concrete bunkers across all of Albania.
- **Three types**: QZ (*Qender Zjarri*) — smallest, single-soldier, often deployed in coastal triple groups; PZ (*Pike Zjarri*) — medium, for groups or light artillery, prefabricated; *Struktura Speciale* — largest, for political leadership and military bases, the only type with anti-nuclear tunnels.
- **Economic cost**: ~2% of GDP annually; equivalent to one small apartment per bunker. The programme devastated an already fragile economy.
- **Military failure**: Materials and construction quality did not meet basic military standards. The objects' function was political-psychological, not defensive. When tested during the Kosovo conflict in 1999, bunkers crumbled.
- **Heterotopia** (Foucault, "Of Other Spaces", 1986): bunkers as spaces that hold contradictory social roles — shelter/prison, protection/threat, monument/ruin.
- **Liminality** (Turner, 1969): objects "neither here nor there" — suspended between their designed function (never exercised) and the landscape they inhabit. Their failure to be used keeps them permanently liminally.
- **Afterlife**: incorporated into Albanian landscape as quasi-natural formations; repurposed informally as storerooms, restaurants, landscape elements; recognized as architectural symbols of a difficult past.
- **Source book**: Gjorgi Mydyti and Elian Stefa, *Concrete Mushrooms: Reusing Albania's 750,000 Abandoned Bunkers* (Barcelona, 2012) — the main empirical reference cited by Halek.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The exact number of bunkers is irrecoverable — estimates range from 100,000 to 750,000. This uncertainty is itself an outcome of the regime's destruction of documentation.
- Halek frames the bunkers' repurposing as a form of adaptive reuse but stops short of an explicit design or policy recommendation for managing 750,000 objects. The essay is diagnostic, not prescriptive.
- The heterotopia/liminality reading is productive but risks aestheticizing a programme that caused real material harm (impoverishment) and psychological damage (collective trauma, sustained fear).
