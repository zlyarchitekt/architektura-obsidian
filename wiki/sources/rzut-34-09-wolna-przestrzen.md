## Summary
An interview with Mexican architect Tatiana Bilbao exploring architecture as a non-prescriptive platform for diverse ways of living. Bilbao recounts her journey from irritation at being asked "what it is like to be a woman in architecture" to accepting the responsibility of trailblazing for younger Mexican architects — a network now including Rozana Montiel, Fernanda Canales, Frida Escobedo and Gabriela Carrillo. The core argument is that standard housing is designed for the nuclear family (only 18% of society), ignoring single people, groups of friends, multi-generational households, and changing life stages. Bilbao criticises the pretence that men and women compete on equal terms when pregnancy, birth and breastfeeding constitute a full-time parallel job. Her own care network — parents, sister, husband — is presented as a privilege, not a system. During the pandemic she tried to organise shared childcare across her six-flat building but failed because the architecture lacked shared kitchens, laundries, or study rooms. She advocates for flexible, non-programmed structures where rooms are not assigned fixed functions (kitchen, bathroom, bedroom) but become whatever inhabitants need — enabled by modern technology (movable kitchens, dry toilets in mountainous areas). She recounts the Reconstruir MX post-earthquake programme where her studio offered only technical advice, letting indigenous communities decide spatial solutions — including a family that refused street-facing windows. Bilbao concludes that radical change happens in built space and political space, not only in intellectual discourse; architects must negotiate with reality, accept compromises, but never cross ethical lines (she abandoned a commission that demanded servant quarters).

## Key Points
- **Nuclear family bias**: Standard apartments are designed for 2+1 or 2+2 nuclear families — a model that describes only ~18% of society and lasts only ~20 years per lifecycle. There is no market offer for groups of friends, multi-generational households, or single people who want shared infrastructure.
- **Pregnancy as architectural problem**: Bilbao argues that ignoring biological difference (pregnancy, birth, breastfeeding) places women at a structural disadvantage. If a mother is performing a second full-time job while competing intellectually, equality is impossible without acknowledging difference.
- **Pandemic care experiment**: In a six-flat building, Bilbao proposed shared childcare rotating by household. It failed because there was no shared kitchen (cooking for 20 impossible in a private flat), no study room for all children, no shared laundry. Architecture prevented collective care.
- **Non-prescriptive architecture**: Rooms should not be named and fixed ("this is the bathroom"). Technology allows movable kitchens, water and light anywhere. Dry toilets work in rural Mexico; standard sewerage is an environmental problem there. The architect's role is to create a platform, not to design someone's life.
- **Reconstruir MX**: Post-earthquake technical advice only. Communities chose materials and layouts. One family refused street-facing windows; Bilbao's team accepted it. "It is their house, we are not the designers."
- **Ethical lines**: Abandoned a residential commission when the client insisted on maid's rooms. "We cannot be part of your business." The developer changed the project and removed her office's name.
- **Transfer across budgets**: Same spatial strategies for rich and poor; only scale differs. Alejandro Aravena's strategy: make low-tech solutions sexy by building them for celebrities — "build a mud hut for the Kardashians."

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- If architects should not prescribe lifestyles, how do building codes (which mandate kitchens, bathrooms, bedrooms) allow true flexibility?
- Bilbao's own privilege (family care network, successful practice) enables her radical position. Can non-prescriptive architecture scale to mass housing for people without such support?
- The Mexican state's incapacity to provide care infrastructure (nurseries, eldercare) is compensated by family networks. Is Bilbao's praise of Latin American family solidarity a realistic model, or does it romanticise the absence of public welfare?

> (I have no right to design someone's life. I can only create a platform through which others can fulfil themselves as they wish.)

> (Change can only be introduced through what gets built.)
