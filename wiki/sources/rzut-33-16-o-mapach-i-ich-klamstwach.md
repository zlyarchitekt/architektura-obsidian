## Summary

Weronika Kozak's essay opens with a foundational claim: maps lie. Every 2D representation of 3D reality requires simplifications — manipulation of scale, shape, and size — but this is concealed by maps' authoritative visual register. States adopted cartography precisely because it produced and guaranteed territory, giving maps a persuasive power that George Orwell already understood in *1984*. Denis Wood's concept of a *cartography of reality* challenges this: since every place is infinite and incommensurable, cartography cannot reduce to one abstracted version valid for all.

From this epistemological critique, Kozak develops the concept of **counter-cartography** (*kontrmapowanie*), coined by Nancy Lee Peluso in the context of Dayak land rights in Indonesian Borneo. Counter-mapping inverts cartography's standard logic: instead of producing hierarchies and erasures, it exposes them. It gives voice to excluded entities — communities, ecosystems, memories — and builds collective political agency. Bureau d'Etudes argues that new maps create collective memory and thus collectivity itself; unlike raw datasets, maps help communities organise and claim their rights.

The essay surveys five cases of counter-mapping practice across different scales and media: the Anti-Eviction Mapping Project in San Francisco (tracking displacement networks); Nermin Elsherif's participatory Port Said map (community memories replacing official cartography); cuerpo-territorio / body mapping (Latin American embodied technique bypassing language barriers); Unanyan's *Unintended City* in Kolkata (protecting 2 million undocumented residents); and Lucas LaRochelle's *Queering The Map* (global LGBTQ+ memory map that simultaneously creates community and disrupts spatial-temporal cartographic logic). Kozak also discusses Rebecca Solnit's *Unfathomable City*, which maps corruption/lead-level correlations and oil-industry ecosystem destruction in New Orleans.

A final section acknowledges counter-cartography's paradoxes: counter-maps are still maps and still distort; exposing undocumented communities can endanger them (AEMP's Erin McElroy; Iconoclasistas' consent-based mapping protocol); anonymity need not mean invisibility (*Queering The Map* as proof). Counter-mapping is not an infallible democratic rescue tool but a useful instrument for care-oriented, ground-up spatial practice.

## Key Points

- Maps are political instruments masquerading as objective representations; their authority conceals subjectivity.
- From one dataset, infinitely many maps can be made — receiving one map means receiving one story (Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie's "danger of a single story").
- Counter-cartography (*kontrmapowanie*) = inversion of mapping's standard logic; term coined by Nancy Lee Peluso for Dayak land rights in Kalimantan/Borneo.
- Denis Wood: a "cartography of reality" must be more human and diverse, based on emotion and experience, including "light, stars, shadows, birdsong."
- Mark Monmonier (*How to Lie with Maps*): "If from one dataset many maps can arise, which is correct? Is that even the right question?"
- Bruno Latour (*Down to Earth*, 2018): "Everything will have to be remapped."
- Bureau d'Etudes: new maps create collective memory and community; unlike raw data, maps help marginalised groups organise and fight for rights.
- **Anti-Eviction Mapping Project (AEMP, San Francisco)**: tracked shell companies and Airbnb connections behind displacement; built *Narratives of Displacement and Resistance* (murals, zine, resident rights guide); found displacement most frequently affects women, elderly, working class, ethnic minorities.
- **Port Said community mapping (Nermin Elsherif)**: replaced official city image with first-person community narrative; pins and strings connecting stories across Egypt and diaspora; no hierarchy of stories; map as participatory both in making and reading.
- **Cuerpo-territorio / body mapping** (Latin American practice): embodied memories and emotions mapped onto a drawing of one's own body; reverses map logic (body over space); bypasses language barriers entirely (Unanyan, S. Zaragocin & M.A. Caretta).
- **Unanyan (Kolkata, 1977–)**: founded by Jai Sen; *Unintended City* project mapped ~2 million undocumented low-income residents in Panchannagram, invisible on city maps; enabled negotiation against highway destruction; part of broader Dalit/working-class rights activism.
- **Queering The Map (Lucas LaRochelle, Montreal)**: LGBTQ2IA+ memory map — anonymous entries (love stories, coming-out memories, traumas, trivia) without dates or markers; global palimpsest; attacks the map's standard orienting function; hacked by Trump supporters 2019; builds visible community without exposing individuals.
- Rebecca Solnit & Rebecca Snedeker (*Unfathomable City*): two New Orleans counter-maps — corruption/lead-level correlations; Gulf oil industry ecosystem destruction (water not as empty blue space but as pipeline-crossed surface where animals die).
- Paradoxes: counter-maps also distort; exposing undocumented communities can endanger them legally; Iconoclasistas advocate consent-based, anonymity-respecting mapping; AEMP must keep some data secret.
- Ideal: counter-cartography as component of a wider care-ethics spatial strategy — starting from personal relation to space, not a "universal" top-down perspective; "What if top-down planning were replaced by information extracted during counter-mapping workshops?"

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Counter-cartography contains an internal contradiction: it uses maps to fight maps, inheriting their distortions. There is no neutral practice of spatial representation.
- The tension between visibility (making excluded communities legible) and safety (exposing undocumented or vulnerable people) is unresolved. Kozak presents best practices (consent protocols, anonymity design) but no structural solution.
- *Queering The Map* was hacked (2019) and the Port Said map was physically destroyed — the essay ends with "The possibilities of maps end where the possibilities of people end."
