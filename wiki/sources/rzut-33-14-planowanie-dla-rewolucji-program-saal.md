## Summary

Wiktor Martin's essay reconstructs the history and spatial logic of SAAL (*Serviço de Apoio Ambulatório Local* — Mobile Local Support Service), Portugal's participatory housing programme created on 6 August 1974, three months after the Carnation Revolution (25 April 1974). SAAL was conceived by Nuno Portas, Secretary of Housing and Urbanism in the provisional government, as a humane alternative to the barracks-estates the government had initially planned to construct on city peripheries. Inspired by Aldo van Eyck's Otterlo Circles philosophy and by the self-built *pueblos jóvenes* of Peru, Portas designed a programme based on six principles: community self-organisation, preservation of social-spatial ties, reduction of bureaucracy, residents' own resources as co-financing, decentralisation of estates to prevent ghettoisation, and an Incremental Housing strategy allowing future expansion.

The programme decentralised into three regional delegatures: SAAL Norte (Porto), SAAL Lisboa e Centro-Sul, and SAAL Algarve. Each created *Brigadas de Construção* — field design teams comprising the country's best architects (Álvaro Siza, Gonçalo Byrne, Artur Rosa, Fernando Távora, Manuel Vicente) who worked directly with resident cooperatives. Brigades received full autonomy in design decisions, functioning as social interpreters rather than government agents — deliberately so, to prevent residents from perceiving SAAL as a political tool.

Martin's essay focuses on Porto, where the programme found its most consistent architectural expression. Porto's working class lived in *ilhas* — linear vernacular settlements embedded inside urban city blocks, with no direct access to public space. Unlike Lisbon (which sought to move the poor to peripheral estates), SAAL Norte aimed to rehabilitate ilhas *in situ*, preserving residents' right to their place and preventing homogeneous peripheral ghettoes. Álvaro Siza's two Porto projects — SAAL São Victor (1974–1975) and SAAL Bouça (1975–2006) — are examined in depth as exemplars of this approach. Both engage the ilha typology (row houses, intimate shared space), integrate ruins and historical traces as counterpoints, and redefine the public/private boundary through articulated facades and threshold spaces.

SAAL lasted 26 months. Political instability in the post-revolutionary government (six provisional governments before the July 1976 elections), administrative conflicts between SAAL and municipal housing agencies, and the systematic withdrawal of financial support caused the programme's dissolution in October 1976. In its brief lifetime, 170 projects were designed for over 40,000 families; 33 projects were prepared for Porto affecting 11,500 families; only 11 were started; 375 homes were completed. Bouça was fully finished only in 2006, and São Victor was never completed. The programme's influence outlasted its execution: Siza's SAAL work attracted international attention and led to his Berlin commissions, launching his global career.

## Key Points

- **Context**: Portugal 1970–1973: 35,000 families in camps, 620,000 in overcrowded housing; 40% of housing stock over 50 years old; 48% without water, 57% without sanitation.
- **Carnation Revolution** (25 April 1974): near-bloodless coup ending Estado Novo; democratization unleashed working-class demands for housing rights.
- **SAAL**: created 6 August 1974; Nuno Portas, Secretary of Housing and Urbanism; six principles; financed via non-repayable state grants (15–20× minimum monthly wage per household) + 25-year preferential loans; land usually offered as long-term lease of communal plots.
- **Philosophical roots**: Aldo van Eyck's Otterlo Circles (1959, last CIAM congress); Peruvian *pueblos jóvenes* as self-organised housing reference; emphasis on collective identity over individual units.
- **Three delegatures**: SAAL Norte (Porto, most coherent), SAAL Lisboa e Centro-Sul (fragmented, less successful architecturally), SAAL Algarve (southern Portugal).
- **Brigadas de Construção**: field teams of leading architects with full design autonomy; deliberately framed as technical assistants ("hand of the people"), not government agents.
- **Ilhas** (Porto): linear vernacular worker settlements inside urban block interiors; 60,000 residents (~25% of Porto) in mid-1970s; threatened by Estado Novo–era speculation and municipal relocation programmes. SAAL Norte chose rehabilitation *in situ* over demolition.
- **Álvaro Siza — SAAL São Victor** (1974–1975): 102 row-house units at Senhora das Dores; building A1 retains ruins of the original ilha as historical counterpoint; politically the most committed SAAL project; never completed.
- **Álvaro Siza — SAAL Bouça** (1975–2006): 131 duplex units (58 completed in 1978, remainder in 2006); trapezoid site at Rua da Boavista; 4 parallel rows of row houses; library, laundry, commercial units, cooperative offices; influenced by Aalto (Sunila, 1939) and Bruno Taut (Britz, 1925–1930); redefines public/private boundary through rhythmic staircases, vertical partitions, recessed terraces, and horizontal galleries.
- **Self-help criticism**: Lisbon residents protested in February 1975 ("self-help is double exploitation — after a full working day, they order us to build our own homes late into the night"); SAAL Lisboa e Centro-Sul suspended self-help requirement in May 1975.
- **Dissolution** (October 1976): six provisional governments, administrative conflict with Lisbon municipality, withdrawal of government support; programme lasted 26 months.
- **Legacy**: 170 projects designed, 40,000 families; 33 Porto projects (11,500 families); 11 started; 375 homes built. SAAL's participatory methodology attracted international attention; Siza was invited to Berlin, launching his international career.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- SAAL's stated neutrality (Portas deliberately framed Siza as "hand of the people" not a government agent) was a strategic fiction — the programme was explicitly created as a political response to post-revolutionary pressures. This tension between technical neutrality and political purpose is unresolved.
- The Lisbon delegature's failure (administrative interference, design incoherence) compared with Porto's success raises the question of how much programme quality depended on architectural leadership vs. political conditions.
- Bouça's 2006 completion reprogrammed the project for the middle class (parking, redesigned units), raising the question of whether the completed project belongs to the SAAL heritage or represents its transformation into something else.
- Eduardo Souto de Moura is mentioned as the 22-year-old student who initiated SAAL São Victor — his later career as a Pritzker architect (2011) is not discussed.
