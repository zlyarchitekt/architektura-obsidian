## Summary

Kacper Borek's essay is structured as a chronological reportage — a walk through Bełchatów in late 2022 that triggers a backward journey through the history of Galeria Bawełnianka, a 35,000 m² shopping center completed in 2012 and never opened. The Bawełnianka case functions both as a local tragedy and as a diagnostic instrument for the systemic failures of Polish spatial planning.

The building stands on the site of the former Bełchatowskie Zakłady Przemysłu Bawełnianego (BZPB), a cotton factory whose history from 1947 to its final liquidation in 2013 is reconstructed in detail. The developer (Bawełnianka sp. z o.o., est. 2008) designed a mixed-use shopping centre incorporating the factory's historic chimney as an architectural feature, with plans from architects Pallado i Skupin Architekci. Despite positive initial reception (>75% of surveyed residents supported the plan), the project was completed simultaneously with a competing mall — Galeria Olimpia, developed by Echo Investment — just a few kilometres away on the southern edge of the city. Olimpia succeeded; Bawełnianka never attracted a single tenant and went bankrupt in 2015 without being able to afford the bankruptcy proceedings itself.

Borek identifies three structural pathologies: (1) Polish spatial planning documents (*studia*, *plany miejscowe*) systematically over-allocate development land — Bełchatów's 2010 Studium permitted six large retail sites, with a theoretical capacity of 566,200 m² of additional service floor space, *while* projecting population decline from 57,000 to 51,000 residents; (2) local governments lack legal tools to coordinate competing investments, negotiate development conditions with private investors, or prioritise central-area development over peripheral sprawl; (3) vacant private properties have no effective legal mechanism to resolve — the Dutch "Leegstandswet" (2010), which requires owners to register vacancies and allows municipalities to assign users after 12 months, is cited as a model that Poland lacks.

The essay's future dimension examines the structural crisis of the shopping centre typology: post-pandemic footfall collapsed (–49% vs March 2019); the next generation of retail properties is hybrid buildings (mixed-use, flexible, experience-driven); e-commerce has made logistics/warehousing the dominant real estate asset class; and Generation Z's consumption patterns demand spaces that dissolve the online/in-real-life boundary. The Bawełnianka story thus becomes a symptom of a transitional moment in retail urbanism.

## Key Points

- Galeria Bawełnianka (Bełchatów): 35,000 m², built on the site of BZPB (Bełchatowskie Zakłady Przemysłu Bawełnianego, est. 1947, liquidated 2013). Architects: Pallado i Skupin Architekci. Interior redesign concept: HSA (Human Scale Architecture), Kraków.
- Simultaneously built: Galeria Olimpia (Echo Investment), ~32,000 m², on the southern periphery of Bełchatów. Olimpia succeeded; Bawełnianka failed to attract any tenant.
- Bełchatów's 2010 Studium permitted six large retail sites; the 2017 update maintained 566,200 m² of theoretical service capacity while projecting population decline from 57k to 51k by 2030 — no market analysis was conducted.
- Prof. Przemysław Śleszyński (IGiPZ PAN): Polish planning documents nationally over-allocate buildable land — studies designate ~13% of Poland's territory for development, while only ~6% is actually urbanised and 1.2% is residential. In some municipalities, 10× more land is designated for housing than is currently built.
- Dr Dorota Celińska-Janowicz (EUROREG): Polish regulations on large retail are "liberal by European standards"; municipalities lack legal tools to regulate, coordinate, or negotiate with large retail investors; arbitrariness of administrative decisions is high.
- The Dutch "Leegstandswet" (Vacancy Act, 2010): owners must register vacancies of >6 months; after 12 months, the municipality may assign a user (physical or legal person); owner must offer a usage contract within 3 months. No equivalent exists in Polish law.
- Residents' proposed alternatives for Bawełnianka: penthouses/apartments, civic offices hub, university with student accommodation, amphitheatre, indoor skatepark, urban exploration site.
- The Centre for Revitalisation Advisory Services (IMRiR) proposes converting municipal-owned vacant buildings into affordable housing.
- Post-pandemic retail: international trends point to hybrid buildings (mixed-use), specialisation, and renovation/adaptation of existing buildings rather than new construction. "Generation 5 gallery" = experience-driven hybrid building.
- E-commerce growth is making logistics/warehousing the leading real estate asset class; the traditional gallery model is shrinking market share.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The article is primarily a post-mortem, not a prescriptive proposal — the Dutch Leegstandswet is flagged but its Polish implementation path is not detailed.
- The "Generation 5 gallery" concept (experience-driven hybrid) is cited from industry sources; whether this is a sustainable typology or itself a transitional response to the e-commerce disruption remains open.
- The Bełchatów situation is complicated by the planned closure of the coal mine and power plant (the city's largest employer) — the structural demographic decline makes any commercial development uncertain, which the article notes but does not develop.
