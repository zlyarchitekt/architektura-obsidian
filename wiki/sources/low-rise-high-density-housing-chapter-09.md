## Summary

Chapter 9 surveys fourteen international low-rise, high-density housing projects built between 1989 and 2004, documenting how the typology evolved under the twin pressures of globalisation and the rise of the "starchitect." Perényi notes that communications technology accelerated cross-cultural pollination, producing global trends while simultaneously allowing local conditions to reassert themselves through material, climatic, and urban specificity. The chapter is organised around three cross-cutting themes: (1) strategies for breaking the monotony of repetition versus fortifying rhythmic uniformity; (2) the growing challenge of automobile parking in low-rise dense developments; and (3) the tension between increased demand for privacy/seclusion and the shrinking provision of communal space. The projects span six countries and a wide stylistic range: Swiss material minimalism (Zumthor), Portuguese tectonic severity (Souto de Moura, Aires Mateus), Dutch experimental density (West 8, MVRDV, NIO), London infill urbanism (Peter Barber), Japanese micro-living (NKS Architects), and Spanish climate-responsive sculptural massing (La Mareta, Cadiz, Madrid). Notable formal strategies include multi-architect masterplans (Borneo Island), single-material identity per unit (Hageneiland), De Stijl–inflected roof plans (Bom Successo), periscope-like waterfront masses (Rotterdam), and parking-as-critique concrete umbilical cords (Kanoya).

## Key Points

- **Globalisation vs. locality.** The chapter frames contemporary housing as suspended between global intellectual mobility and inherited local conditions: Spanish sculptural massing responds to sun, Dutch density to experimental tradition, and London infill to Victorian street morphology.
- **Breaking monotony.** Borneo Island (Amsterdam) assigns a different architect to each plot; Kabelwerk uses colourful façades; Hageneiland assigns a single material per house. By contrast, Maurice Nio's Cyclops row and Rafael Otero's Cadiz houses reinforce uniform rhythm.
- **Parking as design crisis.** The automobile's dominance in the new millennium has made parking placement a central design question, resolved variously through covered under-ledge bays (Nio), exterior barrier-level garages (Zumthor), on-street provision (Patio Island), or satirical concrete "umbilical cords" linking cars to flats (Kanoya).
- **Privacy up, communal space down.** Prosperous societies demand greater seclusion; shared areas shrink. The chapter documents this shift across all fourteen projects, from fenced garden enclosures to inward-turned patios and sound-absorbing street walls.
- **Flexible, minimal interiors.** Flat sizes have not grown; instead, adaptable plans and American-style open kitchens predominate. Japanese minimalism appears in Kanoya's 25–45 m² Tetris-like units; Zumthor's Spittelhof uses cabinet-like opening parking levels.
- **De Stijl resurgence.** Aires Mateus's Bom Successo housing in Óbidos explicitly references Theo van Doesburg and Piet Mondrian, translating Neo-Plasticist compositions into tectonic roof plans and megalithic wall masses.
- **Waterfront experiments.** Dutch and Japanese projects treat water as garden substitute: Joke Vos's Periscope Houses feature jetties and floating docks; West 8's Borneo Island turns harbour water into a blue "green space."

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The source celebrates global cross-pollination but also asserts that local specificity is "inherited over generations." How do these two claims coexist in projects like Borneo Island, where 30 architects from diverse backgrounds work within strict Dutch regulations?
- The claim that "social housing and rental flats are built more rarely" is stated as a global trend, but several projects here (Donnybrook Quarter, La Mareta) include social or affordable units. Is the trend overstated?
- Zumthor's Spittelhof is praised for respecting the landscape, yet its three-storey apartment house at the "vortex" of the composition is a conspicuous vertical marker. Does this contradict the low-rise brief?
- The Kanoya project's concrete plates linking cars to flats are described as a "critique of the motorised world," but the building itself accommodates nine cars for nine flats. Is this critique compromised by complicity?
