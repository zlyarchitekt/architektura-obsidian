## Summary

A statistical analysis by [[Aleksandra Gryc]] mapping the presence of women at Polish public technical universities, with architecture as the primary focus. The analysis is based on the annual "Kobiety na Politechnikach" (Women at Polytechnics) reports, data from OPI-PIB (State Research Institute for Information Processing), and the RAD-on academic database. The article is notable for presenting quantitative measures of the glass ceiling — moving beyond anecdote to structured indices — and offers a carefully qualified note that the available data uses a binary male/female distinction, which excludes non-binary persons.

The headline finding is a structural paradox: **architecture is simultaneously the most feminised technical discipline in Poland and still structurally unequal at senior career levels**. Women constitute 70% of architecture students (a proportion that has held for over 10 years), and 90% of interior design students (2022). This makes architecture by far the most female-dominated technical field. Yet against this feminised base, the glass ceiling persists:

The **Glass Ceiling Index (GCI)**, developed by Eurostat, measures the ratio of women among all academics to women among full professors. A score of 1.0 indicates perfect equality; scores above 1.0 indicate disadvantage. For all public technical universities in 2021/22, the GCI was **1.88** — almost twice the structural barrier. For architecture faculties specifically, the GCI was **1.29** — the best (lowest) score across all technical fields, meaning architecture has the smallest but still-present professorial glass ceiling.

The **flow index** (*indeks przepływów*) measures what proportion of women are retained at each successive step of the academic career (no degree → doctorate → habilitation → full professorship). The scale is 0–10, where 10 represents full retention parity. For architecture in 2021, the flow index was **8.45**; in 2022 it improved to **8.84** — the highest and fastest-improving in the technical sector.

The article concludes on a cautiously optimistic note: "the situation of women architects in universities is one of the best in the technical sector, though still some way from ideal and genuinely equal." The fact that architecture is already preting the way for other technical faculties is presented as grounds for continued pressure rather than complacency.

## Key Points

- **70% women** among architecture students (10+ years); **90%** in interior design (2022).
- **35% women** overall at technical universities — below gender balance threshold (40–60%).
- **GCI (Glass Ceiling Index)**: ratio of women among all academics ÷ women among full professors.
  - 1.0 = equality; higher = greater barrier.
  - Technical universities 2021/22: **GCI = 1.88**.
  - Architecture specifically: **GCI = 1.29** — best in the technical sector.
- **Leaky pipeline** (*dziurawy rurociąg*): women systematically lost at each career stage (doctorate → habilitation → professorship).
- **Flow index** (0–10, where 10 = full retention parity):
  - Architecture 2021: **8.45** → 2022: **8.84** — improving, highest in technical sector.
- Architecture faculties are the most gender-equal in the Polish technical university sector, but still below parity.
- Data limitation: binary male/female split in source data excludes non-binary persons.
- Architecture is described as a potential model for other technical faculties in the transition to gender equality.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The GCI and flow index measure academic career pathways; they do not capture the glass ceiling in private practice or competition juries — which is where article 02 ([[rzut-34-02-100-lat-architektek]]) shows the starkest inequality. The picture may be more severe outside academia.
- The 2022 data (GCI 1.29, flow index 8.84) represents a snapshot; it is unclear whether the trend is driven by retirements of a previously male-dominated cohort or by genuine structural change in hiring/promotion practices.
- Non-binary exclusion in the source data is acknowledged but not resolved — the author correctly notes the limitation without being able to correct for it.
