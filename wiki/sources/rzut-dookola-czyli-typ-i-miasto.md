## Summary
This article explores the fundamental human capacity for classification and categorization—a skill crucial for survival and social functioning. The author argues that this innate ability to group, classify, and name structures our chaotic environment into an understandable order. Drawing parallels between architectural typology and language, the piece posits that while basic concepts (like "dog" or "house") are universal categories, their specific expression is always bound by a local convention—be it a language ("chien" in French) or a scientific/cultural framework (the genre of film, or the definition of a building). The author traces this idea from early architectural theories (Laugier's primitive hut model) through Modernism and Postmodernism. Ultimately, the piece argues that the **City** (*Miasto*) serves as the most powerful "umbrella" concept—a unifying narrative structure that gives meaning to individual buildings and components. This shift suggests that architecture should be viewed not as a collection of isolated objects, but as an integral part of its urban context, much like storytelling is fundamental to human civilization.

## Key Points
- **Classification is Fundamental:** The ability to classify and categorize is a core human survival skill.
- **Contextual Meaning:** Concepts (like "dog" or "city") are not universal; their meaning is dictated by the surrounding system—language, culture, or scientific paradigm.
- **The City as Umbrella:** The concept of the *City* provides a superior, overarching framework for understanding architecture compared to focusing only on individual buildings or functions.
- **Narrative Power:** Both film and architecture rely on a shared "genre" or narrative convention (the 'umbrella') to provide structure and meaning to their components.
