## Summary

Essay by Warsaw-based architects Martyna Kędrzyńska and Anna Kotowska (JAZ+Miasto) diagnosing public sports spaces — skateparks, squares, parks, pitches — as gendered territories dominated by boys and men, and proposing a set of feminist design principles for inclusive recreation infrastructure. The text combines international research data (Her City Kenya, Yorkshire Sport Foundation, WHO), European municipal case studies (Vienna, Umeå, Barcelona, Glasgow), and the authors' own participatory practice in Julianów (Piaseczno) and a national skatepark survey.

**PL:** Esej warszawskich architektek Marty Kędrzyńskiej i Anny Kotowskiej (JAZ+Miasto) diagnozujący przestrzenie sportowe — skateparki, skwery, parki, boiska — jako zgenderyzowane terytoria zdominowane przez chłopców i mężczyzn, oraz proponujący zestaw feministycznych zasad projektowania inkluzywnej infrastruktury rekreacyjnej. ## Key Points

- **Disappearing girls**: Her City research shows that after age 8 girls almost vanish from public space, which is 80% dominated by boys. By adolescence, only 34% of girls willingly use parks vs. 63% of boys (Yorkshire Sport Foundation 2022).
- **Socialisation gap**: Girls are socialised not to stand out, not to disturb, not to get dirty, to look good, and to serve others' needs. Boys are granted permission to learn from mistakes and take risks — bruises and risky sports become markers of stereotypical masculinity.
- **Fear and harassment**: The biggest barrier for women is lack of safety. 84% of women in Poland experience catcalling (Ipsos 2021). Poor lighting, narrow paths, dense vegetation creating hiding spots, and fenced sports courts with single exits all amplify insecurity.
- **Activity differences**: Boys prefer competitive group sports; girls prefer walking, social integration, and cooperative play. Girls use spaces in groups or with family, highlighting the need for well-organised seating.
- **JAZ+Miasto consultations**: In Julianów (Piaseczno), 112→173→475 residents engaged across stages. Boys prioritised sports; girls prioritised meeting places. A male official later complained "you made too many benches" — precisely what was needed for seniors, teenage girls, young mothers, parents, and neighbours.
- **Skatepark survey (53 women)**: Top requests for more frequent use: public toilet (65.4%), friendly greenery (51.9%), better male behaviour (42.3%), co-creation opportunity (42.3%), better lighting (40.4%), better public transport (38.5%). Improvement ideas: more benches, varied obstacle difficulty, better lighting, public toilet, designated supervisor, "girls-only" days.
- **Design principles**: (1) Meeting places — benches, swings, hammocks, walls, facing each other or at an angle for dialogue, with armrests and backrests for limited mobility, set in bays not rows (to avoid "catwalk effect"); (2) Diversity — smaller zones with varied functions and difficulty levels for different ages; (3) Safety — good visibility, lighting, wide paths, open entrances, no high fences; (4) Public toilets — adapted for women, elderly, disabled, with changing tables; (5) Greenery — integration of hard surfaces with natural terrain, shade trees against heat islands; (6) Physical accessibility — no steps, no high curbs, smooth surfaces (Kraków's "roller-skating resolution" since 2018); (7) Participatory design — co-design with sociologists, psychologists, environmental psychologists; (8) Dedicated spaces — "girls-only" days as seen in Canada and Norway.
- **Case studies**: Einsiedler Park Vienna (gender mainstreaming since 2000, divided play zone by seating); Umeå (training hours split by gender since 1990s, now equal teams); Barcelona Plan for Gender Justice (2016–2020); Glasgow ("UK's first feminist city", started with pedestrian lighting); Frizon Umeå (2016, open structure with swings and seating for teenagers, roofed and lit); BluePark Bielany Warsaw (blue surface winding through greenery, shaded intimate spaces).
- **Feminist urbanism**: Citing bell hooks, the authors argue sexism, racism, and capitalism are inseparable instruments of exploitation. A feminist city must resist the system that excludes users based on life situation, economic status, religion, sexuality, disability, and gender. Design as care, based on sensitivity, can create balanced and integrated environments.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- The "girls-only" days solution (Canada, Norway) is effective for participation but risks ghettoisation rather than integration — how to balance temporary safe spaces with long-term mixed-use transformation?
- The authors note that officials who did not participate in consultations later resisted the outcomes ("too many benches"). This suggests participatory design without institutional buy-in may be systematically overridden.
- The 84% catcalling figure (Ipsos 2021) is shockingly high; its implications for lighting/visibility design are clear, but the deeper question is whether spatial design alone can address behaviour that is social and cultural.
