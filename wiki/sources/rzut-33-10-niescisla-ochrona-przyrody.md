## Summary

Jakub Węgrzynowicz's essay opens with a sombre baseline: we are in the sixth mass extinction. WWF's 2022 Living Planet Report records a 69% decline in wild species populations since 1970. The dominant market model exploits both individual species and entire ecosystems. Węgrzynowicz argues that nature protection in Poland is structurally broken at every administrative scale — not because of lack of knowledge, but because of insufficient political will, misaligned economic incentives, and outdated legal instruments.

The essay maps the Polish protection hierarchy: national parks and nature reserves cover only ~1.5% of Poland's territory under strict protection — far below the EU Biodiversity Strategy 2030 target of 10% strict protection and 30% overall (the so-called "30×30" commitment from the Montreal COP15). No national spatial planning document has been in force since the KPZK 2030 was revoked in 2020 without replacement, creating a planning vacuum that allows short-sighted local decisions to override long-term ecological priorities. Local governments systematically prioritise economic development over habitat protection because strict reserves generate no tax revenue.

Węgrzynowicz proposes a multispecies planning agenda: creating new national parks (Jurajski, Mazurski, Turnicki, Środkowej Wisły, Doliny Dolnej Odry); legally integrating the existing national ecological corridor map (prepared by ZBS PAN Białowieża in 2011) into spatial planning obligations at all administrative levels; expanding strict reserve cores and their buffer zones; and embracing the "fourth nature" — urban and peri-urban wastelands, post-industrial sites, and self-regenerating areas — as legitimate planning assets rather than development targets. The essay references a key empirical case: the "Szast" protection forest in Puszcza Piska, where two decades after a natural disaster, self-regenerating forest sections showed *higher* biodiversity than sections actively managed by foresters. This becomes evidence for rewilding as a planning strategy.

## Key Points

- Poland has ~1.5% territory under strict protection (EU definition) — all 23 national parks cover only ~1.1% of the country. EU Biodiversity Strategy 2030 requires 10% strict + 30% total protected.
- Poland's national spatial planning document (KPZK 2030) was revoked in 2020; no replacement has been adopted, leaving a vacuum at the top of the planning hierarchy that hampers effective sub-national policy.
- A national ecological corridor map was commissioned by the Ministry of Environment in 2005 and completed in 2011 by ZBS PAN Białowieża (Włodzimierz Jędrzejewski et al.), covering large mammal species as umbrella species. It has never been made legally binding in spatial planning.
- The EU Biodiversity Strategy 2030 mandates 30% protected area and 10% strict protection across EU territory by 2030. Poland (under the PiS government as of the article's writing) lobbied to weaken the definition of "strict protection" to count existing State Forests management as qualifying.
- Montreal COP15 (CBD Conference, December 2022) adopted the 30×30 strategy; Poland successfully opposed including a mandatory strict protection percentage.
- "Fortress conservation" (closed, access-restricted reserves) is critiqued as a colonial legacy — it creates "fantasy islands" cut off from human communities and fails to provide genuine ecological continuity.
- The alternative is "freedom to roam" (*swoboda migracji*) — planning that allows species to move, adapt, and colonize new habitats as climate changes. Ecological corridors, not isolated reserves, are the key instrument.
- "Fourth nature" (*czwarta przyroda*) — self-organizing urban and peri-urban wastelands — is proposed as a positive planning asset: withholding human intervention can produce richer ecosystems than designed parks.
- Forest case study: Las Ochronny "Szast" in Puszcza Piska, where post-disaster self-regenerating forest zones achieved higher biodiversity than zones actively restored by foresters.
- Cities should be dense human settlements that minimise their ecological footprint, not sprawl; green rings, pockets, and networks within cities serve as habitat and corridors for species adapted to urban environments.
- Doughnut Economics (Kate Raworth) is invoked as a framework that sets an ecological ceiling within which human needs should be met — the implicit reference framework for multispecies planning.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The article is optimistic about EU leverage on Polish planning law, yet Poland's political positions at COP15 and domestically suggest strong resistance. The question of whether EU regulations can override national and local planning culture remains open.
- The article does not address the political economy of national park creation in detail — it mentions the constitutional opinion from UW Faculty of Law but doesn't propose a legal mechanism to overcome the local government veto on park establishment.
- "Fourth nature" as a planning concept is nascent in Polish urbanism — the article advocates for it but doesn't provide a legal or procedural framework for its protection in planning documents.
