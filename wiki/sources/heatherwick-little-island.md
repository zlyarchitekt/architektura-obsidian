## Summary

Little Island is a 2.4-acre urban park and performance venue built above the Hudson River on the site of the former Pier 54, New York City, designed by [[Heatherwick Studio]] with landscape architects [[MNLA]] and engineers [[Arup]]. The project took over eight years from initial concept to opening. It is owned by Hudson River Park. The source is a press announcement from Heatherwick Studio at the time of opening, structured around three themes: the vision, the planters, and the experience.

The project began with a brief for a waterfront pavilion. Heatherwick rejected this in favour of reimagining the pier itself as a new piece of topography — raising the park on its existing foundations rather than adding a building on top of a flat deck. The existing wooden piles in the Hudson, a protected habitat for marine life, became the structural and conceptual generator: their varying heights are used directly to shape the park's landscape — hills, amphitheatre, viewpoints, and a corner lifted to allow sunlight into the marine habitat below. Structure and landscape are explicitly described as inseparable: "the pier and its supporting structure are one."

The concrete planters — "pots" — that hold the park's 100+ species of indigenous plants were formally derived from a natural phenomenon observed at the site: the mosaic of ice that forms around the wooden piles when the Hudson freezes. The design team reinterpreted this as a tessellated geometric pattern that reads as organic but is composed of standardised, locally fabricated precast elements. Three performance venues are cut into the park's topography: a 700-seat acoustically optimised amphitheatre, a 200-seat spoken word stage, and a 3,500-capacity flexible venue.

## Key Points

- **Location**: Hudson River, New York City; Hudson River Park; site of former Pier 54
- **Scale**: 2.4 acres; 8+ years in development
- **Design team**: [[Heatherwick Studio]] (design), [[MNLA]] (landscape architecture), [[Arup]] (engineering)
- **Structural concept**: park raised on existing wooden piles of varying heights → topography determined by pile heights; marine habitat preserved beneath; pier and structure are one
- **Marine ecology**: existing piles are protected fish breeding habitat; corner of pier lifted to allow sunlight into marine environment below
- **Planters**: form derived from river ice mosaic around wooden piles → tessellated pattern → standardised precast concrete elements; locally fabricated and assembled by boat
- **Planting**: 100+ indigenous species suited to waterside New York climate; biodiversity emphasis
- **Performance**: 700-seat amphitheatre, 200-seat spoken word stage, 3,500-capacity flexible venue — all carved into the topography
- **Access**: two "gangplanks" aligned with NYC street grid; timber-planked
- **Brief rejected**: team converted a pavilion brief into a new urban landscape — a recurring Heatherwick pattern of reframing the commission

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Source is Heatherwick Studio's opening press announcement — entirely promotional; no independent critical assessment.
- The brief rejection ("we felt this was a missed opportunity") is presented as a heroic reframing; in reality this kind of brief negotiation requires a willing client. The identity of the principal funder (from external knowledge: Barry Diller and Diane von Furstenberg, who donated $260 million) is not mentioned — the source presents the project as Hudson River Park's, which is incomplete.
- 100+ indigenous species is an ambitious ecological claim; no data on survival rates, biodiversity outcomes, or maintenance costs.
- The "tessellated ice mosaic" origin story for the pot form is compelling but unverifiable from the source alone — a design narrative may be retrospectively tidied.
- Performance venue completion dates and operational details absent.
