## Question / Prompt
How does the history of the Pritzker Architecture Prize reflect changing definitions of architectural authorship, the myth of the "lone genius", and the transition from formal spectacle to collaborative and ethical design?

**PL:** W jaki sposób historia Nagrody Pritzkera odzwierciedla zmieniające się definicje autorstwa w architekturze, mit "samotnego geniusza" oraz przejście od formalnego spektaklu spektaklu do opartego na współpracy i etyce projektowania?

## Analysis
The [[Pritzker Architecture Prize]], inaugurated in 1979 with [[Philip Johnson]] as its first laureate, fundamentally shaped the late 20th-century concept of [[Starchitecture]]. By bestowing a "Nobel-level" award upon a singular individual, the prize institutionalized the myth of the architect as a "lone male genius"—an auteur whose singular formal vision transcends the collaborative reality of building. This reinforced the cult of personality surrounding figures like [[Frank Gehry]], [[Rem Koolhaas]], and [[I.M. Pei]].

However, a close reading of the prize's history reveals a deep underlying tension between this myth and the shifting realities of the profession:

1. **The Gender Crisis and Authorship Conflict**
The 1991 awarding of the prize solely to [[Robert Venturi]], ignoring the equal collaborative partnership of [[Denise Scott Brown]], became the definitive flashpoint for feminist critique in architecture. Scott Brown correctly identified that the prize relied on the "fallacy" of the lone genius at the expense of true collaborative work. This occurred again in 2012 when [[Wang Shu]] was awarded without his partner [[Lu Wenyu]]. The 2013 petition at Harvard GSD by Women in Design forced a public reckoning, although the jury formally declined to retroactively award Scott Brown.

2. **The Institutional Shift**
Following intense backlash and a broader cultural shift away from the [[Bilbao Effect]], the Pritzker jury recalibrated. In 2004, [[Zaha Hadid]] finally broke the gender barrier. More importantly, the prize began altering its definition of authorship. It shifted from honoring single individuals to pairs (Herzog & de Meuron in 2001, Sejima & Nishizawa in 2010), then to female partnerships (Farrell & McNamara in 2020, Lacaton & Vassal in 2021), and even a three-person collective (RCR Arquitectes in 2017).

3. **From Form to Ethics**
In recent years, the prize has clearly pivoted from formal spectacle (the golden age of "starchitecture") toward acknowledging ethical, environmental, and socially embedded practices. [[Alejandro Aravena]] (2016), Anne Lacaton and Jean-Philippe Vassal (2021), and Francis Kéré (2022) represent an era where "significant contributions to humanity" are defined by civic empowerment, adaptive reuse, and sustainability rather than sculptural iconicity.

4. **Institutional Vulnerability**
The 2026 revelation of [[Thomas Pritzker]]'s ties to the Jeffrey Epstein scandal introduced a profound vulnerability regarding the moral authority of the prize's sponsoring foundation, casting a shadow over an award striving to reposition itself as an arbiter of ethical and social value in architecture.

4. **Kryzys instytucjonalny:** Skandal wokół Thomasa Pritzkera w 2026 r. uderza w fundacyjną bazę nagrody.
