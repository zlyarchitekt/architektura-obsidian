## Overview

Casa de Música is a concert hall in Porto, Portugal, designed by [[OMA]] under [[Rem Koolhaas]], opened in 2005. The building is a faceted concrete polyhedron — a many-sided volume — sitting as an autonomous sculptural object in the Rotunda da Boavista, Porto's main traffic circle. It contains two concert halls (the main Sala Suggia seating 1,238; the smaller Sala 2 seating 300), rehearsal spaces, education facilities, and public areas. Its origin story is the canonical example of "fuck the context" ([[Bigness]]) in practice: the concept was developed for a rejected Dutch private house in 1999 and transposed, with altered proportions and programme, to win a concert hall competition in three weeks.

Historia genezy to kanoniczny przykład „fuck the context" ([[Bigness]]) w praktyce: koncepcja opracowana dla odrzuconego holenderskiego domu prywatnego (1999) i przetransponowana, ze zmienionymi proporcjami i programem, by wygrać konkurs na salę koncertową w trzy tygodnie.

## Design

**The polyhedron**: The building's form is a concrete irregular polyhedron clad in white concrete. It sits on a plinth in the Rotunda da Boavista — the building does not acknowledge the street or the city; it is self-referential, oriented internally. This is [[Bigness]] in its most literal expression.

**Origin concept**: In 1999, OMA was working on a private house for a Dutch client described as "wealthy and fascinated by the avant-garde." The concept was a polyhedral volume with rooms as voids excavated from the solid, the central space a rectangular tunnel running the full length of the building. The client was not convinced. When an invitation to a concert hall competition arrived — deadline three weeks away — the OMA team recognised that the central void of a house (living room) and the central void of a concert hall (auditorium) have the same programme: a large rectangular space with good acoustics at the core. They transposed the concept: changed proportions, added new rooms, adapted the skin.

**Concert hall walls**: the Sala Sugcia's side walls are corrugated glass — transparent and acoustically treated — so that the audience, while hearing music, can see the city through the walls. The walls of other spaces are lined with contemporary azulejo (hand-painted ceramic tile panels), a reference to Portuguese vernacular material culture.
