## Overview

The Atrium Houses at Víznyelő Street, Budapest (1992) are a twelve-unit low-rise, high-density housing development designed by architects János Mónus, Zsuzsanna Szőke, and Sándor Nagy. What distinguishes this project is not only its disciplined spatial composition — two parallel rows of six L-shaped units framing private atriums — but the fact that the architects acted as their own developers. In the chaotic early years of Hungary's post-communist transition, the team rejected the uncertain financing practices typical of the era and instead self-managed the entire process: purchasing the sites, revising the development plan, designing, constructing, and marketing the apartments. The result is a tightly composed enclave that turns inward from its heterogeneous surroundings, using spiralling approach paths, moat-like car parks, and level-high atrium walls to create a world of deliberate slowness and seclusion.

## Ideas & Influences

- **Architect-as-developer**: The project is a rare case where design purity was preserved because the architects controlled the entire value chain, from land acquisition to sale.
- **Inward-turning composition**: Every formal device — spiral streets, moat-and-bridge parking, level-high walls — serves to distance the estate from its heterogeneous context and create a pedestrian sanctuary.
- **Atrium as private outdoor room**: The central atrium is extended by a turfed sundeck on the roof of the living room, multiplying the sense of enclosed garden space within a tight plot.

**PL:**
- **Architekt jako deweloper**: Projekt to rzadki przypadek, w którym czystość projektowa została zachowana, ponieważ architekci kontrolowali cały łańcuch wartości, od zakupu gruntu po sprzedaż.
- **Kompozycja odwrócona do wewnątrz**: Każdy środek formalny — spiralne ulice, fosy z mostkami, sięgające sufitu ściany — służy oddaleniu osiedla od zróżnicowanego kontekstu i stworzeniu pieszego sanktuarium.
- **Atrium jako prywatny pokój zewnętrzny**: Centralne atrium jest rozszerzone o trawiasty taras na dachu salonu, potęgując wrażenie zamkniętej przestrzeni ogrodowej na ciasnej działce.
