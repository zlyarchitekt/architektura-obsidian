## Overview

Katarzyna Kajdanek is a Polish urban and community sociologist specialising in suburbanisation processes. She is the author of *Powrotnicy* (*The Returners*), a qualitative study of people who moved from Polish suburbs back to city centres after experiencing the failure of the suburban ideal. Her research provides the most detailed sociological account in Polish academic literature of the lived experience of suburban development — its cultural drivers, economic compulsions, spatial consequences, and individual life-trajectories.

## Ideas & Influences

Kajdanek's central argument reframes Polish suburbanisation: it is not primarily a cultural preference for the detached house (though cultural factors exist — the house as rejection of socialist block-living, as family aspiration) but above all a structural consequence of **state abdication from housing policy**. In the absence of affordable urban housing, suburban land becomes the only affordable option. The house is compulsion more than choice.

Her concept of the *podmiejski nomad* (suburban nomad) is ethnographically striking: suburban teenagers who, denied urban life by geography, develop elaborate survival strategies — meticulously planning their day to arrive home as late as possible, packing their whole life into a backpack, building urban social networks to extend city time, sleeping and studying on commuter trains.

She also documents the **air quality inversion**: cities that invested in district heating upgrades and anti-smog programs are now cleaner than the suburbs sold to residents as "clean countryside," creating a new trigger for reurbanisation.
