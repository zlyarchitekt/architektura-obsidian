## Overview

Magdalena Krzoska-Hołody is a Polish researcher and cultural practitioner working at the intersection of urban ecology, cultural studies, and participatory research. She is associated with the University of Warsaw (Faculty of Artes Liberales, where she taught a course "Od sztuki ziemi do designu spekulatywnego" / From Earth Art to Speculative Design, 2021/22). Her published research in RZUT-33 (2022) argues that Warsaw's cultural institutions and NGOs constitute a collectively significant **sieci kultury** (cultural network) that functions as a bottom-up mapping tool for the city's [[Blue-Green Infrastructure]].

Her work engages with the concept of **naturokultura** (*natureculture*) — the sphere where urban culture and urban nature intersect and co-produce each other. Her 2022 fieldwork consisted of participatory observation of 20 ecological-cultural events in Warsaw (Targówek, Praga Północ, and other districts), generating a typology of cultural nature engagement in the city.

Jej badania opublikowane w RZUT-33 (2022) pokazują, że warszawskie instytucje kultury i NGO tworzą zbiorowo istotne "sieci kultury" — oddolne narzędzie mapowania zielono-błękitnej infrastruktury. Operuje pojęciem *naturokultura* jako sferą wzajemnego przenikania kultury i przyrody w mieście.

## Ideas & Influences

Krzoska-Hołody's theoretical framework draws on: (1) **ecological philosophy** — Arne Naess (deep ecology), James Lovelock (Earth as living subject), Peter Wohleben (forest intelligence), Piet Oudolf (garden naturalism); (2) **embodied and collective knowledge** — Andrzej W. Nowak's concept of "lost knowledge" (*wiedza utracona*): when a skill vanishes, an entire "horizon of meaning" vanishes with it (from *Perfumy. Posłowie do dezindustrializacji*, 2016); (3) **deliberative methods** — the value of preferences revealed through participatory interaction rather than survey instruments; (4) **postcolonial botany** — the concept of "chwasty" (weeds) as a cultural-agronomic rather than botanical category.

Her central claim is that cultural networks can serve as a *responsive*, *embedded* source of BGI knowledge that complements and corrects top-down planning: they know which spaces residents actually value, which projects have failed despite good intentions, and where the city's ecological priorities actually lie.

Centralna teza: sieci kultury jako responsywne, zakorzenione źródło wiedzy o BZIW — komplementarne i korygujące planowanie odgórne.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Her concept of sieci kultury as planning partners has an inherent tension: cultural networks are strongest in already-activated neighbourhoods (Targówek, Praga), but BGI planning gaps may be largest in areas where cultural networks are weak or absent.
- The claim that cultural networks restore "lost knowledge" is compelling but understates the role of social-media ecology communities and apps (iNaturalist, Merlin) in doing the same work without physical gathering — a possible counterargument or complement.
