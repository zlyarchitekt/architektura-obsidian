## Overview

Charles-Édouard Jeanneret, known as Le Corbusier, was the most programmatically ambitious architect of the 20th century. Born in La Chaux-de-Fonds, Switzerland, and working primarily in France, he shaped the global vocabulary of Modernism through both buildings and polemical writing. His famous dictum — "a house is a machine for living in" — encapsulated a generation's belief that industrial methods and rational design could solve the housing crisis. He died in 1965 in Roquebrune-Cap-Martin. He never received the Pritzker Prize (established 1979); he did receive the RIBA Gold Medal (1953) and AIA Gold Medal (1961).

## Key Works / Projects

- **Dom-Ino House (1914–15)** — patented structural prototype: three concrete slabs supported by six columns with a staircase; walls fully freed from structure. Precursor to the Five Points; designed for postwar mass housing reconstruction.
- ***Vers une Architecture*** **(1923)** — polemical manifesto; contrasts grain silos, cars, and aeroplanes (functional perfection) with the inadequacy of conventional architecture; contains the dictum "a house is a machine to live in."
- **Plan Voisin (1925)** — unbuilt proposal to demolish a large section of central Paris north of the Seine and replace it with 60-storey cruciform towers in a park grid; displayed at the 1925 Exposition; triggered the first modern urban renewal debates.
- **Villa Savoye, Poissy (1928–1931)** — canonical demonstration of the Five Points: pilotis, roof garden, free floor plan, ribbon windows, free façade; also the first full realisation of the *promenade architecturale* (ramp from ground to roof terrace).
- **Unité d'Habitation, Marseille (1947–1952)** — 337-apartment housing block conceived as a "vertical garden city"; integrated shops, gym, and rooftop nursery; Modulor proportions throughout; influenced every large public housing project of the next 30 years.
- **Capitol Complex, Chandigarh, Punjab (1950s–1960s)** — master plan for the entire governmental sector of a new Indian state capital; Assembly, Secretariat, and High Court; Open Hand monument; among the most powerful civic architecture of the 20th century.
- **Notre-Dame du Haut, Ronchamp (1950–1954)** — sculptural pilgrimage chapel; marks Le Corbusier's late turn from white rectilinear purism toward plastic, emotive form; UNESCO World Heritage.
- ***La Ville Radieuse*** **(1935)** — book-length urbanism manifesto proposing a city where housing is assigned by family size (not class) in towers on pilotis in parkland; the theoretical model that drove postwar European public housing policy.

## Ideas & Influences

**Formation:** Self-taught; 14 months under Auguste Perret in Paris (1908–1910) — mastered reinforced concrete; 4 months under Peter Behrens in Berlin (1910–11) — alongside Mies van der Rohe and Walter Gropius; 1911 Eastern tour through Serbia, Greece, Turkey filled 80 sketchbooks including Parthenon studies.

**Purism and L'Esprit Nouveau:** In 1918 Le Corbusier founded **Purism** with painter Amédée Ozenfant (*Après le Cubisme* manifesto), rejecting Cubism as "irrational and romantic." Their journal *L'Esprit Nouveau* (from 1920) was the vehicle for his theoretical programme. The journal articles became *Vers une Architecture* (1923).

**Structural innovation — Dom-Ino:** The Dom-Ino House (1914–15) — three concrete slabs on six columns, staircase — decoupled structure from wall, enabling the **Five Points of Architecture** (1927): *pilotis*, roof terrace, free plan, ribbon window, free façade. First fully realised in Villa Savoye (1928–31).

**Promenade architecturale:** Architecture should be experienced in movement — ramps, changing views, inside-outside ambiguity. Villa Savoye's ramp from entry to roof terrace is the canonical example: "Arab architecture gives us a precious lesson: it is best appreciated in walking, on foot."

**Modulor:** Proportional system based on the human figure, golden ratio, and Fibonacci numbers; introduced progressively from the late 1920s; applied fully to Unité d'Habitation. Intended to reconcile metric and imperial systems while grounding architecture in human scale. Critics note the irony: "It's the mathematization of the body, the standardization of the body."

**CIAM and the Athens Charter:** Co-founded the *Congrès International d'Architecture Moderne* (CIAM, 1928) with Gropius, Mies, Perret, Aalto, and others. The **Athens Charter** (1943), drafted on a cruise ship and shaped principally by Le Corbusier, prescribed functional zoning (housing, work, recreation, circulation as separate zones) for cities worldwide. It became the founding document of postwar urban planning and the direct ancestor of the tower-in-a-park housing estates of the 1950s–70s.

**Typological significance:** Anthony Vidler identifies a *second typology* rooted in Le Corbusier: classification based not on historical form but on **process of production** — the type derived from industrial method, standardisation, and economy, as opposed to [[Marc-Antoine Laugier]]'s primitive hut or [[Aldo Rossi]]'s traditional city.

Le Corbusier fused three sources: the classical tradition of axis and proportion (Parthenon studies, Modulor), the machine aesthetic of industrialism (Purism, *Vers une Architecture*), and the utopian social programme of providing *soleil, espace, verdure* to the urban poor.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

**The political record:** In the 1920s Le Corbusier co-founded and contributed to the French fascist journals *Plans*, *Prélude*, and *L'Homme Réel*, and had direct connections to *Le Faisceau* (a short-lived fascist party) from 1925 to 1928. During World War II he actively sought commissions from the Vichy regime; eugenicist surgeon Alexis Carrel appointed him to a Vichy eugenics committee (*Foundation for the Study of Human Problems*). He wrote to his mother in October 1940: "The Jews are having a bad time. I occasionally feel sorry. But it appears their blind lust for money has rotted the country." He proposed the redesign of Algiers specifically framing it as the city "after its Jewish population had been forcefully removed." The gap between his humanitarian rhetoric (*soleil, espace, verdure* for the urban poor) and these positions is deep and unresolved.

**The urban planning legacy:** Le Corbusier's tower-in-a-park model, transmitted through the Athens Charter (1943), drove postwar European and American public housing policy and produced systematically failing social environments. Pruitt–Igoe (demolished 1972) is the most cited American example. **Jane Jacobs** (*The Death and Life of Great American Cities*, 1961) delivered the most influential critique: his urban design theory isolates communities, breaks social ties, and destroys street-level life. **Witold Rybczynski** (1998): "His urban vision was authoritarian, inflexible and simplistic. Wherever it was tried—in Chandigarh by Le Corbusier himself or in Brasilia by his followers—it failed."

The central irony: his utopian programme for the liberation of the urban poor produced the most dehumanising housing estates of the 20th century.
