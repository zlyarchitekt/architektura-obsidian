## Overview

The manor workers' housing in Káposztásmegyer, designed by György Lehoczky in 1939, is an ingenious solution to the problem of providing both communal and private outdoor space within a low-rise, high-density estate. The development consists of 20 dwelling units arranged in two zigzagging parallel lines. This open-ended, non-orthogonal geometry allows for the differentiated use of intermediate areas: the building lines surround a spacious communal courtyard, while the triangular bays created by the domino-like components — each rotated 45° — function as private gardens. Each triangular bay belongs to an apartment and faces a small porch where the entrance opens. The neighbouring unit overlooks the other side of the same bay. The design borrows the internal system of simple two-part peasant houses with porches, but by sequencing them, a much more economical and compact development was achieved. The estate includes central ancillary functions such as privies, crop depots, and outbuildings for domestic animals — creating a self-sufficient residential environment for the manor's agricultural workers.

## Key Data

- **Year:** 1939
- **Architect:** György Lehoczky
- **Location:** Káposztásmegyer, Hungary
- **Units:** 20 dwelling units
- **Form:** Two zigzagging parallel lines
- **Private space:** Triangular bays (45° rotated) as individual gardens
- **Communal space:** Central courtyard enclosed by building lines
- **Ancillary functions:** Privies, crop depots, animal outbuildings

## Ideas & Influences

- **Zigzag as social compromise**: The non-orthogonal geometry is not merely formal but functional — it generates both collectivity (the courtyard) and individuality (the triangular bays) from a single move.
- **Peasant house typology**: Lehoczky sequences the traditional two-part peasant house with porch, proving that vernacular types can be densified without losing their social logic.
- **Self-sufficiency**: The inclusion of crop depots and animal outbuildings acknowledges that the residents are agricultural workers who need infrastructure for subsistence, not just shelter.

- **Zygzak jako kompromis społeczny**: Nieortogonalna geometria nie jest jedynie formalna, lecz funkcjonalna — generuje zarówno kolektywność (dziedziniec), jak i indywidualność (trójkątne zatoki) za pomocą jednego ruchu.
- **Typologia domu chłopskiego**: Lehoczky sekwencjonuje tradycyjny dwuczęściowy dom chłopski z ganeczkiem, udowadniając, że typy wernakularne mogą być zagęszczone bez utraty swojej społecznej logiki.
- **Samowystarczalność**: Włączenie spichlerzy i zabudowań dla zwierząt przyznaje, że mieszkańcy to robotnicy rolni potrzebujący infrastruktury do samowystarczalności, nie tylko schronienia.
