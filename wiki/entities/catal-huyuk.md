## Overview

Çatal Hüyük is the best-known ancient example of additive urban growth, a Neolithic settlement on the Konya Plain in Anatolia (modern Turkey) occupied from approximately 7500 to 5700 BCE. Houses with square floor plans were built directly against one another, creating an extreme degree of density with no street grid in the modern sense. Dwelling units were accessed through roof openings by climbing ladders; the roofs formed a continuous terraced surface along the slope. The homogeneously spreading development lies between a river and a range of hills like a carpet, exposing compositional features of the pattern type. Apartments of one or two rooms averaged 25 m² in floor area. The extreme density reduced the garden to a small courtyard or eliminated it entirely. The settlement also contains what are interpreted as sacred or ceremonial spaces — rooms with bull-horn installations and wall paintings — suggesting that the dense fabric integrated religious and domestic functions from the very beginning of urban life.

## Key Data

- **Period:** c. 7500–5700 BCE
- **Location:** Konya Plain, Anatolia, Turkey
- **Population estimates:** 5,000–8,000 at peak
- **Access:** Roof ladders (no ground-level streets)
- **Average unit size:** ~25 m²
- **Notable features:** Bull-horn shrines, wall paintings, plastered interiors

## Ideas & Influences

- **Streetless city**: Çatal Hüyük inverts the modern assumption that streets precede buildings; here, the roof is the public realm and the house is a cell within a collective mass.
- **Density as community**: The absence of private outdoor space forces social life onto the roof terraces and into the shared shrines — density generates collectivity.
- **Sacred domesticity**: The integration of shrines into ordinary houses suggests that religion was not segregated but woven into the fabric of daily life.

**PL:**
- **Miasto bez ulic**: Çatal Hüyük podważa nowoczesne założenie, że ulice poprzedzają budynki; tutaj dach jest sferą publiczną, a dom jest komórką w zbiorowej masie.
- **Gęstość jako wspólnota**: Brak prywatnej przestrzeni zewnętrznej zmusza życie społeczne na tarasy dachowe i do wspólnych sanktuariów — gęstość generuje kolektywność.
- **Święta domowość**: Integracja sanktuariów w zwykłe domy sugeruje, że religia nie była segregowana, lecz wpleciona w tkankę codziennego życia.
