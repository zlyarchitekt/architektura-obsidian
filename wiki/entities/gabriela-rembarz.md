## Overview

Gabriela Rembarz is a Polish urbanist and adjunct at the Department of Urban and Regional Planning (Katedra Urbanistyki i Planowania Regionalnego), Gdańsk University of Technology (Politechnika Gdańska). She has spent over two decades organising and leading architectural field trips for students, evolving her methodology from German-inspired *Stadtgestaltung* and structured thematic assignments to her own *Urban Safari* model of engaged, on-foot group exploration.

Rembarz’s pedagogical philosophy centres on embodied cognition: she argues that the hippocampus encodes spatial memory through physical movement, making direct experience irreplaceable by digital representations. Her practice is rooted in the legacy of Professors Danuta and Mieczysław Kochanowski, who founded the "Zakład Rozwoju Miasta" at PG to break with communist tabula-rasa planning and teach urbanism through work in existing fabric.

**PL:** Gabriela Rembarz to polska urbanistka i adiunktka w Katedrze Urbanistyki i Planowania Regionalnego Politechniki Gdańskiej. Przez ponad dwie dekady organizuje i prowadzi wyjazdy studialne dla studentów, ewoluując metodologię od niemieckiej *Stadtgestaltung* i strukturyzowanych zadań tematycznych do własnego modelu *Urban Safari* — zaangażowanej, pieszej eksploracji grupowej.

Jej filozofia pedagogiczna koncentruje się na ucieleśnionej kognicji: argumentuje, że hipokamp koduje pamięć przestrzenną przez ruch fizyczny, czyniąc bezpośrednie doświadczenie niezastąpionym przez reprezentacje cyfrowe. Praktyka jest zakorzeniona w dziedzictwie profesorów Danuty i Mieczysława Kochanowskich, którzy założyli Zakład Rozwoju Miasta na PG, by zerwać z komunistycznym planowaniem na czystej kartie i uczyć urbanistyki przez pracę w istniejącej tkance.

## Key Works / Projects

- **Urban Safari pedagogical method** (c. 2000s–present) — on-foot, engaged group exploration of cities as an alternative to tourist-style sightseeing.
- **Field trips to Germany, Switzerland, Denmark, and Poland** (2002–present) — long-running student trips documented through archival photography; early trips preceded budget airlines and required bus rental or hitchhiking logistics.
- **Postgraduate studies, Städtebauliches Institut, Stuttgart** — training in *Stadtgestaltung* (urban fabric shaping) that shaped her rejection of planning "in a clear field."

## Ideas & Influences

Rembarz’s thinking is shaped by three influences: (1) the Kochanowskis’ insistence on working in existing urban fabric rather than on greenfield diagrams; (2) her Stuttgart training in *Stadtgestaltung*, where fieldwork with municipal officials and on-site observation was the default methodological starting point; and (3) the German *Wandergesellen* tradition of craft apprenticeship through travel. She also cites Kevin Lynch’s implicit influence via the Stuttgart institute where he had guest-taught.

Her key pedagogical argument is that the teacher’s role has shifted: from dictating what students should see (early 2000s) to re-framing familiar places through a professional lens (2020s), because students now arrive pre-travelled but untrained in spatial analysis.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Rembarz’s dismissal of Street View as inferior to physical presence is theoretically grounded but empirically unverified in her account. Post-pandemic hybrid teaching may require a more nuanced position.
- Her idealisation of early-2000s field trips as more emotionally intense risks nostalgia bias; the educational outcomes of past vs. present trips are not compared.
- The *Urban Safari* method is named but not systematised — it remains unclear how it differs from conventional field teaching in urban design programmes elsewhere.

- Odrzucenie Street View jako gorszego od fizycznej obecności jest teoretycznie ugruntowane, ale empirycznie niezweryfikowane. Hybrydowe nauczanie pokowidowe może wymagać bardziej złożonego stanowiska.
