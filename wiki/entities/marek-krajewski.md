## Overview

Professor Marek Krajewski is a Polish sociologist employed at the Faculty of Sociology at Adam Mickiewicz University (UAM) in Poznań. His research spans urban sociology, the sociology of culture, material culture studies, and the social dimensions of contemporary cities. In the context of this wiki, he appears in RZUT-33 as an external expert commentator — a social scientist offering a sociological assessment of the new Warsaw Studium Uwarunkowań i Kierunków Zagospodarowania Przestrzennego.

Krajewski's intervention in the RZUT-33 debate is notable for two things: first, an unusually thorough and appreciative reading of the Studium's diagnostic quality (he calls it a "textbook" of contemporary urban problems); and second, a precise critique — that the document, sophisticated as it is in ecology, mobility, and aging, is insufficiently sensitive to **class differences** and the risk of planning-induced gentrification.

## Ideas & Influences

Krajewski's sociological frame for evaluating urban plans rests on several key concerns: (1) **social differentiation** — cities are more socially diverse than planners typically assume; plans must not inadvertently privilege one social group; (2) **non-human urban actors** — cities are not exclusively human habitats; wildlife and nature have legitimate claims on urban space; (3) **temporal conflicts** — different user groups (residents vs. visitors, young vs. old, day vs. night) have conflicting rhythms that space must mediate rather than resolve for one group; (4) **singularization** (*singlizacja*) — the rapid growth of single-person households changes spatial and service demands fundamentally; (5) the **aging society** as a design constraint — proximity and accessibility, not aesthetics or novelty, are the decisive variables for an aging population.

His class critique is the most distinctive and underrepresented point in the RZUT-33 cluster: the Studium's quality-of-life investments (greenery, mixed-use, public space) are likely to raise property values in treated areas, potentially displacing lower-income residents. He does not elaborate a solution, but naming the absence is itself a contribution.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Krajewski's class critique is pointed but brief — he names the gap without proposing how planning instruments might address it. This question connects to [[rzut-uspoleczniamy-berlin]] and the [[Deutsche Wohnen und Co enteignen]] experience of explicit anti-gentrification policy tools.
- His appreciation of wild urban enclaves ("w formie zenklawizowanej") aligns with Clément's [[Third Landscape]] concept and Krzoska-Hołody's fieldwork ([[rzut-33-09-wychodzenie-za-rog]]) but the article does not draw these connections explicitly.
