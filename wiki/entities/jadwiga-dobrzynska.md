## Overview

Jadwiga Dobrzyńska (1898–1940) was one of the two first women to graduate from a Polish architecture school — she received her diploma from the Wydział Architektury Politechniki Warszawskiej in 1922, alongside [[Wanda Wierzbicka-Nettowa]]. Unlike Wierzbicka-Nettowa, Dobrzyńska went on to achieve significant professional recognition: she was a multiple competition laureate and an active designer, described by contemporaries as a "chodząca encyklopedia swojego zawodu" (walking encyclopedia of her profession) and "człowiek żelaznej pracy" (a person of iron work). She is considered a pioneer and icon of Polish women in architecture, although her career is characterised as an exception rather than a representative of the average trajectory of 20th-century women architects in Poland.

## Ideas & Influences

Dobrzyńska represents the narrow path available to women in Polish architecture before WWII: extraordinary talent combined with exceptional work ethic as the minimum conditions for any professional visibility. Her success did not open structural doors for other women — the vast majority of her contemporaries remained in subordinate positions in private practices or administrative roles.
