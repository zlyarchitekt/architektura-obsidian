## Overview

Monika Konrad is deputy director and chief designer of the *Studium Uwarunkowań i Kierunków Zagospodarowania Przestrzennego* at [[BAiPP]] — Warsaw's official planning and architecture office. She led the design team that developed the new Warsaw Studium (2018–2022), overseeing its analytical methodology, the morphological typology of urban fabric, and the spatial concepts that translate the 30-year vision into concrete planning designations. Her framing of the Studium as *ramy* (frameworks) rather than a rigid plan reflects a deliberate methodological choice: the document must remain flexible enough to absorb unforeseen change while firmly defending core spatial values.

## Key Works / Projects

- **Nowe Studium Uwarunkowań i Kierunków Zagospodarowania Przestrzennego Warszawy** (2018–2022/2023) — chief designer; 30-year spatial framework; morphological typology; ecosystem services analysis; BZIW designation.

## Ideas & Influences

**Framework thinking**: Konrad's central argument is that the Studium must set the *model* of the target city, not predetermine every decision. The document's value lies in the values it encodes and the direction it commits to — not in its legal formality. COVID-19 and the Ukrainian refugee crisis (both occurring during the Studium's preparation) proved this approach necessary.

**City as ecosystem — implemented**: while [[Marlena Happach]] articulates the ecosystem metaphor philosophically, Konrad operationalises it. The practical consequence is the *system of communicating vessels*: social, economic, and natural planning spheres must be integrated; decisions in one affect all others. Urban resilience is defined as the capacity to adapt and recover from unforeseen disruption.

**Morphological typology (MU1–MU10, M1–M3)**: Konrad's team developed a typological classification of Warsaw's urban fabric — ten mixed-use types (from single-family detached MU1 to diverse contemporary development MU10) and three large-scale modernist estate types (M1–M3). This analytical foundation allows spatial designations to respond to existing character rather than imposing uniform rules.

**Mixed-use flexibility**: the Studium moves away from separate residential and commercial designations. Most areas are *mieszkaniowo-usługowe* (residential-service), allowing horizontal and vertical mixing — including industry 4.0 and research/campus functions. Flexibility is the explicit response to market dynamism over a 30-year horizon.

**Ecosystem services analysis**: first systematic analysis of urban ecosystem services in Poland (comparable to Barcelona, Berlin, New York); forms the analytical basis for all BZIW designations in the new Studium.

**Data gaps as planning problem**: Konrad explicitly names the missing data that limits planning: property tax data by function (unavailable), health data (dispersed), real estate market data (requires private purchase), demographic data (not systematically collected). Planning precision is a data governance problem as much as a design one.

**Legal limitations**: WZ decisions need not comply with the Studium; Poland lacks a land bank; compulsory purchase mechanisms underused. Konrad names these as structural constraints — the Studium is more powerful than WZ-era chaos but weaker than German or Dutch planning systems.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Konrad presents the ecosystem services analysis as the methodological basis for BZIW but does not specify how the analysis outcomes translated into specific map designations — the chain from data to spatial decision is asserted but not demonstrated.
- The morphological typology (MU1–MU10) implies typological precision, while the *mieszkaniowo-usługowe* flexibility implies programmatic openness; how these two logics interact in specific zones is unresolved.
