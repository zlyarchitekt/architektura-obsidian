## Overview

Grant Hildebrand is an American architect and architectural historian, Professor Emeritus at the University of Washington School of Architecture. He is best known for applying evolutionary psychology to architectural aesthetics, arguing that the pleasure we take in certain built environments is grounded in deep adaptive responses that evolved over millennia. His work bridges biological theory and architectural analysis.

**PL:** Grant Hildebrand to amerykański architekt i historyk architektury, Professor Emeritus w School of Architecture Uniwersytetu Waszyngtońskiego. ## Ideas & Influences

Hildebrand builds on Jay Appleton's **prospect-and-refuge theory** (from geography/landscape aesthetics) and applies it to the interior of buildings. The core idea: architectural pleasure arises from a simultaneous sense of *prospect* (overview, openness, safety-by-visibility) and *refuge* (enclosure, shelter, safety-by-concealment). Spaces that offer both — a sheltered alcove with a wide view — produce the deepest satisfaction. This is not a cultural preference but an evolutionary one: it mirrors the savannah conditions in which Homo sapiens evolved.

In the context of [[Typology]], Hildebrand's theory supports the argument that architectural classification is not arbitrary — it responds to stable, biologically-grounded human needs. This is the theoretical bridge [[Krzysztof Jaraczewski]] uses to connect the pleasure of categorisation to the pleasure of architectural order.

**PL:** Hildebrand buduje na teorii **prospect-and-refuge** Jay'a Appletona (z geografii i estetyki krajobrazu) i stosuje ją do wnętrz budynków. Centralna idea: przyjemność architektoniczna rodzi się z jednoczesnego poczucia *prospect* (widok, otwartość, bezpieczeństwo przez widzialność) i *refuge* (zamknięcie, schronienie, bezpieczeństwo przez ukrycie). Teoria ta łączy klasyfikację architektoniczną z biologicznie zakorzenionymi ludzkimi potrzebami.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

Prospect-and-refuge theory has been criticised for biological reductionism — applying savannah-derived categories to complex cultural and urban environments risks oversimplifying what is also socially, historically, and culturally constructed.
