## Overview

The 2012 Summer Olympics and Paralympics cauldron was designed by [[Heatherwick Studio]] at the request of Danny Boyle, artistic director of the London 2012 opening ceremony. The design departed from the tradition of a raised bowl: it consisted of 204 individual copper "petals" hand-made by skilled car body workers — one per participating nation — brought into the Olympic Stadium by children representing each team as part of the Parade of Nations, then mounted on blackened steel gas pipes and lit simultaneously by seven torchbearers. Once lit, the petals rose in concentric circles from the centre outward, merging 204 individual flames into a single Olympic flame — symbolising the nations coming together. After the Games, each participating country was offered one petal as a keepsake. An exhibit on the cauldron opened at the Museum of London in July 2014.

The cauldron became subject to a documented design originality dispute. In June 2013, New York design studio Atopia alleged that the design was substantially identical to a composite solar-powered flower they had developed in 2007 at the request of the London Olympic Committee for a proposed One Planet Pavilion. A non-disclosure agreement had prevented Atopia from raising the issue until 2013. In summer 2014, the London Olympics organisers reached an out-of-court settlement acknowledging that key elements of the cauldron design were present in Atopia's prior proposal. Heatherwick nonetheless maintained that "the design process was categorically our own, from start to finish."
