## Overview

Filippo Brunelleschi (1377–1446, Florence) was a Florentine goldsmith, sculptor, and self-taught architect, widely regarded as the first architect of the Italian Renaissance. He is best known for the dome of Santa Maria del Fiore (the Florence Cathedral), completed 1436 — a structural achievement that had been considered impossible for over a century. His solution — a double-shell octagonal dome without a central supporting armature, using herringbone brick patterns to distribute load — drew on his study of Roman antiquity (particularly the Pantheon). The project is cited by Katarzyna Kapusta as a historical precedent for the starchitect type: the visionary-architect who wins a major competition through conceptual audacity, refuses to reveal his method, and is vindicated by history.

Kapusta cytuje projekt jako historyczny precedens dla typu stararchitekta.

## In the Sources

- [[rzut-sceny-stararchitekci]]: Kapusta opens with the Brunelleschi anecdote — the dome competition, the egg trick (when competitors failed to balance an egg upright, Brunelleschi tapped it against the table, breaking the shell and setting it flat; when they protested "anyone could do that," he replied that the same applied to his dome design once revealed), and his refusal to show his model publicly for fear of idea theft. Giorgio Vasari's account cited. The parallel to starchitect competition culture: win through audacity and mystery, reveal only when commissioned.
