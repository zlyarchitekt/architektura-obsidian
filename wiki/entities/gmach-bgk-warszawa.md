## Overview

The headquarters of Bank Gospodarstwa Krajowego (BGK) at Aleje Jerozolimskie 7 in Warsaw is one of the most representative monuments of interwar Polish modernism. Designed by [[Rudolf Świerczyński]] and built 1928–1931, it occupies a key urban corner at the junction of Nowy Świat and Mysia streets adjacent to Rondo de Gaulle'a. Its striking andezyt pieniński (Pieniny volcanic stone) cladding, Jan Szczepkowski bas-reliefs, and richly marbled interiors mark it as a building where austere modernist structure meets deliberate institutional grandeur. Unlike most Warsaw buildings, it survived World War II intact (though looted), and was listed as a heritage monument in 1965. Since 2020 its interiors are undergoing renovation by [[JEMS Architekci]].

## Key Works / Projects

- **Original BGK building** (1928–1931): 4,835 m² floor area on 100% of plot; 7 storeys on Al. Jerozolimskie, 5 on Nowy Świat side; three glass-roof courtyards as banking halls; andezyt pieniński exterior; marble/alabaster interiors from Bolechowice, Zelejowa, Dębnik, Szewce, Morawica quarries.
- **Postwar extension**: after Mysia street was opened, building extended toward Bracka with new Mysia elevation; architects matched original detailing → unified monumental composition from three streets.
- **JEMS Architekci interior renovation** (2020–present): comprehensive refurbishment of interiors.

## Ideas & Influences

The building exemplifies the institutional strand of Polish interwar modernism — stripped of historicist ornament but invested with the monumental mass and material richness appropriate to a state bank. Structurally, Ludwik Tylbor's over-reinforced foundations and pile-supported structure were innovative for their period. The combination of andezyt pieniński (a dark volcanic stone) with chromonikel columns and steel fittings creates a specific tectonic language of gravity and permanence.
