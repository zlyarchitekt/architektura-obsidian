## Overview

Arup is a multinational professional services firm specialising in engineering, architecture, planning, and project management. Founded in 1946 by Danish-British engineer Ove Arup, it operates as an employee-owned organisation with offices across more than 30 countries. Arup is among the most significant structural and environmental engineering practices in the world, having contributed to some of the most technically complex buildings and infrastructure projects of the 20th and 21st centuries. Its philosophy — articulated by Ove Arup — treats engineering as inseparable from design: technical solutions should be beautiful, not merely functional.

Arup is unusual in that it is fully employee-owned (held in trust for staff), which the firm argues gives it unusual freedom to prioritise design quality over short-term profit.

## Key Works / Projects

- **Sydney Opera House** (1973) — structural engineering for Jørn Utzon's concrete shell vaults; one of the most technically demanding structures of the 20th century
- **Centre Pompidou** (1977, Paris) — structural engineering with Piano + Rogers
- **HSBC HQ Hong Kong** (1985) — Norman Foster; suspension structure
- **Millennium Bridge** (2000, London) — structural engineering; notorious for "wobble" on opening day, fixed by retrofitting dampers
- **Beijing National Aquatics Centre / Water Cube** (2008) — ETFE foam bubble structure
- **Little Island** (2021, New York) — structural engineering for the pile-and-planter landscape system with [[Heatherwick Studio]] + [[MNLA]]. [[Little Island]]

## Ideas & Influences

Ove Arup's founding lecture (1970, "The Key Speech") remains the firm's philosophical touchstone: he argued that engineers and architects should work as equal creative partners, not in sequence (architect designs, engineer makes it stand). The firm has consistently positioned structural engineering as a form of design intelligence, not a service function.

Influences: Arup's involvement in the Sydney Opera House shell vaults helped establish the precedent that complex curvilinear forms are structurally achievable — enabling the parametric building boom of the 2000s–2010s.
