## Overview

Bartosz Rozbiewski is deputy director of [[BAiPP]] for spatial policy, responsible for integrating transport and mobility into Warsaw's planning framework. Under his leadership, mobility became a core dimension of spatial planning rather than a separate technical domain — a structural shift that catalysed visible changes in Warsaw's street design (Marszałkowska, Aleje Jerozolimskie). His contribution to the new Warsaw Studium ([[SUiKZP]]) is the *inverted transport pyramid* as an organising planning principle, the metro network strategy (M3–M5), and a dynamic, transit-quality-based parking policy.

## Key Works / Projects

- **Nowe Studium Warszawy — transport i mobilność** (2018–2022): mobility integration into spatial planning; metro corridor analysis; parking policy reform; street typology standards.
- **Reorganizacja biura (BAiPP)**: integration of transport policy within spatial planning office — previously separate municipal units.

## Ideas & Influences

**[[Inverted Transport Pyramid]]** (*odwrócona piramida transportowa*): Rozbiewski's central planning concept. The modal hierarchy — pedestrian → bicycle → public transport → carpooling/carsharing → private car — inverts the traditional engineering priority (car first) and informs all street and spatial decisions in the Studium. The private car is not banned but moves to the bottom; streets become places to be, not corridors to drive through.

**Mobility as spatial planning**: the key organisational insight. For decades, Warsaw's urbanists and transport engineers worked in separate units; the Studium requires them to design together. This enabled the reconfiguration of Marszałkowska and the new approach to Aleje Jerozolimskie.

**Metro strategy — pragmatic corridors**: metro line routing was determined by peak-hour passenger flows (>5,000 passengers per direction per hour = metro/heavy rail; lower flows = tram or light rail). M3 (Gocław), M4 (highest ridership projection, cross-city), M5 (western section possibly surface light rail). Target: 80% of residents within 10-minute walk of rail transit by 2050, in 17 of 18 districts.

**Dynamic parking policy**: parking ratios not fixed in the Studium but set by presidential decree, adjustable by algorithm based on local transit quality. Equal treatment of bicycle parking required — covered, accessible from building exit, not relegated to cellars or attics. Car parking capacity will decrease over time.

**Warsaw's mobility paradox**: 800 cars per 1,000 residents (one of Europe's highest), yet transit heavily used. Explanation: 20–30% of registered vehicles are fleet cars (Poland-wide, not Warsaw-local); even adjusted, Warsaw significantly exceeds Amsterdam or Berlin. Policy goal: make transit so good that car ownership becomes unnecessary for daily life, not to coerce car removal.

**Car-dependent peripheries**: post-transformation (1990s) piecemeal peripheral development created zones systemically dependent on cars — low density, dispersed layout, transit not economically viable. Named as a planning failure the Studium acknowledges but cannot retroactively fix.

**Transit value-capture gap**: comparison with the Spanish model where transit investment is bartered against land surrender — landowners accept corridor land transfer in exchange for the value uplift transit creates. This mechanism does not exist in Polish law; Warsaw pays market price for every transit corridor.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The Studium's polycentric compact city vision requires metro extensions to drive densification in peripheral corridors — but without transit value-capture mechanisms, the funding model is not addressed.
- Developer reluctance to reduce parking below 1 space per flat even at central, transit-served locations (Browary, Towarowa) directly contradicts the compact city logic — a market reality the Studium cannot overcome through designation alone.
- Rozbiewski does not own a car but was forced to buy a parking space when purchasing his flat, which he now rents out — an inadvertent illustration of the structural mismatch between planning aspiration and market norms.
