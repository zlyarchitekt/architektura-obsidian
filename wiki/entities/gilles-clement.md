## Overview

Gilles Clément (b. 1943) is a French gardener, landscape architect, botanist, and essayist whose theoretical trilogy — the Planetary Garden, the Third Landscape, and the Garden in Motion — has become a foundational framework for ecological urbanism and regenerative landscape practice. Trained at the École nationale supérieure du paysage in Versailles, Clément combines rigorous botanical observation with political philosophy: his Third Landscape is simultaneously an ecological category and a political manifesto positioning neglected spaces as the equivalent of the Third State (Sieyès 1789) — space expressing neither power nor submission to power. His work is now central to environmental humanities, urban ecology, and the European green infrastructure policy framework.

## Key Works / Projects

- **Parc André-Citroën** (1992, Paris) — Major Parisian park co-designed with Alain Provost; exemplifies Garden in Motion principles; serial gardens, white garden, black garden.
- **Manifeste du Tiers paysage** (2004; TEH reprint 2022) — manifesto outlining the theoretical, political, and spatial stakes of the Third Landscape; structured in 12 sections and a policy programme.
- **Le Jardin Planétaire** (1999) — exhibition concept at the Grande Halle de la Villette; elaborated the Planetary Garden framework.
- **Jardin des tempêtes** (Vassivière / Saint Denis de la Réunion, 2002–2003) — installation exploring how natural trauma (storm clearings) opens ground for pioneer species; exhibition of the "gardening power of natural traumas."

## Ideas & Influences

Clément's thinking rests on three interlocking concepts:

**[[Planetary Garden]]**: The entire planet understood as a single enclosed garden — the biosphere as enclosure, with all species (including humans) as co-tenants. The metaphor reframes ecology as shared responsibility rather than either industrial management or romantic wilderness protection.

**[[Third Landscape]]**: The sum of all spaces neglected by human activity — wasteland, roadsides, urban voids, abandoned industrial sites, alpine meadows, primary forests — which function as the main planetary refuges for biodiversity. Structured as three subtypes: primary sets (never exploited, climax ecosystems), neglected lands (abandoned from prior use, dynamic), and reserves (administratively protected). The political analogy is explicit: Third Landscape = Third State; indecision as positive category.

**Garden in Motion** (*Jardin en mouvement*): A design philosophy that observes spontaneous plant colonisation of a site before intervening, then works with rather than against the dynamics of "volunteering" plants. Minimum intervention, maximum biological intelligence.

Clément's urban analysis introduces the **urban mesh**: urbanisation creates a grid of built fabric; closed meshes eliminate [[Biological Corridor|biological corridors]] between vacuoles of biodiversity; open meshes sustain them. This has direct implications for urban planning — mesh permeability becomes a biodiversity index.

Temporally, Clément argues for a Lamarckian (inconstant, transformative) model of evolution as more resilient than a Darwinian (constant, competitive) one. The Third Landscape is the site of biological invention precisely because its inconstancy prevents the "hardening" that leads to collapse under environmental pressure.

**PL:** Myślenie Clémenta opiera się na trzech wzajemnie powiązanych pojęciach: **Ogród Planetarny** (planeta jako jeden ogród), **Trzeci Krajobraz** (suma przestrzeni zaniedbanych jako główne schronienie bioróżnorodności) oraz **Ogród w Ruchu** (minimum interwencji, maksimum biologicznej inteligencji). Analiza urbanistyczna Clémenta wprowadza **siatkę miejską**: zamknięte oczka sieci eliminują korytarze biologiczne; otwarte je podtrzymują.

- [[clement-manifesto-third-landscape|Manifesto of the Third Landscape]]: the primary theoretical source — the manifesto itself, in its 2022 TEH edition. Provides the full analytical framework (12 sections) and the complete policy programme, including: the three-part taxonomy (primary sets / neglected lands / reserves), the political analogy with Sieyès's Third State, the urban mesh theory, the critique of heritage fixation, and the call for non-doing as a political principle.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The Planetary Garden metaphor inadvertently reintroduces anthropocentrism — a garden implies a gardener, raising the question of human authority over the non-human.
- Clément's use of "Lamarckian" evolution is scientifically contentious (Lamarckism as inheritance mechanism is rejected); he uses it as an analogy, but the framing risks imprecision.
- The 2022 TEH reprint includes no updated postface — the manifesto's 2004 arguments are presented without revision despite two decades of subsequent urban ecology research, policy development, and climate change discourse.
- The manifesto names demography as the central taboo but does not develop a position — a deliberate reticence that critics read as evasion.
