## Overview
The Narkomfin Communal House in Moscow (1930), designed by Moisei Ginzburg and Ignaty Milinis, is one of the canonical buildings of Soviet Constructivism. Built for employees of the Ministry of Finance, it was conceived as a "social condensator" that minimised private apartments and maximised shared facilities — a communal canteen, laundry, gymnasium, and nursery. Private kitchens were abolished entirely. The architecture featured innovative spatial devices: duplex apartments, glazed internal galleries, and elevated walkways between blocks.

**PL:** *Dom Komunalny Narkomfina w Moskwie (1930), zaprojektowany przez Moiseja Ginzburga i Ignatego Milinisa, to jeden z kanonicznych budynków sowieckiego konstruktywizmu. Zbudowany dla urzędników Ministerstwa Finansów, miał być „społecznym kondensatorem" minimalizującym prywatne mieszkania i maksymalizującym zaplecze wspólne — stołówkę, pralnię, salę gimnastyczną, żłobek. Prywatne kuchnie zostały całkowicie zniesione. Architektura wyróżniała się innowacyjnymi rozwiązaniami: mieszkania dwupiętrowe, przeszklone galerie korytarzowe, kładki między blokami.*

## Ideas & Influences
Narkomfin embodied the belief that architecture could re-engineer social relations. The elimination of private kitchens was explicitly framed as women's emancipation from domestic slavery. However, after Stalin's rise the building was criticised, and miniature kitchens were forcibly inserted into flats designed without them — a spatial reversal that signalled the broader political retreat from avant-garde experimentation.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- The Narkomfin residents were elite bureaucrats, not workers. Was the experiment a genuine social revolution or a privileged architectural laboratory?
- Today's co-living spaces voluntarily share kitchens, but residents retain private bedrooms. Does this make them a successful descendant of Narkomfin, or a commodified, individualist parody?
