## Overview

Ieoh Ming Pei (1917 Canton/Guangzhou – 2019 New York) was a Chinese-American architect, one of the most significant modernist practitioners of the 20th century. Known for rigorous geometric modernism — pyramids, triangles, prismatic forms in glass, stone, and steel — his Louvre Pyramid (1989) remains his most iconic work and an early precedent for the starchitect-as-presidential-commission model. He trained at MIT and Harvard (under Walter Gropius), received the Pritzker Prize in 1983, and practised for over six decades. Kapusta identifies him as a "pre-starchitect" figure — the Louvre Pyramid anticipated the starchitect dynamic but belonged to an earlier mode of prestige public commission.

**PL:** Ieoh Ming Pei (1917 Kanton/Guangzhou – 2019 Nowy Jork) był chińsko-amerykańskim architektem, jednym z najważniejszych modernistycznych praktyków XX wieku. Znany z rygorystycznego geometrycznego modernizmu — piramidy, trójkąty, pryzmatyczne formy ze szkła, kamienia i stali — Piramida Luwru (1989) pozostaje jego najważniejszym dziełem i wczesnym precedensem dla modelu stararchitekta-jako-prezydenckiego-zlecenia. Pritzker 1983. Kapusta identyfikuje go jako postać „przed-stararchitekta."

## Ideas & Influences

Pei trained under Walter Gropius at Harvard's Graduate School of Design, absorbing Bauhaus functionalism but departing from its puritanism toward a more monumental, institution-building modernism. His buildings consistently favour clarity of geometry, quality of material, and the drama of internal light — his atria and skylights are recurring devices. The Louvre Pyramid exploits this: the glass admits daylight into the underground Napoleon Hall; the pyramidal form is simultaneously the most rational (structural) and the most culturally loaded.

His architecture straddles modernism and cultural representation: the Bank of China Tower is both a structural expression and a symbol of Hong Kong's financial ambition; the Museum of Islamic Art in Doha is both a rigorous geometric exercise and a gesture toward the institutional ambitions of Qatar.

**PL:** Pei szkolił się u Gropiusa na GSD Harvard, wchłaniając funkcjonalizm Bauhausu, ale odchodząc od jego purytanizmu ku bardziej monumentalnemu modernizmowi instytucjonalnemu. Jego budynki konsekwentnie faworyzują czystość geometrii, jakość materiału i dramatyzm wewnętrznego światła. Piramida Luwru: szkło wpuszcza światło dzienne do podziemnej Sali Napoleona; forma piramidalna jest jednocześnie najbardziej racjonalna (strukturalna) i najbardziej kulturowo naładowana.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The Pei/Mitterrand dynamic raises the question of authorship in politically-commissioned landmark architecture: the president's political will vs. the architect's design vision are inseparable.
- The glass supply intervention story (Kapusta: "maybe the presidential intervention was a staged spectacle, calculated to emphasise the project's prestige") suggests the entire controversy narrative may have been managed.
