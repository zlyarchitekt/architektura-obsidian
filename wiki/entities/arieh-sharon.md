## Overview

Arieh Sharon (born Ludwik Kurzmann, 1900, Jarosław, Poland — died 1984) was Israel's most significant mid-20th-century urban planner and architect. Born into a Jewish family in the Polish city of Jarosław, he emigrated to Palestine in 1920 and became active in the kibbutz movement. He returned to Europe in 1926 to study at the Dessau Bauhaus under Hannes Meyer — the Bauhaus director who succeeded Gropius and who shifted the school from art-craft synthesis toward social engineering and collective housing. Sharon graduated in 1931 and emigrated permanently to Palestine, where he became a central figure in Israeli architecture and planning.

Sharon is best known for directing the **National Plan of Israel** (1949–1952), a comprehensive spatial planning document prepared for the newly independent State of Israel under conditions of mass immigration and active military conflict. The plan established the spatial structure of the modern Israeli state through five interdependent pillars: agriculture, industry, roads and water, landscape protection, and a network of **New Towns**. It was immediately implemented, reshaping Israel's settlement geography within a decade.

## Key Works / Projects

- **National Plan of Israel** (1949–1952) — comprehensive national spatial plan; five pillars: agriculture, industry, roads/water, landscape protection, New Towns; 20 masterplans for towns of 20–50,000 residents; co-produced with a team of 80–170 specialists. Immediately implemented upon adoption.
- **New Towns masterplans** (1949–1953) — 20 settlements combining garden city and kibbutz spatial models; car-free interior zones; 750-metre maximum radius to all essential services; industry on periphery.
- **Kibbutz planning** — pre-statehood involvement in collective settlement design, informed by Bauhaus pedagogy (Hannes Meyer's emphasis on social function over aesthetics).

## Ideas & Influences

Sharon's planning philosophy combined **Bauhaus social rationalism** (Hannes Meyer's strand: housing as social instrument, not aesthetic object) with the **Zionist collective settlement tradition** (kibbutz as the archetype of functional, non-hierarchical, nature-embedded community). For the National Plan, he synthesised these with **Garden City principles** (Howard's hierarchy of urban scales, green belts, functional zoning) into a distinctly Israeli hybrid.

Three interlocking factors defined the National Plan: **land** (topographical and climatic determinism), **people** (dynamic demographic change, ethnic diversity of immigrants), and **time** (the imperative for immediate action in a war-context state). The plan treated **decentralisation** as a simultaneously planning, military, and political tool — dispersed settlement = defensive positions, legitimisation of post-war borders, and demographic diffusion of Mizrahi immigrants.

The critical reading of Sharon's work (developed by Zvi Efrat and Sharon Rotbard) identifies the plan's complicity in Palestinian displacement (the Abandoned Property Act of 1950 made ~90% of land available by confiscating property vacated by refugees) and in the ethnic segregation of Mizrahi Jews in peripheral New Towns while Ashkenazi Jews remained in established cities. Sharon himself may not have intended either outcome; the plan was instrumentalised by political actors with goals beyond the planners' purview.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The gap between Sharon's apparent egalitarian intent and the segregationist outcome of the New Towns remains unresolved. Was this a failure of the plan or of its implementation?
- Sharon's Bauhaus training (under Hannes Meyer specifically — not Gropius's aesthetic Bauhaus but Meyer's social-engineering Bauhaus) deserves deeper attention as a formative influence.
- The 750-metre service radius in the New Towns is structurally similar to the "15-minute city" concept — a comparison not yet drawn in the wiki.
