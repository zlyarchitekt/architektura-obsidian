## Overview

Kuba Snopek is a Polish urbanist and researcher known for his work on Soviet-era housing estates and the urbanism of mass housing. He participated in the Warsaw Studium development process as an external consultant advising on project communication. He has also collaborated with the Real Estate Development and Design programme at UC Berkeley, giving him a comparative lens on American real estate and development practice relative to Warsaw's planning challenges.

In RZUT-33, Snopek is presented as a critical but broadly supportive external voice on the new Warsaw Studium. His intellectual contribution to the Studium debate is the concept of **mądre dogęszczanie** ("smart densification") — the argument that Warsaw's future spatial development must be calibrated to the morphological type of each urban fragment rather than applied uniformly. He is also notable for framing the Studium primarily as a tool for *quality* rather than *quantity* of development, and for insisting that the era of large-scale greenfield construction in Warsaw is effectively over.

## Ideas & Influences

Snopek's contribution to Warsaw planning discourse rests on three arguments: (1) the **typological diversity** of Warsaw's urban fabric requires typologically differentiated densification responses — there is no single formula; (2) the **quality imperative** — the Studium's value lies in pushing development towards quality, which means selective exclusion zones, activation of brownfield reserves, and more complex project typologies; (3) the **PPP necessity** — as Warsaw runs out of straightforward development plots, future development will be forced into smaller, more complex, multi-actor negotiations (PPP, lex developer, mixed-use programmes). This is not a threat but an opportunity to learn cross-sector collaboration as a disciplinary skill.

His Berkeley connection informs a cautionary parallel: California cities' pathologies (housing unaffordability, poor transit, fragmented public space) are not inherent to market urbanism but to the *absence* of sophisticated collaborative development practice. The lesson is methodological, not programmatic.

[[Monika Konrad]] (BAiPP) provided the morphological typology (MU1–MU10) that underpins the differentiated densification approach Snopek advocates.
